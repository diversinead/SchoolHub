📋 Short Answer Questions
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Dominic works as a software developer at a large tech company. He earns a strong salary, has a permanent contract and works in a social team. His manager recently set him a goal: "Improve your code quality." Despite this, Dominic remains disengaged and his output has not improved.
Using Locke & Latham's Goal-Setting Theory, identify TWO reasons why this goal is unlikely to motivate Dominic and suggest how each could be improved. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Problem 1 — Lack of specificity (1 mark): "Improve your code quality" violates the specificity principle — it is vague and unmeasurable. Dominic cannot know what success looks like. Improved version: "Reduce code defect rate from 8% to below 3% by the end of Q2" — a precise, measurable target with a clear deadline.

Problem 2 — Lack of commitment (1 mark): The goal was imposed by the manager rather than agreed collaboratively. Without genuine buy-in, Dominic has no personal ownership. The manager should involve Dominic in setting the goal — asking him what a meaningful code quality target looks like — so commitment is built from the outset.

Linked explanation (1 mark): Both issues explain Dominic's disengagement: he does not know what he is working toward, and he does not feel personally invested. Locke & Latham's research shows that specific, self-committed goals produce significantly higher performance than vague, imposed ones.

⚠ Specificity, commitment, feedback, challenge and task complexity are all valid answers. Award marks for any two correctly identified and explained principles.
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Coastal Brewing Co. employs 45 staff. Despite competitive wages, turnover among taproom staff is high. Exit interviews mention staff feel like "just a number," never receive feedback on how they are going, and see no future at the business.
Identify and justify TWO non-financial motivation strategies the owner could introduce to address the issues raised in exit interviews. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Strategy 1 — Recognition (1.5 marks): Recognition involves publicly acknowledging employee achievements. A monthly "Brewer's Choice" shoutout where managers and customers nominate outstanding taproom staff directly addresses feeling "like just a number." Satisfies Maslow's esteem needs and Lawrence & Nohria's drive to bond. Costs almost nothing to implement.

Strategy 2 — Career advancement (1.5 marks): A structured development pathway where taproom staff who complete a hospitality or brewing course are eligible to progress to senior server or bar supervisor roles addresses the finding that staff see no future. Gives employees a visible reason to stay, reducing turnover by showing the business values their growth.

⚠ Up to 1.5 marks per strategy: 0.5 for naming and defining, 1 mark for linking to a specific exit interview issue. Generic strategies not referenced to the scenario earn 0.5 marks each maximum.
Short Answer★ HOT4 marks
A retail chain employs 500 staff. The HR manager is reviewing the business's approach to managing underperforming employees. Two supervisors take different approaches: Supervisor A immediately issues written warnings. Supervisor B first refers employees to the business's counselling service and arranges mentoring before taking any formal action.
Compare the use of support strategies and sanction strategies in managing underperformance. Explain which approach you believe Supervisor B's method reflects and why it may be more effective in the long term. (4 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Support strategies defined (1 mark): Support strategies are non-punitive approaches that help employees improve through counselling, mentoring or workplace adjustments. Supervisor B's approach — EAP referral and mentoring — is a support strategy. It addresses the underlying cause of underperformance (e.g. personal stress, skill gap) rather than punishing the symptom.

Sanction strategies defined (1 mark): Sanction strategies are disciplinary consequences applied when performance falls below standards, escalating from verbal warning to written warning to demotion or dismissal. Supervisor A's approach begins with formal sanctions without investigating why performance has declined.

Why Supervisor B's approach may be more effective (1 mark): By addressing root causes first, support strategies can produce lasting improvement. If the underperformance stems from personal difficulties or skill gaps, a written warning does not solve the problem — it may increase stress, worsen performance and damage the employment relationship, potentially leading to resignation or an unfair dismissal claim.

Role of sanctions (1 mark): However, sanctions remain necessary when support strategies have been tried and the employee continues to underperform. The correct approach is to exhaust support options first, then escalate through the sanction process only if needed. This protects the business legally and ethically.

⚠ Always try support strategies before sanctions. Skipping to dismissal without documented warnings and a genuine opportunity to improve is the most common cause of unfair dismissal claims.
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Brendan has worked at an accounting firm for 7 years. His position has been eliminated after the firm introduced AI software. His manager has explained that his role no longer exists.
Identify the type of termination Brendan is experiencing and explain the entitlements and transition considerations he should receive. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Type of termination (1 mark): Brendan is experiencing redundancy — an involuntary, employer-initiated termination where the position is eliminated because it is no longer required. The cause is technological change (AI replacing his role), not anything Brendan has done. Redundancy is about the job, not the person.

Entitlements (1 mark): Under the NES and Fair Work Act 2009, Brendan is entitled to: redundancy pay based on years of service (approximately 13 weeks for 7 years under the NES scale), payment of all accrued annual leave, and a notice period (or payment in lieu) based on length of service.

Transition considerations (1 mark): Beyond legal entitlements, the firm should provide transition support such as outplacement services (career counselling, resume assistance, interview coaching), a reference letter confirming Brendan's role and performance, and an exit interview to gather feedback. These are not required by law but reflect ethical responsibility and protect the business's reputation.

⚠ Redundancy is about the POSITION being gone — not performance. The business cannot immediately re-hire someone else into the same role.
📚 Extended Response Questions
Extended Response★ HOT5 marks
Suncoast Resorts employs 300 staff across four beachside resorts with 48% annual turnover — more than double the industry average. Exit interviews identify three issues: staff find their work repetitive and unstimulating, they are never recognised for good performance, and the company does not care about their long-term careers.
Using Lawrence & Nohria's Four Drive Theory, explain how the HR manager at Suncoast Resorts could redesign the business's approach to employee motivation to address the issues identified. (5 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Introduce theory (1 mark): Lawrence & Nohria's Four Drive Theory proposes that all employees are simultaneously motivated by four emotional drives: to acquire, bond, comprehend and defend. Unlike Maslow, all four operate at the same time — an effective strategy must address all four concurrently.

Drive to comprehend — "repetitive and unstimulating" (1 mark): The desire to learn and be challenged is unmet. The HR manager could introduce job rotation across different roles and resorts, and cross-training in other departments. This introduces variety and learning, directly addressing this exit interview theme.

Drive to acquire — lack of recognition (1 mark): Extends beyond money to include status and esteem. A structured "Resort Star" award program voted by colleagues and guests publicly acknowledges performance. Costs little but directly addresses the recognition gap.

Drive to bond — feeling uncared for (1 mark): Regular one-on-one manager check-ins, team social events and an open-door culture strengthen the bond between the organisation and its staff, helping employees feel valued as individuals.

Drive to defend — no career path (1 mark): A formal career ladder for housekeeping and kitchen staff with clear criteria for progressing to supervisor and management roles gives employees security and a reason to stay, directly addressing the third exit interview theme.

⚠ Always apply the theory — don't just list strategies. Each strategy must be explicitly linked to a named drive AND to a specific exit interview finding.
Extended Response★ HOT6 marks
Alpine Ski Resort employs 180 staff. Each winter it hires 60 additional seasonal staff on fixed-term contracts. Seasonal staff consistently underperform in their first two weeks, causing guest complaints about safety briefings, equipment hire errors and poor food service. Currently new seasonal staff are given a 30-minute safety video and sent to their posts.
Evaluate the use of on-the-job training and off-the-job training as strategies to improve seasonal staff performance at Alpine Ski Resort. Recommend which type of training should be the PRIORITY and justify your recommendation. (6 marks)
✅ Model Answer

On-the-job — strengths (1 mark): Employees learn while performing actual duties alongside experienced colleagues. For Alpine, this is valuable for lift operations and equipment hire — staff learn the resort's specific systems in real conditions. Skills transfer immediately, reducing the two-week underperformance window.

On-the-job — limitations (1 mark): Quality depends on the trainer assigned. During peak winter periods, permanent staff may lack time or patience. Bad habits or shortcuts may be passed on — a particular concern given safety issues already occurring. Also takes the experienced staff member away from their own duties.

Off-the-job — strengths (1 mark): Delivered by specialist external providers, ensuring consistent, up-to-date knowledge. For ski instructors, external certification (e.g. Australian Ski Instructors Alliance) would ensure a nationally recognised standard before staff interact with guests — important given the safety briefing failures that pose legal and reputational risks.

Off-the-job — limitations (1 mark): Main limitation is cost and timing. Sending 60 seasonal staff to external providers each winter is expensive. Coordinating training for staff arriving from different locations within a tight pre-season window is logistically complex. Content may not be specific enough to Alpine's equipment and layout.

Recommendation with justification (2 marks): On-the-job training should be the priority. The most urgent problem — underperformance in the first two weeks — is best addressed by structured pairing with experienced permanent staff, which can be designed and implemented immediately at minimal cost. Alpine should identify its best permanent staff as designated trainers with a checklist for each role, paired with two seasonal hires each during week one. Off-the-job certification for safety-critical roles (ski instructors) should be introduced as a medium-term priority in the following season when budget and logistics can be planned.

⚠ A recommendation must state which is the priority AND explain WHY it suits this specific scenario. Simply stating which is "better" without justification earns 1 mark maximum.
