💡 Note that Lewin's leadership styles (Topic 5) are distinct from Topic 1's management styles. The key difference: leadership focuses on inspiring people through change; management focuses on day-to-day task direction. In exams, identify which framework the question is asking about.

▶ Reasons for resistance & strategies to overcome it  ★ HOT TOPICEvaluate a strategy to overcome resistance is one of the most common Topic 5 questions ▼

Why do people resist change?

Reason for resistanceWhat it involvesExample
Fear of the unknownEmployees are uncertain about what change will mean for their role, workload or future in the businessStaff at a manufacturing plant resist automation because they are unsure whether their jobs will still exist after the machines are installed
Lack of understandingEmployees do not understand why the change is necessary or what it will involve, leading to mistrust and speculationWorkers assume a merger will lead to mass redundancies because management has not communicated the reasons or planned outcomes
Self-interestEmployees believe the change will negatively affect their pay, status, workload or working conditionsSenior staff resist a new flat organisational structure because it removes their managerial titles and perceived authority
Peer pressureInfluential employees or informal leaders oppose the change and encourage colleagues to do the same, creating collective resistanceA respected long-serving employee publicly criticises a new rostering system in the lunchroom, convincing others to refuse to use it
Lack of trust in managementPast broken promises or poor communication has eroded employee confidence in management's intentions or competenceStaff at a company that previously announced "no redundancies" before cutting 200 jobs are deeply sceptical of management's assurances during the current restructure

Strategies to overcome resistance — advantages & disadvantages

🗣 Communication

+ Advantages

  • Reduces fear of the unknown by giving employees accurate information about what the change involves and why it is happening.
  • Builds trust between management and staff, making employees more receptive to future changes.
  • Low-cost strategy that can be implemented quickly through meetings, emails or town halls.

− Disadvantages

  • Ineffective if management's past credibility is low — staff who distrust leadership will not believe the message regardless of how it is delivered.
  • Communication alone does not address resistance driven by self-interest or job security fears — words without action ring hollow.
  • Can create unrealistic expectations if management over-promises outcomes that the change cannot deliver.
🤝 Empowerment

+ Advantages

  • Involving employees in the design and implementation of change gives them ownership, dramatically reducing resistance.
  • Draws on employee knowledge and experience, often improving the quality of the change process itself.
  • Builds a culture where employees feel valued and trusted, improving long-term engagement and morale.

− Disadvantages

  • Time-consuming process — consulting widely before acting significantly slows the pace of change implementation.
  • Employees empowered during the design phase may feel betrayed if their input is ultimately not adopted, worsening resistance.
  • Not effective in crisis situations where rapid, decisive action is required without the luxury of broad consultation.
🏫 Support and training

+ Advantages

  • Equips employees with the skills and knowledge needed to succeed under the new system, replacing anxiety with competence and confidence.
  • Demonstrates that the business is investing in employees rather than discarding them, improving trust and loyalty.
  • Reduces errors and productivity loss during the transition period by ensuring staff are prepared before the change goes live.

− Disadvantages

  • Expensive and time-consuming, particularly if a large workforce needs to be retrained across multiple locations or skill areas.
  • Training takes staff away from their normal duties, temporarily reducing productivity during the change period.
  • May not address resistance that is driven by ideological opposition to the change rather than skills gaps.
💵 Incentives

+ Advantages

  • Directly addresses self-interest resistance by giving employees a tangible personal benefit for embracing the change.
  • Can accelerate adoption of new systems or practices by making cooperation financially attractive.
  • Provides a measurable, visible signal that management values employee effort during a difficult transition.

− Disadvantages

  • Expensive — using financial incentives to manage resistance adds significant cost to the change program.
  • May create a precedent where employees expect to be financially rewarded every time the business needs to change, increasing future resistance leverage.
  • Does not address the root cause of resistance — once the incentive is removed, underlying opposition may resurface.
✎ Negotiation

+ Advantages

  • Provides a structured, respectful process for resolving disputes between management and resistant employees or unions.
  • Outcomes agreed through negotiation are more likely to be honoured by all parties, improving long-term cooperation.
  • Can prevent costly industrial action (strikes, work bans) that would otherwise disrupt change implementation.

− Disadvantages

  • Compromises reached through negotiation may mean the change is implemented in a diluted or less effective form.
  • Lengthy negotiation processes can significantly delay the timeline for change, increasing costs and uncertainty.
  • May be perceived by management as rewarding resistance, encouraging other groups to resist future changes to extract similar concessions.

💡 Match the strategy to the reason for resistance in the scenario — e.g. if resistance is due to lack of understanding, communication is most appropriate; if it is due to skills gaps, training is the right fit. Generic answers that do not match the strategy to the cause will lose marks.

📊 Kotter's 8-step change model  ★ HOT TOPICApplying Kotter to a scenario is a very common extended response question ▼
Kotter's 8-step model is a sequential framework for leading large-scale organisational change. Steps 1–4 create the conditions for change, steps 5–7 implement change, and step 8 makes it permanent. Each step must be completed before moving to the next.

Phase 1 — Creating urgency and direction (Steps 1–4)

STEP 1

Create urgency

Convince the majority of stakeholders that change is necessary NOW. Use data, KPI trends, competitive threats or market analysis to build a compelling case for why the status quo is no longer acceptable.

Eg: The CEO of Acme Manufacturing shares declining profit margin and rising competitor data with all staff and the board to create a shared sense of crisis.

STEP 2

Build a guiding coalition

Assemble a team of influential, credible leaders from across the business who are committed to the change and have the authority and relationships to drive it forward.

Eg: Acme's CEO forms a steering committee including the operations manager, HR director, union delegate and two respected senior supervisors.

STEP 3

Form a strategic vision

Develop a clear, compelling and memorable vision of what the business will look like after the change. The vision must be easy to communicate and must make sense to all employees.

Eg: Acme's vision: "By 2026 we will be the most efficient and lowest-waste manufacturer in our sector, with every employee proud of the quality we deliver."

STEP 4

Enlist a volunteer army

Communicate the vision broadly and recruit as many employees as possible to actively support and champion the change. The more people who own the vision, the more momentum builds.

Eg: Acme runs town hall sessions and creates a change ambassador program where enthusiastic employees from each department become internal advocates.

Phase 2 — Enabling and implementing change (Steps 5–7)

STEP 5

Enable action by removing barriers

Identify and eliminate obstacles that prevent employees from acting on the vision — including outdated processes, unsupportive managers, structural bottlenecks and skills gaps.

Eg: Acme removes the approval requirement that previously forced production workers to seek manager sign-off for every process improvement suggestion, enabling faster kaizen cycles.

STEP 6

Generate short-term wins

Plan for and create visible early successes that demonstrate the change is working. Short-term wins build momentum, reward supporters and silence critics who argue the change will fail.

Eg: Within 90 days Acme's new production system reduces defect rates by 15% — management publicly celebrates this result across all communication channels.

STEP 7

Sustain acceleration

Use the credibility gained from early wins to tackle larger, more complex change challenges. Maintain momentum by continuing to remove barriers and launching further improvement initiatives.

Eg: Building on its initial success, Acme rolls out lean practices to two additional production lines and begins a supplier partnership program to extend quality improvements into the supply chain.

Phase 3 — Making change stick (Step 8)

STEP 8

Institute change — anchor it in the culture

Make the new behaviours, processes and ways of working a permanent part of the organisational culture. Update policies, reward systems, recruitment criteria and induction programs to reinforce the new way of doing things. Change becomes permanent only when "this is how we do things here" replaces the old default.

Eg: Acme updates its performance appraisal criteria to include continuous improvement contributions, incorporates lean principles into all new-employee induction, and publicly links the business's improved results to the change program in its annual report.

+ Kotter advantages

  • Sequential structure provides clear guidance for managers, reducing the risk of critical steps being skipped during complex change programs.
  • Explicitly addresses the human side of change (urgency, vision, coalition) rather than treating change as a purely technical or operational challenge.
  • Step 8 ensures change is embedded in culture, preventing the common problem of people reverting to old habits after initial enthusiasm fades.

− Kotter disadvantages

  • The strict sequential nature can be inflexible — real-world change is often messy and non-linear, and forcing every change through 8 ordered steps may be impractical.
  • Time-consuming to implement all eight steps properly — not suited to businesses that need to respond rapidly to urgent competitive or market threats.
  • Heavily top-down in design — relies on senior leaders to drive urgency and vision, which can limit genuine bottom-up employee ownership of the change process.

💡 You do NOT need to apply all 8 steps in an exam response. If asked to "apply Kotter's model", select the 3–4 most relevant steps for the scenario and explain each one fully with scenario links. Quality beats quantity.

📊 The learning organisation — Senge's five disciplines  ★ HOT TOPICSenge is frequently examined as an alternative change framework ▼
A learning organisation is one that continuously facilitates the learning of all its members and transforms itself as a result. Peter Senge identified five disciplines that characterise a learning organisation — businesses that embed these disciplines are better equipped to manage ongoing change and adapt to a dynamic environment.

1. Personal mastery

Individual employees continuously develop their own skills, knowledge and capabilities, pursuing lifelong learning as a personal commitment rather than a corporate obligation.

Eg: A Linfox driver proactively pursues additional certifications in logistics management and safety, going beyond the minimum required by the business.

2. Mental models

Employees and leaders challenge their own deeply held assumptions and beliefs about how the world works, remaining open to new ways of thinking rather than defending existing views.

Eg: A factory manager who assumed automation always reduces quality challenges this belief after visiting a highly automated Japanese plant that produces fewer defects.

3. Shared vision

The organisation develops a common, genuinely held picture of the future that all members are committed to — not a vision imposed from the top, but one that individuals help create and believe in.

Eg: All Woolworths team members contribute to a store's sustainability targets and understand how their daily actions connect to the company's broader environmental commitments.

4. Team learning

Teams engage in genuine dialogue and collective inquiry, suspending individual assumptions to think together and produce insights that no individual could reach alone.

Eg: A Bega Cheese production team holds a weekly debrief where all members review what went wrong, share what they learned and collectively decide on process improvements.

5. Systems thinking — the fifth discipline (most important)

The ability to see the business as a whole system of interrelated parts rather than a collection of separate functions. Systems thinking reveals how actions in one area create consequences elsewhere, enabling leaders to find root causes rather than just treating symptoms. Senge called this the "cornerstone discipline" that integrates the other four.

Eg: Rather than simply hiring more staff to cope with rising customer complaints, Acme's manager uses systems thinking to trace the complaints back to a training gap in quality checking, and fixes the root cause instead.

+ Learning organisation advantages

  • Builds an adaptive, resilient culture where employees at all levels are capable of identifying problems and driving improvements without waiting for management direction.
  • Reduces resistance to change over time as employees are conditioned to view change and learning as normal and desirable rather than threatening.
  • Systems thinking helps leaders identify root causes of problems rather than repeatedly treating symptoms, leading to more sustainable solutions.

− Learning organisation disadvantages

  • Takes years to build — transforming an organisation's culture and thinking patterns is a long-term investment with no quick returns.
  • Requires significant investment in training, development and time for team reflection, which competes with short-term productivity pressures.
  • Challenging to sustain in businesses with high staff turnover, as institutional learning and shared mental models are lost when experienced employees leave.

💡 Systems thinking is the most important discipline — if you only have time to explain one, make it this one. Always link it to the idea of seeing the whole business, not just individual parts.

📊 Force field analysis — driving vs restraining forces ▼
Force field analysis (Kurt Lewin) is a framework for evaluating the forces that support a proposed change (driving forces) against those that oppose it (restraining forces). Change succeeds when driving forces outweigh restraining forces — managers can either strengthen driving forces or weaken restraining forces to tip the balance.

Force field analysis framework

▶ Driving forces (FOR change)

  • Declining KPIs (falling profit, rising complaints)
  • Competitive pressure from rivals
  • New technology creating opportunities
  • Supportive leadership and change champions
  • New legislation requiring compliance
  • Customer demand for new products/services
  • Desire to improve productivity and reduce costs

  • Fear of job loss or role changes
  • Lack of skills to operate new systems
  • High cost of implementation

  • Lack of trust in management
  • Comfortable with the status quo

Management response: To implement automation successfully, Acme Manufacturing should focus on weakening restraining forces. This means: communicating transparently about job security (addressing fear), providing retraining programs (addressing skills gap), negotiating with the union (addressing opposition) and staging investment to spread capital costs. The driving forces are already strong — the priority is removing the barriers, not adding more pressure.

💡 In exam responses, always identify at least TWO driving forces and TWO restraining forces from the case study, then recommend specific strategies to either strengthen drivers or weaken restrainers.

📝 Model answer — evaluate a strategy to overcome resistance (6 marks) ▼

Sample question: Evaluate the use of communication as a strategy to overcome resistance to change at Acme Manufacturing. (6 marks)

Step 1 — Define the strategy and link to scenario: Communication as a change management strategy involves managers proactively sharing clear, honest and timely information with employees about the reasons for change, what it will involve and how it will affect them. At Acme Manufacturing, where employees are resisting the introduction of automated production equipment, communication would involve explaining why the change is necessary and what it means for staff roles.
Step 2 — Advantage 1 + scenario link: A key strength of communication at Acme Manufacturing is that it directly addresses the most common source of resistance — fear of the unknown. By clearly explaining that automation will be staged and that affected workers will be retrained rather than made redundant, management can reduce the anxiety driving employee opposition.
Step 3 — Advantage 2 + scenario link: Communication also builds trust between Acme's management and its workforce. Regular updates through team briefings and written communications demonstrate transparency, which is particularly important at Acme where past restructuring has eroded employee confidence in management's intentions.
Step 4 — Disadvantage 1 + scenario link: However, communication alone will be insufficient if Acme's employees do not trust management. If staff have experienced broken promises in the past, verbal reassurances about job security may be met with scepticism regardless of how clearly the message is communicated.
Step 5 — Disadvantage 2 + scenario link: Additionally, communication does not address the practical skills gap that automation creates. Employees who understand the change is coming but lack the technical competency to operate new machinery will remain resistant because understanding the why does not give them the how.
Step 6 — Justified conclusion: Overall, communication is a necessary but insufficient strategy for Acme Manufacturing. It should be combined with training (to address skills gaps) and empowerment (to involve staff in the change design), with communication providing the foundation of transparency that makes the other strategies credible. Used in isolation, communication risks being seen as spin rather than genuine engagement.

💡 The strongest evaluate responses acknowledge that no single strategy is sufficient on its own, and conclude by recommending which combination of strategies best suits the specific scenario.

📝 Model answer — apply Kotter's 8-step model to a scenario (6 marks) ▼

Sample question: Using Kotter's 8-step change model, explain how Acme Manufacturing could successfully implement the introduction of automated production equipment. (6 marks)

Introduce the model: Kotter's 8-step change model provides a sequential framework for leading large-scale organisational change. To successfully introduce automation, Acme Manufacturing should apply the following steps.
Step 1 — Create urgency: Acme's management should present employees and the board with KPI data showing the rising defect rate, declining profit margin and the competitive threat from automated rivals. This data creates a shared understanding that doing nothing is a greater risk than changing.
Step 2 — Build a guiding coalition: Acme should form a cross-functional steering committee including the CEO, operations manager, HR manager, union delegate and two respected floor supervisors. This group has the credibility and authority to champion the change across all levels of the business.
Step 5 — Enable action by removing barriers: Before the new equipment goes live, Acme should invest in retraining affected production workers, update its safety procedures and ensure the physical layout of the factory accommodates the new machines. Removing these barriers prevents the change from stalling once it begins.
Step 6 — Generate short-term wins: Acme should identify a pilot line for the initial automation rollout and publicly celebrate measurable early results — for example, a 20% reduction in defects within the first 60 days. These visible wins build momentum and demonstrate to sceptical employees that the change is working.
Step 8 — Institute the change: Once automation is proven, Acme should embed it permanently by updating its induction programs, revising performance KPIs to include new quality benchmarks, and recognising employees who champion the new way of working. This ensures the change becomes "how we do things here" rather than a temporary initiative.

💡 You do not need to apply all 8 steps — choose the 4–5 most relevant to the scenario and explain each one with a specific reference to the case study. Depth over breadth will always score higher marks.
