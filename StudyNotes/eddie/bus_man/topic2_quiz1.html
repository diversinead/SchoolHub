📋 Short Answer Questions
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Priya works as a senior nurse at a metropolitan hospital. She has a secure permanent contract, earns a competitive salary and works in a supportive team. However, she recently told her manager she feels unchallenged and underutilised, and is considering leaving for a role that offers specialist responsibilities.
Using Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs, explain which level Priya is currently at and recommend ONE strategy her manager could use to increase her motivation. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Identify current level (1 mark): Priya's physiological (salary), safety (permanent contract) and social (supportive team) needs are all met. She is at level 4 — esteem needs — as shown by her desire for specialist status and recognition of achievement. Feeling "unchallenged and underutilised" confirms esteem needs are unmet.

Explain next level (1 mark): Her manager must address esteem needs and begin satisfying level 5 (self-actualisation) by offering meaningful challenges that allow her to fulfil her potential.

Strategy recommendation (1 mark): Job enrichment — assigning Priya specialist responsibilities such as leading a clinical protocol review or mentoring junior nurses. This satisfies esteem through achievement and moves her toward self-actualisation, making her less likely to leave.

⚠ Always identify WHERE the employee sits before recommending what they need NEXT. Recommending physiological needs for a highly-paid professional loses marks.
Short Answer3 marks
A supermarket chain employs 800 staff. Despite paying above-award wages, turnover remains high. Exit interviews consistently mention that staff feel micromanaged, have no say in how their work is done, and see no future at the company.
Identify and justify TWO non-financial motivation strategies the business could introduce to address the issues raised in exit interviews. For each, explain whether it produces short-term or long-term motivation. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Strategy 1 — Job enrichment (1 mark): Involves adding greater autonomy and responsibility to roles. Directly addresses the exit interview finding that staff feel micromanaged and have no say in their work. Managers could give experienced staff ownership of specific sections or tasks (e.g. product placement decisions). This is a long-term strategy — sustained engagement from meaningful, autonomous work.

Strategy 2 — Career advancement (1 mark): A structured promotion pathway (e.g. team member → senior → team leader) addresses the finding that staff see no future at the company. Giving high performers a clear reason to stay produces long-term motivation as employees invest in the business.

Contrast with financial strategies (1 mark): Unlike performance-related pay (a financial strategy that produces only short-term motivation once the bonus is received), both non-financial strategies above create ongoing motivation because they address higher-level needs — esteem and self-actualisation — that are not satisfied by money alone.

⚠ Always link strategies to the specific exit interview findings. Generic strategies not referenced to the scenario earn half marks at best.
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Compare on-the-job training and off-the-job training. In your answer, identify ONE similarity and TWO differences. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Similarity (1 mark): Both aim to develop employee skills and knowledge to improve performance and business outcomes. Both are investments in human capital that benefit the individual and the organisation.

Difference 1 (1 mark): On-the-job training takes place in the actual work environment, with employees learning by doing alongside a colleague. Off-the-job training takes place away from the workplace at an external provider (e.g. TAFE, specialist centre), removing employees from their duties.

Difference 2 (1 mark): On-the-job training is generally lower cost (no external provider fees). Off-the-job is more expensive due to provider costs, travel, and lost productivity while the employee is absent.

⚠ Listing features of each type separately without drawing a direct comparison earns no marks for the "difference" criteria. Must explicitly contrast them.
Short Answer★ HOT4 marks
Workers at a packaging factory have lodged a formal complaint about unsafe working conditions. The employer and employees cannot agree on what changes are required. The matter has been referred to the Fair Work Commission.
Explain the roles of conciliation and arbitration in resolving this workplace dispute, and distinguish between the two processes. (4 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Conciliation (1 mark): A Fair Work Commission conciliator would facilitate a structured discussion between the workers and the employer, helping both sides communicate their concerns and negotiate a mutually agreed solution. The conciliator does NOT impose a decision — the outcome is voluntary and only binding if both parties agree.

Arbitration (1 mark): If conciliation fails, an FWC arbitrator would hear evidence from both sides and make a legally binding ruling on what workplace safety changes the employer must implement. Both parties are legally required to comply, regardless of whether they agree with the decision.

Distinction — process (1 mark): Conciliation is collaborative — the third party guides the parties to their own resolution. Arbitration is adversarial — the third party acts as a judge and imposes a ruling. Conciliation preserves the working relationship; arbitration can damage it.

Distinction — outcome (1 mark): Conciliation produces a voluntary agreement (only binding if accepted). Arbitration produces a legally enforceable determination. Because of this, arbitration is used only when conciliation has been attempted and failed.

⚠ The most common error is confusing the two — stating that conciliation is binding. It is NOT. Only arbitration produces a binding decision.
📚 Extended Response Questions
Extended Response★ HOT4 marks
Titan Engineering employs 120 engineers and project managers. The HR manager has noticed that three top-performing engineers have turned down project leadership roles, preferring technical work. A fourth engineer resigned to join a competitor that offers a clearer path to becoming a principal engineer.
Using ONE motivation theory, explain how Titan Engineering could address the motivation and retention challenges identified in this scenario. (4 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Introduce theory (1 mark): Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs proposes that employees are motivated by five levels — physiological, safety, social, esteem and self-actualisation — with lower levels needing to be met before higher levels become motivating.

Identify current level (1 mark): Titan's engineers appear to have physiological, safety and social needs well-satisfied (they are engaged and productive). The pattern of declining management roles and one resignation for a clearer career path suggests esteem and self-actualisation needs are unmet. They seek recognition of technical expertise and a pathway to fulfil their potential as engineers.

Apply theory — strategy (1 mark): To address esteem needs, Titan could create a formal "Principal Engineer" designation for top technical performers. This provides status and achievement without requiring staff to become managers — directly addressing why the three engineers declined leadership roles.

Address retention (1 mark): To address self-actualisation and prevent further departures, Titan should develop a dual career track — one path for managers, one for technical specialists. This directly responds to the engineer who left for a competitor, signalling that technical excellence is valued and rewarded.

⚠ Lawrence & Nohria (drive to comprehend + acquire) or Locke & Latham (specialist goals) are also valid — award marks for any well-applied theory linked to the scenario.
Extended Response★ HOT6 marks
Metro Manufacturing employs 200 production workers. After restructuring that eliminated 40 positions through redundancy, HR data shows absenteeism has increased from 3% to 9%, productivity has declined by 22%, and formal grievances have tripled. The CEO wants to restore performance and morale.
Evaluate the effectiveness of ONE financial AND ONE non-financial motivation strategy that Metro Manufacturing could use. Justify which strategy should be the PRIORITY. (6 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Financial strategy — profit sharing (1 mark): A portion of profits distributed to employees. Metro could introduce a quarterly bonus linked to factory output. Addresses the drive to acquire (Lawrence & Nohria) and gives workers financial stake in the company's success.

Evaluation of profit sharing (1 mark): However, workers' disengagement stems from fear and loss of trust — not lack of financial incentive. Offering bonuses without addressing underlying concerns may be seen as attempting to "buy" cooperation, potentially deepening resentment. Declining productivity may also make bonuses too small to be motivating.

Non-financial strategy — communication and empowerment (1 mark): Management proactively shares honest information and involves staff in decisions about the future. Open forums where workers can ask questions about job security, with workers invited to contribute ideas for improving remaining roles.

Evaluation of communication (1 mark): More targeted at the actual cause of disengagement. The spike in absenteeism, grievances and productivity loss reflects anxiety and distrust. Maslow's safety needs have been threatened by the redundancies — reassurance and transparency directly restores the security employees need.

Justified recommendation (2 marks): Communication and empowerment should be the priority. The root cause is a breakdown of trust — not a lack of financial reward. Addressing the emotional impact of redundancies through honest communication is the foundation without which any financial incentive will fail. Once trust is restored, profit sharing can be introduced as a secondary strategy to rebuild long-term engagement.

⚠ A justified recommendation must explain WHY one strategy should be prioritised, with reasoning linked to the scenario — not just a preference statement.
