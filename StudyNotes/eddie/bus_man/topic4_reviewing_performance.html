📊 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) — full reference  ★ HOT TOPICKPI interpretation questions appear in almost every exam ▼
KPI What it measures If it increases ↑ If it decreases ↓ Linked to
Net profit figures Total revenue minus all expenses — the business's bottom-line earnings ▲ Positive — business is more profitable; shareholders benefit ▼ Concern — may signal rising costs, falling sales or both Pricing, cost control, sales volume
Net profit margin Net profit as a percentage of sales revenue — how much profit is made per dollar of sales ▲ Positive — business is keeping more of each sale as profit ▼ Concern — costs are rising faster than revenue, or discounting is eroding margins Efficiency, pricing strategy
Percentage of market share The business's share of total industry sales, expressed as a percentage ▲ Positive — business is growing relative to competitors ▼ Concern — competitors are gaining ground; may need strategic review Marketing, product quality, competition
Rate of productivity growth How much output is produced per unit of input (labour, capital) and whether this ratio is improving over time ▲ Positive — producing more with the same or fewer resources; efficiency improving ▼ Concern — operations are becoming less efficient; review processes and staffing Operations, technology, training
Number of customer complaints Volume of formal complaints received from customers about products, services or experiences ▼ Concern — quality or service is declining; risk of reputational damage and customer loss ▲ Positive — customers are more satisfied; quality and service standards are improving Quality management, HRM, customer service
Level of staff turnover The rate at which employees leave and must be replaced, expressed as a percentage of the workforce per year ▼ Concern — suggests poor culture, low morale or inadequate pay; costly to recruit and retrain ▲ Positive — staff are satisfied and engaged; reduces recruitment and training costs HRM, motivation, corporate culture
Level of staff absenteeism The rate at which employees are absent from work without prior approval, expressed as days lost per employee ▼ Concern — may indicate poor morale, workplace stress, illness or disengagement ▲ Positive — staff are engaged, healthy and motivated to attend work HRM, workplace culture, workload management
Number of workplace accidents Frequency of injuries, near-misses or safety incidents in the workplace ▼ Concern — safety systems are failing; legal liability risk and WorkSafe scrutiny ▲ Positive — safety culture and procedures are effective; reduces compensation claims OHS management, training, operations
Number of sales Total volume of units sold or transactions completed in a period ▲ Positive — demand is strong; marketing and sales strategies are working ▼ Concern — demand is falling; may need pricing, product or marketing review Marketing, pricing, customer satisfaction
Wastage rates The proportion of inputs or outputs that are discarded as defective, spoiled or unusable ▼ Concern — quality or process problems are wasting resources and increasing costs ▲ Positive — operations are more efficient; lean and quality strategies are working Quality management, lean operations, CSR

💡 KPI questions in exams always provide data — identify the trend (up or down), state whether it is positive or negative, explain WHY it signals a problem or improvement, and link to a potential cause or solution in the business. Never just say "it went up" — always say what it means for the business.

📈 How to interpret KPI data — exam technique ▼

💡 Four-step KPI interpretation formula

Step 1 — State the trend: Identify whether the KPI has increased, decreased or remained stable. Quote the actual figures from the data.

Step 2 — State what this means: Is the trend positive or negative for the business? (Remember: some KPIs are better when they decrease, e.g. complaints, absenteeism.)

Step 3 — Explain the likely cause: What might have caused this trend? Link to the case study details.

Step 4 — Link to action: What should the business do in response? Which strategy (HRM, operations, etc.) should be reviewed?

📝 Worked example

Data: Metro Cafe's number of customer complaints increased from 12 per month to 47 per month over the past quarter.
Trend: Customer complaints have increased significantly, rising by 292% over the quarter.
Meaning: This is a negative trend — a sharp rise in complaints signals declining product or service quality, which threatens customer retention and Metro Cafe's reputation.
Likely cause: The case study notes that Metro Cafe recently hired several new staff — insufficient training may be leading to inconsistent coffee quality and poor service.
Action: Metro Cafe should review its staff training and onboarding program, and consider implementing a quality assurance system to ensure consistent standards across all staff.

💡 Always use the specific numbers from the data in your answer — writing "complaints increased significantly" without quoting the figures will cost you marks.

📊 Forces for change — internal & external  ★ HOT TOPICIdentifying and explaining forces for change is a core Topic 4 skill ▼
Force Internal or external? What it involves Example
Managers and employees Internal Staff who identify operational problems, champion new ideas or drive change from within the business A Woolworths store manager identifies that current checkout processes cause long queues and proposes a self-service checkout expansion
Pursuit of profit Internal The desire to improve financial performance drives the business to change processes, reduce costs or enter new markets Linfox restructures its logistics routes to reduce fuel costs and improve delivery efficiency, increasing net profit margin
Organisational objectives Internal New or revised strategic goals require the business to change its structure, processes or resource allocation Qantas sets a new objective to become carbon-neutral by 2050, requiring significant investment in fuel-efficient aircraft and renewable fuels
Legislation External New or amended laws require businesses to change their practices, products or operations to remain compliant New workplace safety regulations require all construction companies to introduce mandatory daily safety briefings and updated personal protective equipment
Economic conditions External Changes in interest rates, inflation, unemployment or consumer confidence that affect business operations and demand Rising interest rates in 2023–24 reduced consumer spending, forcing retailers like JB Hi-Fi to discount products and review their pricing strategies
Technology External New technologies that disrupt existing business models, create new competitors or offer opportunities to improve operations The rise of online streaming forced Australian retailers like JB Hi-Fi to reduce their physical CD and DVD floor space and pivot to accessories and services
Competition External Actions by rival businesses that threaten market share and force the business to respond by improving products, prices or services Amazon's entry into the Australian market forced Kmart and Target to accelerate their online delivery capabilities to protect market share
Societal attitudes External Shifts in community values, consumer preferences or social expectations that require the business to adapt its products, operations or communications Growing consumer demand for sustainable packaging has led Woolworths and Coles to phase out single-use plastic bags and invest in recyclable alternatives
Globalisation External Increasing interconnection of world markets, creating both new opportunities (export markets) and new threats (overseas competition, supply chain risks) Bega Cheese expanded into Asian export markets as rising middle-class incomes increased demand for Australian dairy products overseas

Internal vs external forces — key differences

🏠 Internal forces

  • Originate from within the business — management decisions, employee feedback, financial performance.
  • The business has significant control over whether and how it responds to these forces.
  • Often arise from reviewing KPI data, strategic planning or employee-driven innovation.
  • Examples: pursuit of profit, new objectives, manager-driven restructuring.

🌎 External forces

  • Originate outside the business — government, economy, competitors, technology, society.
  • The business has limited or no control over these forces — it can only respond to them.
  • Often unpredictable and can require rapid strategic responses to avoid competitive disadvantage.
  • Examples: new legislation, economic downturn, disruptive technology, competitor actions.

💡 Exam questions often ask you to identify whether a force is internal or external AND explain how it creates pressure for change. Always link the force directly to the case study business.

📊 SWOT analysis — framework & worked example  ★ HOT TOPICSWOT is used to justify the need for change and recommend strategies ▼

What is SWOT analysis?

SWOT analysis is a strategic planning tool used to evaluate the current position of a business by identifying its internal Strengths and Weaknesses and the external Opportunities and Threats it faces. It is used to justify the need for change and determine the direction of future strategy.

★ STRENGTHS (Internal)

  • What does the business do well?
  • What resources or capabilities give it an advantage?
  • What do customers value about the business?

Eg: Strong brand recognition, loyal customer base, skilled workforce, proprietary technology, low cost structure

⚠ WEAKNESSES (Internal)

  • Where does the business underperform?
  • What resources or capabilities are lacking?
  • What do competitors do better?

Eg: High staff turnover, outdated technology, limited product range, poor online presence, high debt levels

💡 OPPORTUNITIES (External)

  • What external trends could the business exploit?
  • Are there underserved markets or customer segments?
  • What technology or regulatory changes create openings?

Eg: Growing export markets, changing consumer preferences, new technology, competitor weakness, demographic shifts

⛔ THREATS (External)

  • What external forces could harm the business?
  • What are competitors doing that is threatening?
  • What economic, legal or social risks exist?

Eg: New market entrants, rising input costs, changing legislation, economic downturn, disruptive technology

Worked example — Metro Cafe SWOT

★ Strengths

  • Award-winning coffee blends with strong local reputation
  • Loyal regular customer base providing stable revenue
  • Prime CBD location with high foot traffic

⚠ Weaknesses

  • High staff turnover leading to inconsistent service quality
  • No online ordering or delivery capability
  • Limited seating capacity restricting peak-hour revenue

💡 Opportunities

  • Growing demand for specialty coffee subscriptions and home delivery
  • Neighbouring business recently closed, freeing up adjacent floor space
  • New office tower opening nearby will increase lunchtime foot traffic

⛔ Threats

  • Two new specialty cafes opening within 200m in the next quarter
  • Rising milk and coffee bean costs squeezing profit margins
  • Increased working-from-home reducing CBD daytime customer base

How to use SWOT in an exam response

SO strategy: Use a Strength to capitalise on an Opportunity — e.g. Metro Cafe could leverage its award-winning coffee reputation to launch a home delivery subscription service.
WO strategy: Address a Weakness to take advantage of an Opportunity — e.g. fix the high staff turnover problem before the new office tower opens so service quality is reliable when demand spikes.
ST strategy: Use a Strength to counter a Threat — e.g. Metro Cafe's loyal customer base provides a buffer against new competitor cafes.
WT strategy: Address a Weakness to minimise exposure to a Threat — e.g. invest in staff retention to ensure quality remains consistent as competition intensifies.

💡 SWOT is a tool, not an answer. In exams, you must use SWOT findings to JUSTIFY a recommended strategy — identifying strengths and threats alone is not sufficient.

📊 Proactive vs reactive approaches to change ▼
FeatureProactive approachReactive approach
DefinitionAnticipating future changes and making adjustments before problems arise or opportunities are missedResponding to changes after they have already occurred and impacted the business
TimingBefore the change or problem occursAfter the change or problem has occurred
Driven byStrategic planning, KPI monitoring, SWOT analysis, forward-looking managementDeclining KPIs, competitive pressure, crisis, legal requirement, customer complaints
Risk levelLower risk — business is prepared and in controlHigher risk — business may be behind the curve and forced to act under pressure
ExampleWoolworths invests in online delivery infrastructure in 2018, before the online grocery boom accelerated in 2020A retailer scrambles to build an online store only after losing significant market share to Amazon following its 2017 Australian launch

+ Proactive advantages

  • Business is better positioned than competitors who are still reacting, creating a first-mover advantage.
  • Change is planned and resourced properly rather than rushed, reducing the risk of costly errors.
  • Staff have time to be consulted and trained, reducing resistance and improving implementation.

− Reactive disadvantages

  • Business may have already suffered financial damage (lost sales, market share) before change is implemented.
  • Rushed changes are more likely to be poorly planned, increasing costs and staff resistance.
  • Competitors who acted proactively may have already captured the market opportunity.

💡 A business that regularly reviews its KPIs and conducts SWOT analysis is positioned to be proactive. A business that only changes when forced to by poor results or external pressure is reactive.

📝 Model answer — interpret KPI data and recommend action (4 marks) ▼

Sample question: Analyse the following KPI data for Acme Manufacturing and explain what action the business should take. Staff turnover: Year 1 — 8%, Year 2 — 14%, Year 3 — 23%. (4 marks)

Step 1 — State the trend with data: Acme Manufacturing's staff turnover rate has increased significantly over three years, rising from 8% to 23% — nearly tripling in that period. This is a sharply negative trend.
Step 2 — Explain what it means: A staff turnover rate of 23% indicates that nearly one in four employees is leaving the business each year. This is well above the typical manufacturing industry benchmark of around 10–15%, suggesting a serious problem with employee satisfaction, engagement or working conditions.
Step 3 — Identify the likely cause: The sustained and accelerating increase suggests an underlying issue with Acme's workplace culture, management practices or remuneration rather than a one-off event. Poor management styles, inadequate pay or a lack of career development opportunities may be driving employees to seek work elsewhere.
Step 4 — Recommend action: Acme Manufacturing should urgently review its human resource management practices, including exit interview data to identify the primary reasons for departure. The business should consider introducing non-financial motivation strategies such as career advancement pathways, improved recognition programs and a review of its management style to rebuild engagement and reduce further losses.

💡 Always quote the specific numbers in your analysis — "staff turnover increased from 8% to 23%" is much stronger than "staff turnover increased".

📝 Model answer — evaluate a force for change (6 marks) ▼

Sample question: Evaluate technology as a force for change for Acme Manufacturing. (6 marks)

Step 1 — Define and introduce: Technology is an external force for change that arises when new innovations disrupt existing business models, create new competitive threats or present opportunities to improve operations. For a manufacturer like Acme, technological change is particularly significant as it directly affects production processes and competitiveness.
Step 2 — Positive impact 1: Adopting new manufacturing technology, such as robotic automation, could allow Acme Manufacturing to significantly increase production speed and consistency. This would improve its rate of productivity growth KPI and reduce unit costs, strengthening its competitive position against lower-cost rivals.
Step 3 — Positive impact 2: Technology also enables Acme to use computer-aided design (CAD) and materials requirement planning (MRP) systems, reducing waste, improving scheduling and lowering input costs — all of which directly improve the net profit margin KPI.
Step 4 — Negative impact 1: However, the high capital cost of acquiring and implementing new technology places significant financial pressure on Acme Manufacturing, particularly if profit margins are already tight. Failing to invest may leave Acme behind technologically, but investing poorly or prematurely can deplete cash reserves.
Step 5 — Negative impact 2: Additionally, automation may displace production workers, increasing staff turnover and triggering resistance to change within the workforce. The social and cultural cost of job losses can damage morale among remaining staff and harm Acme's reputation in the community.
Step 6 — Justified conclusion: Overall, technology represents both a significant opportunity and a genuine threat for Acme Manufacturing. The business should adopt a proactive approach — investing in targeted automation where productivity gains clearly outweigh costs — while actively managing the workforce impact through retraining and transparent communication. Failure to engage with technological change reactively will ultimately be more damaging than the short-term cost of proactive investment.

💡 A strong "evaluate a force for change" response must include: what the force is, its positive impacts, its negative impacts, AND a justified conclusion about whether and how the business should respond. Linking to KPIs adds significant depth.
