📊 Motivation theories — comparison  ★ HOT TOPICOne of the most frequently examined areas in Topic 2 ▼

🏢 Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (1943)

People are motivated by five levels of needs, arranged in a hierarchy. Lower-level needs must be satisfied before higher-level needs can motivate behaviour. Managers must identify which level an employee is currently at and offer rewards that satisfy the NEXT level.

5. Self-actualisation — reaching full potential, creativity, meaningful work
4. Esteem — recognition, status, achievement, respect from others
3. Social — belonging, friendship, teamwork, acceptance
2. Safety — job security, safe working conditions, stable income
1. Physiological — basic pay, food, shelter, rest

⚠ Exam tip: Do NOT just list the five levels. Explain how the theory guides a manager to identify an employee's current level and offer needs at the NEXT level up.

🎯 Locke & Latham's Goal-Setting Theory (1968)

Employees are motivated when they are set specific, challenging but achievable goals. The theory identifies five key principles for effective goal-setting, remembered with the acronym SCCEA:

PrincipleWhat it meansExample
S — SpecificityGoals must be clear and precise, not vague"Increase sales by 10% this quarter" not "do better"
C — ChallengeGoals must stretch the employee but remain achievableA target that requires effort but is not impossible
C — CommitmentEmployees must genuinely accept and agree to the goalInvolving staff in setting their own KPIs
E — FeedbackRegular progress updates help employees stay on trackMonthly performance reviews with the manager
A — Task complexityComplex goals need to be broken into manageable stepsA large project split into weekly milestones

⚠ Exam tip: The exam may ask you to explain ONE of the five principles — always name the principle, define it, and link to the scenario.

👥 Lawrence & Nohria's Four Drive Theory (2002)

Employees are motivated by four fundamental emotional drives. Unlike Maslow, these drives do not form a hierarchy — all four operate simultaneously and independently. Managers should address ALL four drives to fully motivate staff.

DriveWhat it meansHow managers can address it
Drive to AcquireDesire for material goods, money, status and experiencesCompetitive salaries, bonuses, promotions, perks
Drive to BondDesire for social connection, belonging and caring relationshipsTeamwork, social events, inclusive culture, recognition
Drive to ComprehendDesire to learn, grow, make sense of the world and be challengedTraining, interesting work, stretch assignments, autonomy
Drive to DefendDesire to protect oneself, one's achievements and one's valuesJob security, fair processes, transparent communication

⚠ Exam tip: Lawrence & Nohria is often compared to Maslow. Key difference — L&N has NO hierarchy; all four drives operate at the same time.

Financial strategies

StrategyWhat it involves+ Advantage− DisadvantageShort or long-term?
Remuneration Base salary or wages paid for work performed Provides financial security, attracting and retaining staff Does not reward performance above minimum expectations Long-term — ongoing security builds sustained loyalty
Performance-related pay Bonuses or pay increases linked to achieving targets Directly incentivises effort and high performance Can create unhealthy competition and reduce teamwork Short-term — motivation often fades once the bonus is received

Non-financial strategies

StrategyWhat it involves+ Advantage− DisadvantageShort or long-term?
Recognition & awards Publicly acknowledging employee achievements (Employee of the Month, awards) Satisfies esteem needs at low cost; encourages desired behaviour in others Can cause resentment among staff who feel overlooked or unfairly judged Short-term — motivational effect fades quickly without follow-up
Job enrichment Making roles more meaningful by adding greater responsibility and variety Addresses higher-level needs (Maslow) and the drive to comprehend (L&N) Not all employees want more responsibility; can increase stress and workload Long-term — sustained engagement from meaningful work

Flexible working arrangements Options such as working from home, flexible hours or part-time arrangements Improves work-life balance and loyalty, especially for employees with family commitments Can reduce collaboration and make supervision more difficult for managers Long-term — ongoing lifestyle benefit sustains loyalty

Support strategies

StrategyWhat it involves+ Advantage− Disadvantage
Employee Assistance Programs (EAP) Confidential counselling and support services provided to employees experiencing personal or work-related difficulties Reduces absenteeism and improves wellbeing; demonstrates the business cares about staff beyond their work role Costly to provide; employees may be reluctant to use services due to stigma or concerns about confidentiality
Mentoring & coaching Pairing employees with experienced colleagues or external coaches to guide professional development Builds skills and confidence; strengthens relationships and knowledge transfer within the business Effectiveness depends on the quality of the mentor-mentee relationship; time-consuming for senior staff
Wellness programs Initiatives such as gym memberships, mental health days, yoga classes or health screenings provided by the employer Reduces sick leave and improves morale; signals that employee wellbeing is valued May be seen as superficial if deeper workplace issues (e.g. poor culture) are not addressed

Sanction strategies

StrategyWhat it involves+ Advantage− Disadvantage
Verbal warning An informal or formal spoken warning given to an employee whose performance or behaviour falls below required standards Provides an early, low-cost opportunity to correct behaviour before it escalates Easy for employees to dismiss; no written record unless documented separately
Written warning A formal documented notice placed on an employee's file outlining the performance issue and expected improvement Creates a clear record of the issue and the business's response; legally protects the employer Can damage the employment relationship and reduce morale if not handled sensitively
Demotion Reducing an employee's role, responsibility or pay grade as a consequence of serious underperformance or misconduct Retains the employee while signalling that performance must improve; avoids the cost of dismissal and re-hiring Highly demotivating — often leads to resignation; can create resentment among the broader team
Dismissal as sanction Terminating employment as the final consequence of ongoing failure to meet performance standards after warnings have been given Removes a persistent underperformer; signals to remaining staff that standards are enforced Costly and disruptive; risk of unfair dismissal claim if proper process has not been followed

💡 Link motivation strategies to a theory where possible. Also note: financial strategies tend to have a short-term motivational effect (the boost fades); non-financial strategies tied to growth and meaning tend to produce long-term motivation. Sanction strategies motivate through fear — use them only as a last resort after support strategies have been tried.

▶ Training types — advantages & disadvantages  ★ HOT TOPICDiscuss or evaluate training questions appear regularly ▼

On-the-job training

+ Advantages

  • Employees learn skills directly relevant to their specific role, reducing the time before they become productive.
  • Lower cost than off-the-job training as no external provider or venue is required.
  • Training occurs in the real work environment, so skills transfer immediately to daily tasks.

− Disadvantages

  • Quality depends on the trainer's skills and patience — poor trainers produce poor outcomes.
  • Disrupts normal work operations as both trainer and trainee are occupied during working hours.
  • May pass on bad habits or outdated practices if the trainer lacks current best-practice knowledge.

Off-the-job training

+ Advantages

  • Delivered by specialist experts, ensuring employees receive high-quality, up-to-date knowledge and skills.
  • Removes employees from workplace distractions, allowing them to focus entirely on learning.
  • Broadens employee skills beyond current role requirements, supporting career development.

− Disadvantages

  • More expensive than on-the-job training due to external provider fees, travel and accommodation costs.
  • Lost productivity while employees are away from their normal duties during training.
  • Skills learned may not be directly applicable to the specific tools or processes used in the business.

Online (e-learning) training

+ Advantages

  • Flexible — employees can complete training at their own pace and at a time that suits them.
  • Cost-effective for large workforces as the same content can be delivered to many employees simultaneously.
  • Easy to update and keep current without the expense of re-running face-to-face programs.

− Disadvantages

  • Lacks the hands-on, practical component needed for technical or physical skills (e.g. operating machinery).
  • Requires reliable internet access and technology, which may not be available for all staff.
  • Lower engagement and completion rates as employees may defer or rush through modules without supervision.

💡 Match the training type to the scenario — if the business needs staff trained quickly on specific tasks, on-the-job is justified; if specialised qualifications are needed, off-the-job is better.

Unfair vs wrongful dismissal

🔎 Unfair dismissal

  • When an employee is dismissed in a harsh, unjust or unreasonable manner without following proper procedure.
  • Employee can apply to the Fair Work Commission for reinstatement or compensation.
  • Example: dismissing an employee without warnings or without giving them a chance to respond to allegations.

⚠ Wrongful dismissal

  • When an employee is dismissed in breach of their employment contract (e.g. without required notice period).
  • Employee may pursue legal action for breach of contract in court.
  • Example: terminating an employee two weeks before their contract entitles them to redundancy pay.

Entitlement obligations summary

Employers MUST provide: payment of all outstanding wages · accrued annual leave entitlements · required notice period (or pay in lieu) · redundancy pay where applicable
Key legislation: Fair Work Act 2009 · National Employment Standards (NES) · applicable Modern Award

Transition considerations

ConsiderationWhat it involvesWhy it matters
Outplacement services The business provides career counselling, resume writing support or job placement assistance to help departing employees find new roles Demonstrates ethical responsibility; protects the business's reputation and reduces unfair dismissal risk
Reference letters Written references provided by the employer confirming the employee's role, performance and character Supports the departing employee's job search; builds goodwill and reflects positively on the business's culture
Knowledge transfer / handover Ensuring the departing employee documents processes and trains their replacement before leaving Minimises disruption to business operations and prevents loss of institutional knowledge
Exit interviews A structured conversation with the departing employee to understand their reasons for leaving and gather feedback Provides valuable insights for improving retention; identifies management or culture issues before they affect remaining staff

💡 Key distinction: redundancy = the JOB is no longer needed (not the person). Dismissal = the PERSON'S conduct or performance is the reason. Always consider transition support — it protects the business legally and reputationally.

👥 Workplace participants — roles & responsibilities  ★ HOT TOPICRoles of participants and dispute resolution are frequently examined ▼
ParticipantRole & responsibilitiesExample
Human resource managers Responsible for recruiting, developing, motivating and retaining staff. Develop HR policies, manage disputes and ensure legal compliance with the Fair Work Act and NES. An HR manager at Woolworths develops a new performance review framework and manages a redundancy process in compliance with the NES.
Employees Fulfil their contracted duties, meet performance expectations, follow workplace policies and exercise their rights under the NES and applicable awards or agreements. 💡 Exam tip: when asked about participants, always state their ROLE (what they do), not just who they are. The Fair Work Commission is the key independent body — it does not represent employers or employees; it is a neutral tribunal.

📋 Awards & agreements — determining wages & conditions ▼
MethodWhat it isWho it coversKey features
Modern Award A legally binding document set by the Fair Work Commission that outlines minimum wages and conditions for employees in a specific industry or occupation Employees not covered by an enterprise agreement — most award-reliant workers in retail, hospitality, construction etc. Sets the floor — employers cannot pay below award rates. Includes minimum pay rates, penalty rates, allowances, leave entitlements and overtime.
Enterprise Agreement (EA) A negotiated agreement between an employer (or group of employers) and employees (often represented by a union) that sets wages and conditions for a specific workplace Employees at the specific business or site covered by the agreement Must pass the "Better Off Overall Test" (BOOT) — employees must be better off under the EA than under the relevant award. Approved by the Fair Work Commission.
National Employment Standards (NES) 10 minimum standards of employment set by the Fair Work Act 2009 that apply to all national system employees — cannot be excluded by any award or agreement All employees covered by the national workplace relations system Includes: maximum weekly hours, flexible working requests, parental leave, annual leave, personal leave, notice of termination, redundancy pay, and the Fair Work Information Statement.

+ Enterprise Agreement advantages

  • Can offer conditions above the award — e.g. higher pay, extra leave, flexible rosters tailored to the workplace.
  • Provides certainty for both employer and employees over the agreement period (typically 3-4 years).
  • Employees have a say in the negotiation process, increasing ownership and satisfaction.

− Enterprise Agreement disadvantages

  • Negotiation can be lengthy, costly and contentious, especially where unions are involved.
  • Locks the business into fixed conditions that may become uncompetitive if the industry changes.
  • Must pass the BOOT — if employees would be worse off than under the award, the FWC will not approve it.

💡 Hierarchy: NES > Enterprise Agreement > Modern Award. The NES always applies — it cannot be traded away. An enterprise agreement replaces the award for covered employees but must still pass the BOOT.

⚖ Dispute resolution — mediation & arbitration  ★ HOT TOPICDispute resolution process is regularly examined — know each step and who is involved ▼

The dispute resolution process

StageWhat happensWho is involvedOutcome
1. Direct negotiation The employee raises the issue directly with their manager or employer. The parties attempt to resolve the dispute informally. Employee and manager/employer Dispute resolved internally — no external involvement required
2. Grievance procedure If direct negotiation fails, the employee lodges a formal grievance through the business's internal dispute resolution procedure (usually outlined in the enterprise agreement or workplace policy). Employee, HR manager, union representative (if applicable) Formal record created; HR investigates and attempts resolution
3. Conciliation / Mediation An independent third party (conciliator or mediator) facilitates discussion between the parties to help them reach a mutually agreed solution. The conciliator does NOT impose a decision. Employee, employer, Fair Work Commission conciliator Voluntary agreement — both parties must consent to the outcome. No binding decision is made.
4. Arbitration If conciliation fails, an arbitrator (often the Fair Work Commission) hears both sides and makes a legally binding decision that both parties must comply with. Employee, employer, Fair Work Commission arbitrator Binding determination — both parties are legally required to comply with the outcome

Mediation vs Arbitration — key distinction

💬 Mediation (Conciliation)

  • Facilitated by a neutral third party who helps the parties communicate and negotiate.
  • The mediator does NOT make a decision — the parties reach their own agreement.
  • Outcome is voluntary and only binding if both parties agree.
  • Faster, cheaper and less adversarial — preserves the employment relationship.
  • Example: A Fair Work Commission conciliator facilitates a discussion between a dismissed employee and their former employer, resulting in an agreed compensation payment.

⚖ Arbitration

  • The arbitrator hears evidence from both sides and makes a binding ruling.
  • The decision is legally enforceable — neither party can simply reject it.
  • More formal, time-consuming and expensive than mediation.
  • Used when parties cannot reach agreement through negotiation or mediation.
  • Example: After conciliation fails in an unfair dismissal case, the Fair Work Commission arbitrates and orders the employer to reinstate the employee.

💡 Key exam distinction: mediation = parties decide together (no binding ruling); arbitration = third party decides for them (legally binding). Always state which stage the dispute has reached and who the relevant participant is.

📝 Model answer — discuss a motivation strategy (4 marks) ▼

Sample question: Discuss the use of performance-related pay as a motivation strategy at Acme Manufacturing. (4 marks)

Step 1 — Define the strategy: Performance-related pay is a financial motivation strategy where employees receive bonuses or pay increases directly linked to achieving specific measurable targets.
Step 2 — Link to theory (adds marks): This strategy addresses Maslow's physiological and esteem needs by increasing income and recognising achievement. It also satisfies Lawrence & Nohria's drive to acquire, as employees are rewarded with greater financial status.
Step 3 — Advantage + scenario link: For Acme Manufacturing, performance-related pay would directly incentivise production line workers to meet output targets, as higher throughput leads to a measurable financial reward they can see in their pay packet.
Step 4 — Disadvantage + scenario link: However, this strategy may create unhealthy competition between Acme's production teams, with workers focused on individual bonuses rather than supporting colleagues, potentially reducing overall team cohesion and product quality.
Step 5 — Conclusion (evaluate/justify only): Overall, performance-related pay is appropriate for Acme Manufacturing if individual output is measurable and the business prioritises productivity. If teamwork is critical to operations, a profit-sharing model would better balance individual and collective motivation.

💡 Linking to a motivation theory is not always required but will earn you higher marks — do it whenever you can naturally weave it in.

📝 Model answer — apply a motivation theory to a scenario (4 marks) ▼

Sample question: Using Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs, explain how the manager at Metro Cafe could increase staff motivation. (4 marks)

Step 1 — Explain the theory: Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs proposes that employees are motivated by five levels of needs, from physiological at the base to self-actualisation at the top. Lower-level needs must be satisfied before higher-level needs become motivating.
Step 2 — Identify the current level in the scenario: The case study indicates Metro Cafe staff have stable wages and safe conditions, suggesting their physiological and safety needs are already met. The manager should therefore focus on the social (level 3) needs.
Step 3 — Recommend and justify: The manager at Metro Cafe could organise regular team lunches and encourage peer mentoring between experienced and new staff. This would satisfy the social need for belonging and friendship, moving employees toward level 4 (esteem), which would further increase motivation and engagement.
Step 4 — Overall conclusion: By systematically addressing each level of Maslow's hierarchy, Metro Cafe's manager can build a workplace where staff feel secure, connected, valued and challenged — progressively increasing motivation at each stage.

💡 Always identify which level the employees ARE at before recommending what they NEED next. Recommending physiological needs for highly-paid professionals loses marks.
