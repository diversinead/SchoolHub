📊 Motivation theories — comparison  ★ HOT TOPICOne of the most frequently examined areas in Topic 2 ▼

🏢 Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (1943)

People are motivated by five levels of needs, arranged in a hierarchy. Lower-level needs must be satisfied before higher-level needs can motivate behaviour. Managers must identify which level an employee is currently at and offer rewards that satisfy the NEXT level.

5. Self-actualisation — reaching full potential, creativity, meaningful work
4. Esteem — recognition, status, achievement, respect from others
3. Social — belonging, friendship, teamwork, acceptance
2. Safety — job security, safe working conditions, stable income
1. Physiological — basic pay, food, shelter, rest

⚠ Exam tip: Do NOT just list the five levels. Explain how the theory guides a manager to identify an employee's current level and offer needs at the NEXT level up.

🎯 Locke & Latham's Goal-Setting Theory (1968)

Employees are motivated when they are set specific, challenging but achievable goals. The theory identifies five key principles for effective goal-setting, remembered with the acronym SCCEA:

PrincipleWhat it meansExample
S — SpecificityGoals must be clear and precise, not vague"Increase sales by 10% this quarter" not "do better"
C — ChallengeGoals must stretch the employee but remain achievableA target that requires effort but is not impossible
C — CommitmentEmployees must genuinely accept and agree to the goalInvolving staff in setting their own KPIs
E — FeedbackRegular progress updates help employees stay on trackMonthly performance reviews with the manager
A — Task complexityComplex goals need to be broken into manageable stepsA large project split into weekly milestones

⚠ Exam tip: The exam may ask you to explain ONE of the five principles — always name the principle, define it, and link to the scenario.

👥 Lawrence & Nohria's Four Drive Theory (2002)

Employees are motivated by four fundamental emotional drives. Unlike Maslow, these drives do not form a hierarchy — all four operate simultaneously and independently. Managers should address ALL four drives to fully motivate staff.

DriveWhat it meansHow managers can address it
Drive to AcquireDesire for material goods, money, status and experiencesCompetitive salaries, bonuses, promotions, perks
Drive to BondDesire for social connection, belonging and caring relationshipsTeamwork, social events, inclusive culture, recognition
Drive to ComprehendDesire to learn, grow, make sense of the world and be challengedTraining, interesting work, stretch assignments, autonomy
Drive to DefendDesire to protect oneself, one's achievements and one's valuesJob security, fair processes, transparent communication

⚠ Exam tip: Lawrence & Nohria is often compared to Maslow. Key difference — L&N has NO hierarchy; all four drives operate at the same time.

Financial strategies

StrategyWhat it involves+ Advantage− Disadvantage
Remuneration Base salary or wages paid for work performed Provides financial security, attracting and retaining staff Does not reward performance above minimum expectations
Performance-related pay Bonuses or pay increases linked to achieving targets Directly incentivises effort and high performance Can create unhealthy competition and reduce teamwork

Non-financial strategies

StrategyWhat it involves+ Advantage− Disadvantage
Recognition & awards Publicly acknowledging employee achievements (Employee of the Month, awards) Satisfies esteem needs at low cost; encourages desired behaviour in others Can cause resentment among staff who feel overlooked or unfairly judged
Job enrichment Making roles more meaningful by adding greater responsibility and variety Addresses higher-level needs (Maslow) and the drive to comprehend (L&N) Not all employees want more responsibility; can increase stress and workload

▶ Training types — advantages & disadvantages  ★ HOT TOPICDiscuss or evaluate training questions appear regularly ▼

On-the-job training

+ Advantages

  • Employees learn skills directly relevant to their specific role, reducing the time before they become productive.
  • Lower cost than off-the-job training as no external provider or venue is required.
  • Training occurs in the real work environment, so skills transfer immediately to daily tasks.

− Disadvantages

  • Quality depends on the trainer's skills and patience — poor trainers produce poor outcomes.
  • Disrupts normal work operations as both trainer and trainee are occupied during working hours.
  • May pass on bad habits or outdated practices if the trainer lacks current best-practice knowledge.

Off-the-job training

+ Advantages

  • Delivered by specialist experts, ensuring employees receive high-quality, up-to-date knowledge and skills.
  • Removes employees from workplace distractions, allowing them to focus entirely on learning.
  • Broadens employee skills beyond current role requirements, supporting career development.

− Disadvantages

  • More expensive than on-the-job training due to external provider fees, travel and accommodation costs.
  • Lost productivity while employees are away from their normal duties during training.
  • Skills learned may not be directly applicable to the specific tools or processes used in the business.

Online (e-learning) training

+ Advantages

  • Flexible — employees can complete training at their own pace and at a time that suits them.
  • Cost-effective for large workforces as the same content can be delivered to many employees simultaneously.
  • Easy to update and keep current without the expense of re-running face-to-face programs.

− Disadvantages

  • Lacks the hands-on, practical component needed for technical or physical skills (e.g. operating machinery).
  • Requires reliable internet access and technology, which may not be available for all staff.
  • Lower engagement and completion rates as employees may defer or rush through modules without supervision.

LocationAt the workplaceExternal venue/providerAnywhere with internet

💡 Match the training type to the scenario — if the business needs staff trained quickly on specific tasks, on-the-job is justified; if specialised qualifications are needed, off-the-job is better.

📊 Performance management strategies — overview ▼
StrategyWhat it involves+ Benefit− Limitation
Management by objectives (MBO) Manager and employee jointly set specific, measurable performance goals for the period Increases employee commitment because they helped set the goals Time-consuming to set individual objectives for all staff

▶ Termination of employment — types & entitlements  ★ HOT TOPICDistinguishing between termination types is a common exam question ▼
TypeDefinitionWho initiates?EntitlementsExample
Resignation Employee voluntarily chooses to leave the business Employee Annual leave payout; notice period may apply A cafe manager resigns to start their own business
Retirement Employee voluntarily leaves the workforce permanently, usually due to age Employee Superannuation, annual leave payout, farewell recognition A 65-year-old warehouse supervisor retires after 20 years of service
Redundancy The employee's position is no longer required due to restructuring, automation or reduced business activity Employer Redundancy pay based on years of service, notice period, annual leave payout Qantas made 8,500 positions redundant during COVID-19 as flights ceased
Dismissal (summary) Immediate termination due to serious misconduct — no notice required Employer Outstanding wages only; no notice pay An employee is dismissed on the spot after being caught stealing from the register
Dismissal (general) Termination due to ongoing poor performance or behaviour after warnings have been given Employer Notice period, annual leave payout; must follow fair process An employee is dismissed after three documented warnings for repeated lateness

Unfair vs wrongful dismissal

🔎 Unfair dismissal

  • When an employee is dismissed in a harsh, unjust or unreasonable manner without following proper procedure.
  • Employee can apply to the Fair Work Commission for reinstatement or compensation.
  • Example: dismissing an employee without warnings or without giving them a chance to respond to allegations.

⚠ Wrongful dismissal

  • When an employee is dismissed in breach of their employment contract (e.g. without required notice period).
  • Employee may pursue legal action for breach of contract in court.
  • Example: terminating an employee two weeks before their contract entitles them to redundancy pay.

Entitlement obligations summary

Employers MUST provide: payment of all outstanding wages · accrued annual leave entitlements · required notice period (or pay in lieu) · redundancy pay where applicable
Key legislation: Fair Work Act 2009 · National Employment Standards (NES) · applicable Modern Award

💡 Key distinction: redundancy = the JOB is no longer needed (not the person). Dismissal = the PERSON'S conduct or performance is the reason. This difference matters in exam scenarios.

📝 Model answer — discuss a motivation strategy (4 marks) ▼

Sample question: Discuss the use of performance-related pay as a motivation strategy at Acme Manufacturing. (4 marks)

Step 1 — Define the strategy: Performance-related pay is a financial motivation strategy where employees receive bonuses or pay increases directly linked to achieving specific measurable targets.
Step 2 — Link to theory (adds marks): This strategy addresses Maslow's physiological and esteem needs by increasing income and recognising achievement. It also satisfies Lawrence & Nohria's drive to acquire, as employees are rewarded with greater financial status.
Step 3 — Advantage + scenario link: For Acme Manufacturing, performance-related pay would directly incentivise production line workers to meet output targets, as higher throughput leads to a measurable financial reward they can see in their pay packet.
Step 4 — Disadvantage + scenario link: However, this strategy may create unhealthy competition between Acme's production teams, with workers focused on individual bonuses rather than supporting colleagues, potentially reducing overall team cohesion and product quality.
Step 5 — Conclusion (evaluate/justify only): Overall, performance-related pay is appropriate for Acme Manufacturing if individual output is measurable and the business prioritises productivity. If teamwork is critical to operations, a profit-sharing model would better balance individual and collective motivation.

💡 Linking to a motivation theory is not always required but will earn you higher marks — do it whenever you can naturally weave it in.

📝 Model answer — apply a motivation theory to a scenario (4 marks) ▼

Sample question: Using Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs, explain how the manager at Metro Cafe could increase staff motivation. (4 marks)

Step 1 — Explain the theory: Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs proposes that employees are motivated by five levels of needs, from physiological at the base to self-actualisation at the top. Lower-level needs must be satisfied before higher-level needs become motivating.
Step 2 — Identify the current level in the scenario: The case study indicates Metro Cafe staff have stable wages and safe conditions, suggesting their physiological and safety needs are already met. The manager should therefore focus on the social (level 3) needs.
Step 3 — Recommend and justify: The manager at Metro Cafe could organise regular team lunches and encourage peer mentoring between experienced and new staff. This would satisfy the social need for belonging and friendship, moving employees toward level 4 (esteem), which would further increase motivation and engagement.
Step 4 — Overall conclusion: By systematically addressing each level of Maslow's hierarchy, Metro Cafe's manager can build a workplace where staff feel secure, connected, valued and challenged — progressively increasing motivation at each stage.

💡 Always identify which level the employees ARE at before recommending what they NEED next. Recommending physiological needs for highly-paid professionals loses marks.
