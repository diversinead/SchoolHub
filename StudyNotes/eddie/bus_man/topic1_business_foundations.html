▶ Management styles — advantages & disadvantages ▼

Autocratic style

+ Advantages

  • Fast decisions are made without waiting for consultation, which is valuable in a crisis or time-sensitive situation.
  • Clear chain of command reduces confusion about who is responsible for outcomes.
  • Effective with new or unskilled staff who require direct guidance to complete tasks correctly.

− Disadvantages

  • Low employee morale and high staff turnover because workers feel undervalued and ignored.
  • Reduces creativity and innovation as employees are not encouraged to contribute ideas.
  • Dependent on the manager being correct — poor decisions have no check from staff input.

Persuasive style

+ Advantages

  • Staff understand the reasoning behind decisions, which increases buy-in and motivation to carry them out.
  • Retains managerial control while improving staff engagement compared to autocratic style.
  • Suitable when implementing changes that staff may initially resist, as explanation reduces pushback.

− Disadvantages

  • Staff may feel manipulated if they sense the consultation is one-sided, reducing trust in management.
  • Does not make use of employee expertise — valuable insights from the workforce are ignored.
  • Can be time-consuming as the manager must justify every decision to employees.

Consultative style

+ Advantages

  • Draws on employee expertise and experience, which can improve the quality of decisions.
  • Increases staff morale and job satisfaction as employees feel their opinions are valued.
  • Manager retains final decision-making authority, maintaining accountability and control.

− Disadvantages

  • Slower decision-making process as the manager must gather and consider employee input first.
  • Staff may feel frustrated if their suggestions are consistently ignored after being consulted.
  • Requires skilled communication from the manager to consult without creating false expectations.

Participative style

+ Advantages

  • High level of staff ownership over decisions leads to greater commitment and accountability.
  • Encourages creativity and innovation, making it well-suited to businesses needing new ideas.
  • Builds a collaborative culture that can improve team cohesion and long-term retention.

− Disadvantages

  • Very slow decision-making process as reaching group consensus takes significant time.
  • Risk of groupthink where the desire for harmony prevents critical analysis of ideas.
  • Unsuitable in crises or with unskilled staff who lack the knowledge to contribute meaningfully.

Laissez-faire style

+ Advantages

  • Maximises staff autonomy and creativity, ideal for highly skilled professionals such as researchers or designers.
  • Demonstrates high trust in employees, which can boost motivation and job satisfaction.
  • Manager is freed to focus on strategic tasks rather than day-to-day operational decisions.

− Disadvantages

  • Lack of direction and oversight can lead to inconsistent outcomes and missed deadlines.
  • Ineffective with new or unskilled staff who need guidance and structure to perform well.
  • Accountability is unclear when problems arise, making it difficult to address performance issues.

💡 Always use the scenario details to justify your chosen style — marks are lost for generic answers that do not reference the specific business.

🏛 Corporate culture — formal vs informal  ★ HOT TOPICFrequently examined in both short answer and extended response ▼
FeatureFormal (official) cultureInformal (real) culture
DefinitionThe intended culture expressed through official documents, policies and mission statementsThe actual culture that develops through day-to-day behaviour, norms and unwritten rules
ExamplesStaff code of conduct, vision statement, organisational chartLunchroom habits, unofficial hierarchies, how staff actually treat each other
Shaped bySenior management and HR policiesShared experiences, stories, role models within the team
VisibilityVisible — written and communicated explicitlyOften invisible — assumed and rarely stated

+ Strong culture benefits

  • Increases staff motivation and engagement as employees share common values and purpose.
  • Reduces need for close supervision as behaviour is guided by shared norms.
  • Attracts talent who align with the values, improving recruitment and retention.

− Weak or toxic culture risks

  • High staff turnover and absenteeism as employees feel disconnected or undervalued.
  • Resistance to change when a negative informal culture conflicts with new management direction.
  • Can damage brand reputation if toxic culture becomes public knowledge.

💡 Exams may show a gap between formal and informal culture — identify BOTH and explain the impact on the business.

📝 Model answer structure — discuss/evaluate a management style ▼

Sample question: Discuss the use of the autocratic management style at Metro Cafe. (4 marks)

Step 1 — Define the term: The autocratic management style is an approach where the manager makes all decisions independently, without seeking input from employees.
Step 2 — Advantage + link to scenario: This style would benefit Metro Cafe as it allows the owner to make quick decisions during busy service periods without waiting for staff input, maintaining efficiency in a fast-paced environment.
Step 3 — Disadvantage + link to scenario: However, using this style consistently may lower staff morale at Metro Cafe, as experienced baristas and chefs may feel their expertise is not valued, potentially leading to higher turnover.
Step 4 — Conclusion (for evaluate/justify questions only): Overall, the autocratic style is only appropriate for Metro Cafe in short-term crisis situations. For everyday operations, a consultative approach would better utilise staff experience and improve retention.

💡 Step 4 is only required for "evaluate" or "justify" questions, not "discuss". A conclusion without a judgement still loses marks on an evaluate question.
