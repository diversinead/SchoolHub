📋 Short Answer Questions
Short Answer2 marks
Distinguish between internal and external sources of finance, and give ONE example of each. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Distinction (1 mark): Internal sources of finance come from within the business itself (e.g. retained profits or additional owner's capital). External sources come from outside the business, usually involving a lender or supplier (e.g. a bank loan or trade credit from a supplier).

Examples (1 mark): Internal example — reinvesting $8,000 of retained profit to purchase a new espresso machine. External example — a $20,000 term loan from NAB to renovate premises.

⚠ A common mistake is confusing "owner's capital" (internal) with a "bank loan" (external). Owner contributions are internal because they come from the owner, who is part of the business entity.
Short Answer★ HOT4 marks
Debbie Doherty is considering a $6,000 loan to purchase a new computer server for her business. Her bank has offered a 4-year loan at a flat interest rate of 10% p.a., with monthly repayments.
(a) Calculate the total interest Doherty will pay. (1 mark)
(b) Calculate her monthly repayment. (1 mark)
(c) Is the real cost of her loan 10% p.a.? Justify your answer using the concept of effective rate of interest. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Total interest (1 mark): Interest = Principal x Rate x Time = $6,000 x 0.10 x 4 = $2,400.

(b) Monthly repayment (1 mark): Total to repay = $6,000 + $2,400 = $8,400. Over 48 months: $8,400 / 48 = $175 per month.

(c) Effective rate (2 marks): No — the real cost is much higher than 10%. The flat rate is applied to the original $6,000 every year, even though Doherty is paying the principal down throughout the loan. Average interest per year = $2,400 / 4 = $600. Average balance outstanding = $6,000 / 2 = $3,000. Effective rate = $600 / $3,000 x 100 = 20% p.a. — roughly double the stated flat rate.

⚠ Always show the formula AND the calculation. Stating "20%" without workings loses a mark. The effective rate being ~2x the flat rate is a common VCE exam trap.
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Vincent Ngo is taking out a 25-year mortgage of $545,000 at 6% p.a. to purchase a business property. Interest is calculated daily and charged monthly. Repayments of $3,512 are made monthly.
(a) Does the 6% refer to flat rate or reducing balance interest? Explain how it is applied. (2 marks)
(b) Would Ngo be better off making fortnightly repayments instead of monthly ones? Explain why. (1 mark)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Interest type (2 marks): The 6% is a reducing balance rate. The bank calculates interest daily on the outstanding loan balance, which falls every time Ngo makes a repayment. As the balance shrinks, the interest charged each month also shrinks, meaning a larger portion of each $3,512 repayment goes to principal over time.

(b) Fortnightly vs monthly (1 mark): Yes — fortnightly repayments reduce the principal faster because interest is calculated on the outstanding balance. Because there are 26 fortnights (vs 12 months) in a year, paying half the monthly amount every fortnight results in an extra month's repayment each year, reducing total interest paid and shortening the loan term.

⚠ Mortgages are always reducing balance — flat rate mortgages do not exist in Australia. Name the specific advantage (less interest over the life of the loan) rather than just saying "it's better".
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Sammy's Panels has total assets of $177,000, total liabilities of $118,000 and owner's capital of $59,000. Sue's Body Shop has total assets of $194,000, total liabilities of $68,000 and owner's capital of $126,000.
(a) Calculate the debt ratio for each business. (2 marks)
(b) Comment on which business carries greater financial risk. (1 mark)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Debt ratios (2 marks):
Sammy's Panels: $118,000 / $177,000 x 100 = 66.7%.
Sue's Body Shop: $68,000 / $194,000 x 100 = 35.1%.

(b) Financial risk (1 mark): Sammy's Panels carries greater financial risk. With 66.7% of its assets funded by debt, it is highly geared. It must meet substantial interest and principal repayments even if profit falls, and has less buffer before becoming unable to cover its liabilities. Sue's Body Shop is lowly geared and more resilient.

💡 When commenting on risk, always link the number to a consequence (inability to meet interest, insolvency risk) — not just "high" or "low".
Short Answer3 marks
Explain THREE factors a bank would consider when deciding whether to approve a small business loan application. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Factor 1 — Capacity (1 mark): The business's ability to repay the loan from future cash flow. The bank examines cash budgets and income statements to confirm projected cash receipts are sufficient to cover monthly repayments plus existing expenses.

Factor 2 — Collateral (1 mark): Assets the borrower can pledge as security for the loan. Property, vehicles or equipment can be seized and sold by the bank if the borrower defaults, reducing the bank's risk.

Factor 3 — Character / credit rating (1 mark): The borrower's track record — have they repaid previous debts on time? A clean credit history and good reputation with suppliers signals low default risk and improves the chance of approval.

💡 Any three of the "5 Cs of credit" (Character, Capacity, Capital, Collateral, Conditions) earn full marks. Must explain WHAT each factor is and WHY the bank cares.
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Joel Lombardo invested $40,000 in Paintball Arena on 1 July 2027. For the year ended 30 June 2028, the business earned a net profit of $5,000. The current interest rate available on a term deposit is 4.5%.
(a) Calculate Lombardo's return on owner's investment. (1 mark)
(b) Comment on his ROI, taking into account alternative returns in the finance market. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) ROI (1 mark): $5,000 / $40,000 x 100 = 12.5%.

(b) Comment (2 marks): Lombardo's 12.5% return comfortably exceeds the 4.5% he could earn risk-free in a term deposit, so on a pure numerical basis his investment is paying off. However, running a business carries significantly more risk — his capital is not guaranteed, and he is also contributing his own time and labour which are not counted as expenses. A ROI of 12.5% is reasonable but not exceptional; he should consider whether the additional risk and effort justify an extra 8 percentage points over a risk-free alternative.

⚠ A high-scoring ROI answer always COMPARES the return to a risk-free alternative and acknowledges the risk/effort premium required for business investment.
📚 Extended Response Questions
Extended Response★ HOT5 marks
Ace Electricals and Acme Electricals both specialise in hiring electrical appliances. Both have total assets of $125,000 and net profit of $20,000. However, Ace has liabilities of $25,000 while Acme has liabilities of $50,000.
(a) Calculate the owner's equity, debt ratio and return on owner's investment for each business. (3 marks)
(b) Given that the two businesses have the same total assets and same net profit, explain why one owner is receiving a greater return on the funds invested. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Calculations (3 marks):

Ace Electricals: Owner's equity = $125,000 − $25,000 = $100,000. Debt ratio = $25,000 / $125,000 x 100 = 20%. ROI = $20,000 / $100,000 x 100 = 20%.

Acme Electricals: Owner's equity = $125,000 − $50,000 = $75,000. Debt ratio = $50,000 / $125,000 x 100 = 40%. ROI = $20,000 / $75,000 x 100 = 26.7%.

(b) Explanation (2 marks): Acme's owner is receiving a greater return because the business is more highly geared — 40% of its assets are funded by debt compared to only 20% for Ace. The same $20,000 net profit is being divided over a smaller pool of owner's capital ($75,000 vs $100,000), which magnifies the percentage return. This is the positive side of gearing: when a business is profitable, higher gearing amplifies the owner's return. However, this is a double-edged sword — in a bad year where profit falls, Acme's ROI would fall much more sharply than Ace's, and Acme must still meet its higher interest commitments regardless of profit.

⚠ The full 2 marks for (b) require explaining BOTH sides of gearing — the amplification of returns AND the corresponding risk. Answers that only describe the upside earn 1 mark.
Extended Response★ HOT6 marks
Janette Garro, an architect, is considering how to acquire a new $30,000 car for her business. She will keep the car for 3 years, after which it will be worth about $20,000. Her two options are: (1) Pay $5,000 cash and borrow $25,000 from the bank at 10% p.a. flat over 3 years. (2) Lease a similar car at $100 per week for 3 years.
(a) Calculate the total cost of each option over the 3-year period. (2 marks)
(b) State ONE advantage and ONE disadvantage of each option. (2 marks)
(c) Write a recommendation for Garro, justifying your conclusion with reference to both financial and non-financial factors. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Total costs (2 marks):

Option 1 (Buy with loan): Cash deposit $5,000 + Loan principal $25,000 + Interest ($25,000 x 0.10 x 3) $7,500 = $37,500 paid. Less resale value of $20,000 = Net cost $17,500 over 3 years.

Option 2 (Lease): $100 x 52 weeks x 3 years = $15,600 over 3 years. No resale since she does not own the car.

(b) Advantages & disadvantages (2 marks): Buy: Advantage — Garro owns the asset and recovers $20,000 at resale. Disadvantage — $5,000 upfront deposit and $7,500 of interest tie up cash and add cost. Lease: Advantage — no large upfront payment, easier to budget at a fixed weekly cost. Disadvantage — she has nothing to show at the end of 3 years and may face restrictions on kilometres or wear.

(c) Recommendation (2 marks): Garro should choose Option 1 (buy with loan). Although the weekly lease cost looks lower, once the $20,000 resale value is factored in, the net cost of buying ($17,500) is very similar to leasing ($15,600) — and buying leaves Garro with the equity, flexibility over use (no kilometre restrictions), and the option to keep or sell the car as she prefers. If cash flow is tight, leasing may be preferable, but as a long-term strategy for a stable architecture business, ownership builds asset value on the balance sheet and supports future borrowing capacity.

⚠ A strong recommendation must: (1) pick one option clearly, (2) justify with BOTH a financial reason and a non-financial reason, (3) acknowledge conditions under which the alternative might be better. Opinion without justification earns zero.
