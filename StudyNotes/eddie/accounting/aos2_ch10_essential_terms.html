💡 A 6% flat rate over 5 years is roughly equal to an 11% reducing balance rate — always check which one is being quoted.

📈 Flat Rate vs Reducing Balance — Year-by-Year ▼

Scenario: Debbie borrows $6,000 at 10% over 4 years. She repays $1,500 of principal each year.

Year Openingbalance owed Flat rate10% of $6,000 Reducing10% of balance Closingbalance owed
1$6,000$600$600$4,500
2$4,500$600$450$3,000
3$3,000$600$300$1,500
4$1,500$600$150$0
Total interest $2,400 $1,500

Notice Year 4: Debbie only owes $1,500, but the flat rate still charges her $600 — as if she owed the full original amount. That is why flat rate costs nearly $1,000 more over the life of the loan.

The effective rate of the flat loan
Avg interest per year = $2,400 ÷ 4 = $600
Avg balance owing = ($6,000 + $0) ÷ 2 = $3,000
Effective rate = $600 ÷ $3,000 × 100 = 20% p.a.

💡 A 10% flat rate loan really costs about the same as a 20% reducing balance loan. The effective rate is roughly double the flat rate — always check which type is being quoted.

🧮 Key Formulas ▼

Flat Rate Interest

Principal x Rate x Time

Total interest on a loan where rate is applied to the original amount borrowed.

Borrow $10,000 at 8% flat for 3 years
= 10,000 x 0.08 x 3 = $2,400 total interest

Effective Rate of Interest

Avg annual interest / Avg balance x 100

The true annual cost of a flat-rate loan, accounting for the fact the principal is being paid off.

$2,400 / 3 = $800 avg interest p.a.
Avg balance = $5,000
= 800 / 5,000 x 100 = 16% p.a. effective

Debt Ratio

Total Liabilities / Total Assets x 100

Measures what percentage of assets are funded by debt rather than owner equity.

Liabilities $40,000 / Assets $100,000
= 40% (40c of every $1 in assets is debt-funded)

Return on Owner's Investment (ROI)

Net Profit / Owner's Capital x 100

Percentage return the owner earns on the capital they have personally invested in the business.

Net profit $15,000, Owner's capital $60,000
= 15,000 / 60,000 x 100 = 25%

💡 For ROI, use average capital (opening + closing / 2) if the capital balance changed during the year.

📊 Low Gearing vs High Gearing ▼
FeatureLow GearingHigh Gearing
Debt ratio Below ~50% Above ~50%
Funded mostly by safeOwner equity riskyBorrowed funds
Risk Lower — fewer fixed interest commitments Higher — must meet interest payments even in bad years
ROI in good times Modest boost amplifiedMagnified upward
ROI in bad times Mild dip amplifiedMagnified downward
Lender view Easy to get more credit Seen as higher credit risk

💡 Gearing is a double-edged sword — it amplifies returns in both directions. Neither is automatically better; it depends on risk tolerance and stability of profits.

⚒ Gearing's Effect on ROI — worked example ▼

Scenario: Sarah's Cafe needs $100,000 in total assets. Profit before interest is $14,000. Interest rate on debt is 8%. Compare two funding mixes.

ItemOption A: Low gearingOption B: High gearing
Owner's capital$80,000$20,000
Borrowed (debt)$20,000$80,000
Debt ratio20%80%
Profit before interest$14,000$14,000
Interest expense (8%)($1,600)($6,400)
Net profit$12,400$7,600
ROI12,400 / 80,000 = 15.5%7,600 / 20,000 = 38%

💡 The high-gearing owner earns a much bigger ROI because they put in less of their own money — but they also carry much higher risk. If profit fell to $6,000, Option B would make a loss after interest, while Option A would still be profitable.
