💡 The Accounts Receivable column must always equal Sales + GST. This is your check that the journal balances.

📋 Purchases Journal — worked example ▼

Used to record credit purchases — when the business buys goods/services on credit and will pay later.

Date Accounts Payable Inv. No. Equipment asset up Supplies asset up GST asset up Acc. Payable liability up
20 AprEquipment & CoX84 330-33363
Bought a tripod $330 excl. GST on credit — Equipment up, GST Receivable up, Acc. Payable up.
Totals $ 330$ 0$ 33$ 363
Acc. Payable column must equal all other columns combined ✓

💡 The Accounts Payable column must always equal the sum of all other columns. Column headings depend on what the business regularly buys on credit.

📊 Cash vs Credit Transactions — comparison ▼
FeatureCash TransactionCredit Transaction
Payment timing Immediate — cash changes hands now Deferred — pay/receive later
Journal used (sales) Cash Receipts Journal Sales Journal
Journal used (purchases) Cash Payments Journal Purchases Journal
Bank affected? yesBank column used noNo Bank column
GST impact GST Payable (sales) or GST Receivable (purchases) Same GST rules apply — just recorded in different journal

💡 The key difference is timing — credit means the exchange of goods/services happens NOW but the money moves LATER.

🧮 GST Balance Formula ▼

GST Closing Balance

Opening + CRJ + SJ - CPJ - PJ

Add GST collected from sales (CRJ and SJ). Subtract GST paid on purchases (CPJ and PJ).

Opening: $550 (receivable)
+ CRJ: $22 + SJ: $150 = $172 collected
- CPJ: $80 - PJ: $33 = $113 paid
Closing = 550 + 172 - 113 = $609 Receivable (Asset)

GST Extracted from incl. price

Price incl. GST / 11

When a price includes GST, divide by 11 to find the GST component.

$660 incl. GST
GST = 660 / 11 = $60
Sales (excl.) = 660 - 60 = $600

GST Added to excl. price

Price excl. GST x 10%

When a price excludes GST, multiply by 0.10 to find GST to add.

$900 excl. GST
GST = 900 x 0.10 = $90
Total = 900 + 90 = $990

💡 Always check whether a price is stated as including or excluding GST before calculating — this is the most common exam mistake.
