📝 Short Answer Questions

Short Answer2 marks
Distinguish between concentric and eccentric muscle contractions. Give one sporting example of each.
✅ Model Answer
✓A concentric contraction occurs when the muscle shortens as it generates force — the muscle force exceeds the load. Example: the quadriceps contract concentrically during the upward phase of a squat or when kicking a football. (1 mark)
✓An eccentric contraction occurs when the muscle lengthens under tension — the load exceeds the muscle force. The muscle acts as a brake to control movement. Example: the quadriceps contract eccentrically during the downward phase of a squat, or the hamstrings eccentrically control knee extension speed when kicking. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: The key distinguishing phrase is shortens vs lengthens. Students often write "the muscle relaxes" for eccentric — this is wrong. The muscle is still ACTIVELY contracting; it is just being overcome by the load.
Short Answer3 marks
Describe the pathway of nervous control from the brain to muscle contraction. Name the structures involved at each stage.
✅ Model Answer
✓Stage 1-2 (1 mark): The brain (motor cortex) initiates the movement signal, which travels down the spinal cord and branches out into motor nerves.
✓Stage 3 (1 mark): The signal passes into individual motor neurons, travelling along the myelinated axon toward the target muscle.
✓Stage 4 (1 mark): At the neuromuscular junction (NMJ), the nerve impulse triggers the release of acetylcholine (ACh) from the axon terminal into the synaptic cleft. ACh binds to receptors on the motor end plate, generating an action potential in the muscle fibre that spreads via T-tubules, releasing Ca2+ from the SR and triggering contraction.
⚠ Examiner tip: Name specific structures at each stage — brain, spinal cord, motor neurons, NMJ, ACh, sarcolemma, T-tubules, SR. Vague answers like "the signal goes to the muscle" score zero. Use the textbook 4-stage framework as your structure.
Short Answer3 marks
Explain the rank order of force production for the three main types of muscle contraction and provide a reason why the highest force type produces the most force.
✅ Model Answer
✓Rank order (1 mark): Eccentric contractions produce the most force, followed by isometric, then concentric. (Eccentric > Isometric > Concentric)
✓Reason for eccentric superiority (1 mark): During eccentric contractions, the sarcomere is stretched under load, which increases cross-bridge tension and allows elastic energy to be stored in the muscle-tendon unit, generating greater total force than active contraction alone.
✓Practical application (1 mark): This explains why athletes can lower heavier loads than they can lift — for example, a weightlifter can eccentrically lower significantly more weight than they can concentrically press overhead.
⚠ Examiner tip: Many students incorrectly rank isometric as producing the most force. Remember EIC — Eccentric Is Clearly the strongest. The reason (sarcomere stretch and elastic energy) is what separates a high-scoring answer from a basic one.
Short Answer4 marks
Explain how force is graded in skeletal muscle. In your answer, refer to both motor unit recruitment and rate coding.
✅ Model Answer
✓Motor unit recruitment (2 marks): Force is increased by recruiting more motor units — each additional unit activates a new group of muscle fibres, adding their force to the total. According to the size principle, units are recruited smallest to largest (Type I first, then IIa, then IIx) as intensity increases. A light activity such as walking uses only a few small Type I motor units, while sprinting requires near-maximal recruitment of all available units including Type IIx.
✓Rate coding (2 marks): Force can also be increased by raising the firing frequency (rate) of nerve impulses to already-active motor units. At low firing rates, the muscle partially relaxes between twitches (unfused tetanus). As firing rate increases, twitches summate and the muscle sustains higher force (tetanus). A motor unit firing at 50 Hz produces substantially more force than the same unit firing at 15 Hz. Both recruitment and rate coding work simultaneously to fine-tune force output across the full range of movement intensities.
⚠ Examiner tip: This question specifically asks for BOTH mechanisms — answering only recruitment will score a maximum of 2 marks. Use specific examples of low vs high intensity activities to contextualise your answer and demonstrate understanding.

📝 Extended Response Questions

Extended Response6 marks
A netball player performs a chest pass, followed by a lateral shuffle to defend an opponent, then lands from a jump and holds a defensive crouch position.
For THREE of the highlighted movements, identify the type of muscular contraction occurring at a named muscle. Justify your answer by referring to what happens to muscle length and force relative to load in each case.
✅ Model Answer

2 marks per movement (contraction type + muscle + justification):

✓Chest pass — push phase (Concentric): The pectoralis major and triceps brachii contract concentrically — both muscles shorten as they generate force to push the ball. The muscle force exceeds the load (ball mass + air resistance), accelerating the ball forward. The elbow extends and the shoulder horizontally adducts.
✓Landing from a jump (Eccentric): The quadriceps contract eccentrically upon landing — they lengthen under tension as they absorb the landing force, controlling the rate of knee flexion. The load (body weight + impact force) exceeds the muscle force, but the eccentric contraction prevents the knees from buckling. This is why landing drills cause significant DOMS.
✓Holding a defensive crouch (Isometric): The quadriceps contract isometrically while the player holds the semi-squat defensive position — the muscle generates force equal to the load (body weight) without changing length. No joint movement occurs at the knee. This sustained isometric contraction causes rapid fatigue due to restricted blood flow.
⚠ Examiner tip: Each answer needs three elements: (1) name the contraction type, (2) name the muscle, (3) justify by referring to muscle length change and force vs load relationship. Missing the justification is the most common reason students score 1 out of 2 per movement.
Extended Response7 marks
Using your knowledge of neural control of muscles, trace the complete pathway from a voluntary decision to move, through to muscular contraction. In your answer, explain the role of the neuromuscular junction, acetylcholine and calcium ions, and describe how the nervous system grades force output to produce movements of varying intensity.
✅ Model Answer

Award marks across four areas:

✓Neural pathway (2 marks): A voluntary movement begins when the motor cortex of the brain initiates a signal. This travels down the spinal cord, branches into motor nerves, and passes into individual motor neurons. The myelinated axon conducts the electrical action potential rapidly (via saltatory conduction at nodes of Ranvier) toward the target muscle.
✓Role of NMJ and ACh (2 marks): At the neuromuscular junction (NMJ), the action potential reaches the axon terminal and triggers the release of acetylcholine (ACh) from synaptic vesicles into the synaptic cleft. ACh diffuses across and binds to receptors on the motor end plate (sarcolemma), generating an action potential in the muscle fibre that spreads along the sarcolemma and down T-tubules into the interior of the fibre.
✓Role of calcium ions (1 mark): The action potential in the T-tubules triggers the sarcoplasmic reticulum to release Ca2+. Ca2+ binds to troponin, moving tropomyosin aside to expose myosin-binding sites on actin, allowing cross-bridge formation and the power stroke to occur — initiating the sliding filament mechanism.
✓Grading force output (2 marks): The nervous system grades force using two mechanisms. First, motor unit recruitment — more motor units are activated as intensity increases, following the size principle (Type I first, then IIa, then IIx). Second, rate coding — increasing the frequency of nerve impulses to active motor units increases their force output through summation and tetanus. Together these mechanisms allow fine control from delicate tasks (threading a needle) to explosive movements (shot put).
⚠ Examiner tip: This is a 7-mark extended response — examiners expect depth and anatomical detail throughout. Short answers citing only "the brain sends a signal to the muscle" score very poorly. Use specific structural names at every step: motor cortex, spinal cord, motor neuron, axon, myelin sheath, NMJ, axon terminal, ACh, synaptic cleft, motor end plate, sarcolemma, T-tubules, SR, Ca2+, troponin, tropomyosin.
