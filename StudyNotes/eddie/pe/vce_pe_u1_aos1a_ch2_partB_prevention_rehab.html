🩹 Minimum Fitness Requirements

► Fitness Components & Injury Prevention Overview ▼
📚 Minimum Fitness Requirements for Injury Prevention HOT TOPICLinks fitness to injury prevention — key exam concept ▼

A minimum level of physical fitness is required to safely participate in sport and physical activity. Athletes who do not meet the fitness demands of their sport are at significantly higher risk of musculoskeletal injury, particularly as fatigue sets in during activity.

► Fitness Components and Their Role in Injury Prevention ▼
Fitness ComponentHow it Prevents InjuryExample
Muscular strengthStrong muscles support and stabilise joints, absorb impact forces and reduce load on ligaments and tendonsStrong quadriceps reduce stress on the ACL during landing
Muscular enduranceDelays fatigue — fatigued muscles lose coordination and protective function, increasing injury riskHamstring endurance prevents late-game strains in AFL
FlexibilityAdequate range of motion allows joints to move through full range without straining muscles or tendonsHip flexor flexibility prevents lower back and hamstring strains
Cardiovascular fitnessHigher aerobic fitness delays fatigue, maintaining concentration, coordination and movement mechanics late in activityHigher VO2max delays the breakdown in running form that causes ankle sprains
Body compositionExcess body fat increases compressive load on weight-bearing joints (knee, hip, ankle), accelerating wear and raising sprain riskMaintaining healthy BMI reduces knee injury risk in netball
Neuromuscular control / proprioceptionJoint position sense and balance allow rapid protective muscle responses to unexpected forces — critical for re-injury preventionBalance board training reduces ankle re-sprain rate
💡 Fatigue and injury risk: As fatigue accumulates, muscles lose their ability to generate force rapidly and maintain coordination. This means joints become less protected, movement mechanics deteriorate, and the risk of acute injury rises sharply in the final stages of training or competition. Meeting minimum fitness requirements delays this point.
⚠ Exam tip: When asked how fitness prevents injury, always link the specific component to a specific mechanism — e.g. “muscular strength supports joints by absorbing load, reducing stress on ligaments.” Generic answers like “being fit reduces injury” score zero.

🩹 Warm-Up

► Warm-Up Structure Diagram ▼
🩹 Warm-Up: Physiological Effects and Injury Prevention HOT TOPICPhysiological effects of warm-up are a common exam question ▼

A warm-up is a structured period of graduated physical activity performed prior to the main training session or competition. Its purpose is to prepare the body physiologically and psychologically for the demands ahead, and to reduce injury risk.

► Physiological Effects of a Warm-Up ▼
EffectMechanismInjury Prevention Benefit
Increased muscle temperatureIncreased metabolic activity generates heat within working musclesWarmer muscles are more extensible (elastic) and less prone to tearing — reduces strain risk
Increased blood flow to musclesVasodilation of arterioles supplying working muscles; redistribution of cardiac outputGreater oxygen and nutrient delivery; metabolic waste removal; muscles function more efficiently
Increased nerve conduction velocityWarmer temperature speeds electrical signal transmission along motor and sensory nervesFaster muscle activation and reaction time — reduces risk of being caught off-guard by sudden forces
Increased joint range of motionSynovial fluid becomes less viscous with warmth; connective tissue becomes more pliableJoints move more freely through full ROM, reducing strain and sprain risk at extremes of movement
Increased production of synovial fluidJoint movement stimulates synovial membrane to produce lubricating fluidBetter joint lubrication reduces friction and cartilage wear
Psychological preparationFocus, arousal regulation and mental rehearsal during warm-upReduces risk of errors from inattention; improves anticipation of demands
► Dynamic vs Static Stretching in Warm-Up ▼
FeatureDynamic StretchingStatic Stretching
DescriptionControlled, active movements through full ROM (e.g. leg swings, arm circles)Holding a stretch at end range for 20–60 seconds
Best usedDuring warm-up — before activityDuring cool-down — after activity
Effect on performanceMaintains or improves power and speedTemporarily reduces force production if done before activity
Effect on injury riskReduces injury risk when used in warm-upPre-activity static stretching does NOT reduce injury risk and may impair performance
Neural effectActivates neuromuscular system — improves reaction timeInhibitory effect on motor neuron excitability if done before activity
💡 Common misconception: Many athletes still perform static stretching before activity believing it prevents injury. Research shows static stretching before activity does NOT reduce acute injury risk and may impair power output. Dynamic stretching is the evidence-based choice for warm-up.
► The FIFA 11+ Program — Evidence-Based Warm-Up ▼

The FIFA 11+ is a structured warm-up program developed to prevent injuries in football (soccer) players. It is an example of a research-backed, sport-specific warm-up protocol that has been shown to reduce lower limb injury rates by up to 30–50%.

PartContentDuration
Part 1Running exercises at slow speed with active stretching and controlled partner contact~8 min
Part 2Six strength, plyometric and balance exercises (varying difficulty levels)~10 min
Part 3Running exercises at moderate/high speed with cutting and bounding movements~2 min

The FIFA 11+ is widely used as a model for sport-specific warm-up programs across many sports beyond football.

🩹 Cool-Down

🩹 Cool-Down: Purpose, Structure and Benefits HOT TOPICPhysiological effects of cool-down frequently examined ▼

A cool-down is a period of low-intensity activity and stretching performed immediately after the main session or competition. It facilitates the body’s transition from exercise back to a resting state and supports recovery.

► Physiological Effects of a Cool-Down ▼
EffectMechanismBenefit
Gradual reduction in heart rate and blood pressureContinued low-intensity movement maintains venous return — prevents blood pooling in lower limbsReduces risk of post-exercise hypotension (dizziness/fainting)
Accelerated removal of metabolic wasteContinued circulation flushes lactate and other metabolic by-products from musclesReduces muscle soreness and stiffness; speeds recovery
Reduction in muscle temperatureGradual decrease in metabolic rate and heat productionControlled cooling prevents sudden temperature drops that can cause cramping
Restoration of muscle lengthStatic stretching of warm muscles improves flexibility gains and restores resting lengthReduces post-exercise muscle tightness; improves long-term flexibility; reduces DOMS
Psychological recoveryTime to decompress, reflect and transition mentally from competitionReduced psychological stress; better sleep quality
► Cool-Down Structure ▼
1
Low-intensity aerobic activity (5–10 min)
Light jogging transitioning to brisk walking. Maintains circulation and venous return while gradually reducing heart rate and blood pressure toward resting levels.
2
Static stretching of major muscle groups (5–10 min)
Hold each stretch 20–60 seconds. Most effective when muscles are still warm from exercise — this is the optimal time for flexibility development. Target all major muscle groups used during activity.
3
Optional: foam rolling / soft tissue therapy
Self-myofascial release helps reduce muscle tension and trigger points. Supports recovery and reduces next-day soreness when combined with stretching.
► Warm-Up vs Cool-Down: Key Comparison ▼
FeatureWarm-UpCool-Down
TimingBefore activityAfter activity
IntensityGradually increases toward activity levelGradually decreases from activity level to rest
Stretching typeDynamic stretchingStatic stretching
Primary purposePrepare body for exercise; reduce acute injury riskFacilitate recovery; restore resting state; improve flexibility
Heart rate effectRaises HR progressivelyLowers HR gradually — prevents venous pooling
Muscle temperatureIncreases — improves extensibilityDecreases gradually — controlled cooling
⚠ DOMS and cool-down: Delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) peaks 24–48 hours after exercise involving eccentric contractions. A cool-down helps reduce DOMS by maintaining circulation and reducing metabolic waste accumulation. However, it does not eliminate DOMS entirely — adequate recovery time and nutrition are also required.

🩹 Rehabilitation

► Rehabilitation Phases Diagram ▼
🩹 Rehabilitation Principles and Phases HOT TOPICRICER, HARM and rehabilitation phases are frequently examined ▼

Rehabilitation is the structured process of restoring an injured athlete to full function and safe return to sport. Effective rehabilitation follows a progressive, criteria-based approach moving through distinct phases.

► RICER Protocol — Immediate Injury Management ▼

RICER is applied immediately after acute soft tissue injury to minimise bleeding, swelling and pain in the first 48–72 hours.

LetterActionPurpose
R — RestStop activity immediately; avoid weight-bearing if lower limbPrevents further tissue damage; protects the injury
I — IceApply ice pack (wrapped in cloth) for 20 min every 2 hoursVasoconstriction reduces bleeding and swelling; numbs pain
C — CompressionApply firm bandage over the injury siteLimits swelling by reducing fluid accumulation in tissue
E — ElevationRaise the injured limb above heart levelUses gravity to reduce fluid accumulation; decreases swelling
R — ReferralSeek medical assessment from doctor, physiotherapist or sports physicianEnsures accurate diagnosis; rules out fracture or serious injury
► HARM — What to AVOID After Acute Injury ▼

HARM identifies actions that increase bleeding, swelling and tissue damage and must be avoided in the first 48–72 hours after acute soft tissue injury.

LetterAction to AvoidWhy it is Harmful
H — HeatHot packs, heat rubs, hot baths/showersVasodilation increases blood flow to the injury, worsening bleeding and swelling
A — AlcoholDrinking alcoholVasodilation increases swelling; impairs healing and coordination
R — RunningAny vigorous exercise or weight-bearing activityIncreases blood flow and mechanical stress on damaged tissue
M — MassageMassage of the injury siteMechanical pressure disrupts clot formation and increases bleeding and bruising
💡 RICER and HARM work together: Apply RICER (what TO do) and avoid HARM (what NOT to do) simultaneously in the first 48–72 hours. Both address the same goal — minimising swelling and protecting the injured tissue.
► Four Phases of Rehabilitation ▼
PhaseGoalsKey ActivitiesCriteria to Progress
Phase 1: Acute / Protection
Days 1–3		 Control inflammation, protect injury, manage pain RICER, rest, pain-free gentle movement, electrotherapy (TENS, ultrasound) Swelling reducing; pain at rest minimal; able to perform gentle active movement
Phase 2: Sub-Acute / Repair
Days 3–21		 Restore pain-free ROM, begin light loading, maintain fitness Gentle ROM exercises, hydrotherapy, pain-free strengthening, pool running Full pain-free ROM; no swelling with activity; normal gait restored
Phase 3: Remodelling / Strength
Weeks 3–12+		 Rebuild strength, power and neuromuscular control to pre-injury levels Progressive resistance training, proprioception/balance exercises, sport-specific movement patterns Symmetrical strength (≥90% of uninjured side); normal movement patterns; no pain with loading
Phase 4: Return to Sport
Sport clearance required		 Restore full sport-specific function and confidence; re-integrate into team training Sport-specific drills at full speed, contact practice, match simulation, full training Passes return-to-sport criteria (strength, agility, psychological readiness); medical clearance
► TOTAPS — On-Field Assessment ▼

TOTAPS is used by coaches, trainers and first aiders to assess an injured athlete on the field and determine whether they can continue playing.

LetterStepPurpose
T — TalkAsk the athlete what happened and where it hurtsGather history; identify mechanism of injury
O — ObserveLook for deformity, swelling, bruising, abnormal positionVisual assessment of severity
T — TouchGently palpate the injury site for point tendernessLocalise injury; assess severity by pain response
A — Active movementAsk athlete to move the injured part through ROM themselvesAssess pain-free range of motion
P — Passive movementAssessor moves the injured part through ROMAssess ROM without muscle activation; detects ligament/joint issues
S — Skills testAthlete performs sport-specific movements (walking, jogging, sprinting, change of direction)Determines functional readiness to return to play
⚠ If in doubt, sit them out: If any step of TOTAPS produces pain, the athlete should not return to play. An athlete must pass ALL steps before being cleared to continue.
► Principles of Rehabilitation Training ▼
PrincipleApplication in Rehabilitation
Progressive overloadLoad is gradually and systematically increased as tissue heals — too much too soon causes re-injury
SpecificityLate-stage rehab must replicate the exact movements, forces and intensities of the athlete’s sport
ReversibilityFitness declines during injury — rehab must address strength, endurance and skill losses, not just the injury site
IndividualityRehabilitation rate varies between athletes based on age, nutrition, sleep, injury severity and adherence
Pain-free progressionPain is a signal to reduce load — rehabilitation should be challenging but never significantly painful
