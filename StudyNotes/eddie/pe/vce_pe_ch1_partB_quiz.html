📝 Short Answer Questions

Short Answer2 marks
Distinguish between the roles of the agonist and antagonist muscles during movement. Use an example to support your answer.
✅ Model Answer
✓The agonist (prime mover) is the muscle primarily responsible for producing the movement, typically contracting concentrically. For example, during knee extension in a drop punt kick, the quadriceps are the agonist. (1 mark)
✓The antagonist opposes the agonist and must relax or eccentrically contract to allow smooth, controlled movement. In the same example, the hamstrings are the antagonist — they eccentrically control the speed of knee extension. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Always name BOTH muscles in the pair and specify the joint and movement. Writing only "the agonist contracts and the antagonist relaxes" is too vague — you must name the muscles for full marks.
Short Answer3 marks
Describe the three types of muscle tissue. For each, state whether it is voluntary or involuntary and striated or non-striated.
✅ Model Answer
✓Skeletal muscle — voluntary, striated. Found attached to bones. Responsible for all movement, posture and heat production. (1 mark)
✓Cardiac muscle — involuntary, striated. Found only in the heart. Contracts rhythmically and continuously without fatigue. (1 mark)
✓Smooth muscle — involuntary, non-striated. Found in walls of blood vessels and hollow organs. Controls internal functions such as vasodilation during exercise. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Smooth muscle is the only non-striated type — this is commonly tested. Each answer must include voluntary/involuntary AND striated/non-striated to score the mark.
Short Answer4 marks
Outline the five steps of the sliding filament theory of muscle contraction.
✅ Model Answer

4 marks — award 1 mark per two correct steps (or 1 per step if only 4 steps asked):

✓Step 1: A nerve impulse travels down the motor neuron and arrives at the neuromuscular junction (NMJ).
✓Step 2: Acetylcholine (ACh) is released into the synaptic cleft, triggering an action potential that spreads along the muscle fibre membrane (sarcolemma) and into the T-tubules.
✓Step 3: Calcium ions (Ca2+) are released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Ca2+ binds to troponin, moving tropomyosin aside to expose the myosin-binding sites on actin.
✓Step 4: Myosin heads bind to actin, forming cross-bridges. The power stroke occurs — myosin pivots and pulls actin toward the centre of the sarcomere, shortening it.
✓Step 5: ATP binds to the myosin head, breaking the cross-bridge and re-cocking the myosin head. Ca2+ is pumped back to the SR and the muscle relaxes — unless another nerve impulse arrives.
⚠ Examiner tip: The most commonly dropped mark is Step 5 — students forget that ATP is needed to BREAK the cross-bridge, not just form it. Also note that Ca2+ must be returned to the SR for relaxation to occur.
Short Answer4 marks
Compare Type I and Type IIx muscle fibres across FOUR characteristics. For each characteristic, explain how the difference affects athletic performance.
✅ Model Answer

1 mark per characteristic with performance link (any four):

✓Fatigue resistance: Type I = high; Type IIx = low. Type I fibres allow prolonged sustained effort (e.g. marathon), while Type IIx exhaust rapidly, limiting their use to short explosive bursts.
✓Force production: Type I = low; Type IIx = very high. Type IIx generate the powerful contractions needed for sprinting and jumping, while Type I cannot produce the force required for explosive movements.
✓Energy system: Type I = aerobic; Type IIx = anaerobic. Type I are suited to oxygen-dependent activities; Type IIx rely on glycolysis and phosphocreatine, making them ideal for high-intensity short-duration efforts.
✓Mitochondria density: Type I = many; Type IIx = few. High mitochondria in Type I support aerobic ATP production, enabling endurance performance. Low mitochondria in Type IIx limits their aerobic capacity.
✓Myoglobin / colour: Type I = high myoglobin (red); Type IIx = low myoglobin (pale). High myoglobin improves oxygen storage and delivery within the fibre, supporting the aerobic demands of endurance sport.
⚠ Examiner tip: Listing characteristics without linking to performance will not score full marks. The key phrase is "which means..." or "therefore..." — always explain WHY the difference matters for an athlete.

📝 Extended Response Questions

Extended Response6 marks
During a 100m sprint, an athlete accelerates from the blocks, reaches maximum speed at 60m, then maintains speed to the finish line.
Using your knowledge of muscle fibre types and the size principle, explain how the muscular system responds to produce and sustain maximum sprint performance. In your answer, refer to at least TWO fibre types and the concept of motor unit recruitment.
✅ Model Answer

Award marks across four areas:

✓Size principle and recruitment order (2 marks): As the athlete leaves the blocks, motor units are recruited from smallest to largest according to the size principle. Type I (slow twitch) motor units are activated first, followed by Type IIa and then Type IIx as the demand for force and speed increases during acceleration. By maximum speed, near-maximal recruitment of all fibre types has occurred.
✓Role of Type IIx fibres (2 marks): Type IIx (fast glycolytic) fibres are critical to sprint performance because they produce very high force and contract extremely rapidly. They rely on anaerobic metabolism (phosphocreatine and glycolysis) to generate ATP quickly. However, their low fatigue resistance means their contribution begins to diminish as the race progresses and metabolic by-products accumulate.
✓Role of Type IIa fibres (1 mark): Type IIa fibres contribute significantly during the maintenance phase (60-100m), as they can use both aerobic and anaerobic pathways, offering a combination of speed and moderate endurance to sustain near-maximal effort.
✓Fatigue considerations (1 mark): The rapid depletion of phosphocreatine and accumulation of metabolic by-products from anaerobic glycolysis in Type IIx fibres contributes to the velocity decline that many sprinters experience in the final 20-30m of a 100m race.
⚠ Examiner tip: Extended responses must apply knowledge to the specific context — in this case the 100m sprint. Generic statements about fibre types without linking to acceleration, maximum speed, or the phases of the sprint will score poorly. Always use the stimulus to frame your answer.
Extended Response6 marks
Using a physical activity or sport of your choice, analyse the muscular system during ONE specific movement. In your answer, identify the joint, joint type, movement occurring, agonist, antagonist, type of contraction for the agonist, and the muscle fibre types most likely recruited.
✅ Model Answer — Example: Kicking a football (drop punt)
✓Joint and type (1 mark): The knee joint — a hinge joint formed by the femur and tibia.
✓Movement (1 mark): Knee extension — the angle at the knee joint increases as the lower leg swings forward to contact the ball.
✓Agonist and antagonist (1 mark): The quadriceps femoris is the agonist (prime mover) for knee extension. The hamstrings are the antagonist, eccentrically contracting to control the speed of extension and protect the joint.
✓Type of contraction (1 mark): The quadriceps perform a concentric contraction — they shorten as they generate force to accelerate the lower leg forward.
✓Fibre types recruited (2 marks): A powerful, explosive kick requires rapid force production, so Type IIx (fast glycolytic) fibres are predominantly recruited. Type IIa fibres also contribute. According to the size principle, Type I fibres are recruited first but are insufficient alone for the high force demands of kicking — Type II units are essential for generating the speed and power required.
⚠ Examiner tip: This style of question has a checklist of required elements — joint, joint type, movement, agonist, antagonist, contraction type, fibre types. Missing any element costs marks. Practice applying this template to multiple movements: throwing, swimming freestyle, jumping, rowing.
