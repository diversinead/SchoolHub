1. Trace the path of a red blood cell from the right ventricle back to the right ventricle, naming key structures passed.
✅ Model Answer
✓Right ventricle → pulmonary artery → lung capillaries (CO2 out / O2 in) (1 mark)
✓Pulmonary veins → left atrium → left ventricle → aorta (1 mark)
✓Systemic capillaries (O2 delivered / CO2 collected) → veins → vena cava → right atrium → right ventricle (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Name the capillaries and link them to gas exchange — just listing arteries and veins loses marks.

✓Answer: 14.7 L/min (1 mark)

Short Answer4 marks
A sprinter's HR rises from 60 to 110 bpm on the starting blocks before the gun fires.
3. Name this response and explain TWO mechanisms responsible for it.
✅ Model Answer
✓This is the anticipatory rise — HR increases before exercise begins (1 mark)
✓Mechanism 1: Adrenaline released from the adrenal medulla stimulates the SA node to increase its firing rate (1 mark)
✓Mechanism 2: Higher brain centres (motor cortex/limbic system) activate the sympathetic nervous system, increasing drive to the SA node (1 mark)
✓This is a feed-forward response — muscles have not moved, so proprioceptors and chemoreceptors are not yet active (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Do not attribute the anticipatory rise to proprioceptors or chemoreceptors — these require actual exercise. It is driven by the brain and adrenaline alone.
Extended Response6 marks
Athlete A: elite cyclist, resting HR 44 bpm, SV 115 mL. Athlete B: untrained, resting HR 78 bpm, SV 65 mL.
4. Compare cardiac output at rest and maximal exercise. Explain what adaptations account for the SV difference, referencing the Frank-Starling Law.
✅ Model Answer
✓Rest: A = 44 × 115 = 5,060 mL/min; B = 78 × 65 = 5,070 mL/min — similar Q, achieved differently (1 mark)
✓Athlete A achieves the same Q with a much lower HR, compensated by a higher SV — reflecting cardiac efficiency from training (1 mark)
✓At max exercise, A has greater Q (~32 L/min) vs B (~18 L/min), primarily due to larger maximal SV from cardiac hypertrophy (1 mark)
✓Frank-Starling: training causes eccentric cardiac hypertrophy (larger chamber). Greater chamber volume allows larger EDV, more ventricular wall stretch, stronger contraction, and higher SV (1 mark)
✓During exercise, Athlete A also has greater venous return (higher plasma volume from training), further increasing preload and amplifying the Frank-Starling effect (1 mark)
✓Above ~50% VO2max, SV plateaus in both athletes — further Q increases come from HR only, but A starts from a higher SV baseline (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Always show the numbers to prove resting Q is similar. Apply Frank-Starling as a chain: training → larger chamber → greater EDV → more stretch → stronger contraction → higher SV.
Extended Response6 marks
5. Compare arteries, capillaries and veins (structure and function). Describe THREE mechanisms that support venous return during exercise.
✅ Model Answer
✓Arteries: away from heart, high pressure. Thick elastic/muscular walls absorb pulsatile pressure. Small lumen relative to wall. (1 mark)
✓Capillaries: exchange vessels only. One-cell-thick walls minimise diffusion distance for O2, CO2, nutrients and waste. RBCs pass single file. (1 mark)
✓Veins: toward heart, low pressure. Thin walls, large lumen. One-way valves prevent backflow, especially against gravity in limbs. (1 mark)
✓Venous return 1 — Skeletal muscle pump: contracting muscles squeeze veins; valves prevent backflow. (1 mark)
✓Venous return 2 — Respiratory pump: deep inhalation creates negative thoracic pressure, drawing blood into the right atrium. (1 mark)
✓Venous return 3 — Venoconstriction: sympathetic NS reduces venous capacity, pushing blood centrally and increasing preload → Frank-Starling → higher SV. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Always explain WHY capillary walls are thin (diffusion distance). Name all three venous return mechanisms — the muscle pump alone is not sufficient for full marks.
