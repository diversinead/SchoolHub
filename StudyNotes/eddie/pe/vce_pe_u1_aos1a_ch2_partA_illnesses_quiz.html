Short Answer 2 marks
Distinguish between a sprain and a strain. In your answer, name the tissue affected in each injury.
  • ✓ A sprain is a stretching or tearing of a ligament (bone to bone connective tissue that stabilises joints).
  • ✓ A strain is a stretching or tearing of a muscle or tendon (tendon connects muscle to bone and transmits force).
⚠ Examiner tip: The most common error is students using “sprain” and “strain” interchangeably or associating sprains with muscles. You MUST name the specific tissue for each mark. “Connective tissue” alone is not specific enough.
Short Answer 3 marks
Describe the three grades of a sprain and explain what differentiates them from one another.
  • ✓ Grade I (mild) — microscopic tearing of ligament fibres with no structural disruption; minimal swelling/pain, no joint instability, fibres remain intact.
  • ✓ Grade II (moderate) — partial tearing of ligament fibres; moderate swelling, bruising, and some degree of joint instability due to partial structural failure.
  • ✓ Grade III (severe) — complete rupture of the ligament; significant joint instability, marked swelling and bruising, loss of function. May require surgical repair.
⚠ Examiner tip: Students often describe the symptoms (pain/swelling) but forget to explicitly state the DEGREE OF TISSUE DAMAGE (intact, partial tear, complete tear) — this is what actually differentiates the grades. State the structural change, not just the symptoms.
Short Answer 3 marks
Identify THREE differences between an intramuscular haematoma and an intermuscular haematoma.
  • ✓ Location of blood: Intramuscular — blood stays contained within the muscle sheath / compartment. Intermuscular — blood tracks between muscle compartments along fascial planes.
  • ✓ Visible bruising: Intramuscular — bruising may not appear immediately because blood is confined. Intermuscular — bruising appears relatively quickly as blood tracks distally to the skin surface.
  • ✓ Swelling and pressure: Intramuscular — more significant swelling and increased compartment pressure (more painful). Intermuscular — blood disperses, so swelling within the muscle is less pronounced.
⚠ Examiner tip: Students often know one type but confuse which has more pain/swelling. Remember: intra = inside = trapped = more pressure/swelling. Inter = between = disperses = bruising appears faster but less localised pressure.
Short Answer — Stimulus 4 marks
A 17-year-old cross country runner dramatically increased her weekly training distance from 40 km to 80 km over four weeks to prepare for state championships. She now reports a dull, aching pain in her right tibia that worsens during running and eases with rest. An X-ray shows a hairline crack.
Using the information above, identify the most likely injury and explain TWO specific risk factors that contributed to this injury.
  • ✓ Injury identified: Tibial stress fracture — a chronic/overuse injury characterised by a hairline crack caused by repetitive loading exceeding the bone’s repair capacity.
  • ✓ Risk factor 1 — Rapid increase in training load: The athlete doubled her weekly distance in four weeks (“too much too soon”). Bone remodelling cannot keep pace with such rapid increases, leading to cumulative microtrauma and stress fracture.
  • ✓ Risk factor 2 (accept any one of): Age/growth — adolescent bone may not yet be fully mineralised; Female sex — higher risk due to potential hormonal factors and female athlete triad; Inadequate recovery — rest days are when bone remodelling and repair occur; Hard running surface — extrinsic factor increasing ground reaction forces.
  • ✓ Link to stimulus: Specific reference to doubling of training distance from 40 to 80 km as the mechanism of overload, with the pain pattern (worsens with running, eases with rest) consistent with bone stress.
⚠ Examiner tip: Always link your answer back to the stimulus. Students who give a generic stress fracture answer without referring to “the 40–80 km increase in four weeks” miss marks. The stimulus is there to be used.
Extended Response 6 marks
Compare and contrast soft tissue injuries and hard tissue injuries in sport. In your response, define each category, provide TWO examples of each, and explain ONE way they differ in terms of mechanism and management.
  • ✓ Soft tissue injuries defined: Injuries that affect muscles, tendons, ligaments, bursae or cartilage — structures other than bone. They do not involve a break in bony tissue.
  • ✓ Two examples of soft tissue injuries: e.g. Grade II lateral ankle sprain (ligament) and Grade II hamstring strain (muscle). [Accept any two correct examples.]
  • ✓ Hard tissue injuries defined: Injuries that involve bone — including fractures (complete or incomplete breaks) and dislocations (joint surface separation).
  • ✓ Two examples of hard tissue injuries: e.g. Transverse fracture of the tibia and anterior shoulder dislocation. [Accept any two correct examples.]
  • ✓ Mechanism difference: Soft tissue injuries often occur through indirect mechanisms such as excessive stretch or repetitive friction (e.g. ligament sprain from ankle inversion), whereas hard tissue injuries typically require greater direct force or significant joint loading (e.g. direct blow or fall causing a fracture). Stress fractures are an exception — these are hard tissue overuse injuries.
  • ✓ Management difference: Both categories require initial RICER management (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation, Referral) for acute injuries. However, hard tissue injuries such as fractures require immobilisation (splinting, casting or surgical fixation) and a longer recovery period due to the time required for bone remodelling and callus formation, whereas many soft tissue injuries can be managed conservatively with progressive loading rehabilitation.
⚠ Examiner tip: “Compare and contrast” requires you to identify BOTH similarities AND differences. Many students only contrast. Six-mark responses need at least 5–6 discrete, accurate points with appropriate terminology. Do not repeat the same point using different words.
Extended Response 7 marks
An elite netball player lands awkwardly from a jump during a goal attempt. She hears a “pop” and immediately falls to the ground with severe pain in her right knee. She cannot bear weight and the knee appears swollen within minutes. The team physiotherapist suspects an anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) tear.
Using the scenario above, classify the injury, explain the mechanism, describe the signs and symptoms, and identify the tissue structures most likely involved. Evaluate the severity of the injury and outline the likely impact on the athlete’s training and competition.
  • ✓ Classification: Acute soft tissue injury — specifically a Grade III sprain (complete rupture) of the anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) of the right knee.
  • ✓ Mechanism: The ACL is typically injured during sudden deceleration, pivoting or awkward landing from a jump, as described in the scenario. The combined forces of knee valgus and internal tibial rotation stress the ACL beyond its tensile strength, causing complete rupture.
  • ✓ Signs and symptoms: The “pop” heard at time of injury is characteristic of a ligament rupture. Immediate, rapid swelling (haemarthrosis — blood into the joint), severe pain, inability to bear weight, and significant joint instability (knee giving way) are consistent with a Grade III ACL sprain.
  • ✓ Structures involved: The ACL (primary ligament stabilising against anterior tibial translation and rotational instability) is completely ruptured. The menisci and MCL are frequently co-injured in ACL tears (“unhappy triad”).
  • ✓ Severity evaluation: Grade III is the most severe grade of sprain — complete rupture results in complete structural failure of the ligament. Ligaments have limited vascularity and healing capacity. Surgical reconstruction (ACL graft) is typically required for athletes who wish to return to sport at a competitive level.
  • ✓ Impact on training/competition: An ACL rupture typically requires 9–12 months of rehabilitation before return to competition. The athlete will be unable to participate in netball for approximately one season. Rehabilitation involves staged progressions: post-surgical healing, strength restoration, proprioception and neuromuscular retraining, sport-specific drills, and return-to-competition criteria.
  • ✓ Linkage to stimulus: Specific reference to landing from a jump, the audible “pop”, rapid swelling and inability to bear weight as hallmarks consistent with ACL rupture in the scenario.
⚠ Examiner tip: A 7-mark extended response must cover ALL components asked in the question — classification, mechanism, signs/symptoms, structures, severity AND impact. Students who write at length about only one component (e.g. just the mechanism) will lose marks for omitting others. Underline or tick off each component of the question as you answer it.
