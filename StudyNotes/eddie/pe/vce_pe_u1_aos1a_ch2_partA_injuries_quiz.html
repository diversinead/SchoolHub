Short Answer2 marks
Distinguish between osteoarthritis and rheumatoid arthritis. In your answer, identify the primary cause of each condition.
  • ✓ Osteoarthritis is a degenerative joint disease caused by the progressive breakdown of articular cartilage due to age, mechanical wear and previous joint injury (“wear and tear”).
  • ✓ Rheumatoid arthritis is a chronic autoimmune disease in which the body’s immune system mistakenly attacks the synovial membrane lining the joints, causing inflammation and progressive joint destruction.
⚠ Examiner tip: Always use the words “degenerative” or “wear and tear” for OA and “autoimmune” for RA. Saying OA is “caused by inflammation” or RA is “caused by overuse” are both zero-mark answers.
Short Answer3 marks
Explain the role of physical activity in the prevention of osteoporosis. In your answer, refer to Wolff’s Law and identify the MOST effective type of exercise for bone health.
  • ✓ Wolff’s Law states that bone adapts to the mechanical loads placed upon it — when bone is stressed through physical activity, osteoblasts (bone-forming cells) are stimulated to deposit new bone tissue, increasing bone mineral density.
  • ✓ Weight-bearing impact activities (e.g. running, jumping, basketball, netball) are most effective because the ground reaction forces create high mechanical stress through bone, maximally stimulating bone formation. Resistance training also stimulates bone via muscle tension at attachment sites.
  • ✓ Non-weight-bearing activities such as swimming provide minimal bone-loading benefit because the water supports body weight, reducing the mechanical stimulus to bone. Physical activity is particularly important during childhood and adolescence to maximise peak bone mass (reached ~25–30 years), providing a greater “bone bank” for later life.
⚠ Examiner tip: Simply writing “exercise increases bone density” scores zero. You must name Wolff’s Law, explain the mechanism (osteoblast stimulation via mechanical loading), and identify that weight-bearing impact is superior to non-weight-bearing exercise.
Short Answer — Stimulus4 marks
Marcus, a 16-year-old elite cricket fast bowler, presents with central lower back pain that worsens when he arches his back (hyperextension) and is relieved by rest. The pain has developed gradually over the past three months of intensive pre-season bowling. Imaging reveals a fracture at the pars interarticularis of L5.
Identify the injury, explain the mechanism, and outline TWO specific management strategies appropriate for Marcus.
  • ✓ Injury identified: Spondylolysis — a stress fracture of the pars interarticularis at L5, the most common level affected.
  • ✓ Mechanism: Repetitive hyperextension combined with rotation during the fast bowling action places high cyclic stress on the pars interarticularis. Over the pre-season period this cumulative microtrauma has exceeded the bone’s repair capacity, producing a stress fracture. The gradual onset and link to bowling confirm the overuse mechanism.
  • ✓ Management 1 — Rest from aggravating activity: Marcus should cease fast bowling and all hyperextension activities immediately. The pars fracture requires offloading from the repetitive stress that caused it. A rigid brace (Boston brace) may be used to restrict lumbar extension and facilitate healing.
  • ✓ Management 2 — Core and rehabilitation program: Once pain-free, Marcus should undertake a structured physiotherapy program focusing on core stabilisation exercises (deep abdominal and lumbar multifidus strengthening) to support the lumbar spine and reduce stress on the pars during return to bowling. Return to fast bowling should be gradual with progressive load monitoring.
⚠ Examiner tip: Reference the stimulus directly — mention “fast bowling,” “hyperextension,” and “gradual onset over three months.” Generic answers not linked to the scenario lose marks. Also: spondylolysis is NOT the same as spondylolisthesis — do not confuse them.
Extended Response6 marks
Compare and contrast osteoarthritis (OA) and rheumatoid arthritis (RA) as musculoskeletal illnesses. In your response, discuss the cause, joints affected, signs and symptoms, and the role of physical activity in the management of each condition.
  • ✓ Cause — OA: Progressive degeneration of articular cartilage from mechanical wear, age and previous joint injury. Not caused by the immune system.
  • ✓ Cause — RA: Autoimmune disease in which the immune system attacks the synovial membrane. Has systemic effects throughout the body, unlike OA.
  • ✓ Joints affected: OA typically affects weight-bearing joints (knee, hip) and is asymmetrical. RA affects small joints symmetrically (MCP, PIP joints of hands, wrists) — the symmetrical pattern is a key distinguishing feature.
  • ✓ Signs and symptoms comparison: Both cause joint pain, stiffness and reduced ROM. However, RA also produces systemic symptoms (fatigue, fever, anaemia) and prolonged morning stiffness (>1 hour), while OA morning stiffness lasts <30 minutes and systemic features are absent.
  • ✓ Physical activity in OA management: Low-impact weight-bearing exercise (e.g. walking, cycling) maintains cartilage nutrition via synovial fluid, strengthens muscles around the joint to reduce load on cartilage, and maintains ROM. Complete rest worsens OA outcomes.
  • ✓ Physical activity in RA management: During flare-ups, rest is appropriate to reduce inflammation. In remission periods, low-impact exercise improves joint mobility, muscle strength and systemic wellbeing. Exercise does NOT worsen RA when appropriately dosed and must be balanced against disease activity.
⚠ Examiner tip: “Compare and contrast” means you must address BOTH conditions for EACH point. Do not write all about OA then all about RA as separate paragraphs — weave the comparison throughout. Six marks need six distinct accurate points with appropriate terminology.
Extended Response7 marks
Sandra is a 58-year-old post-menopausal woman who has been sedentary for most of her adult life. She has recently been diagnosed with osteoporosis following a wrist fracture sustained from a minor trip. Her doctor has recommended she begin an exercise program.
Using the information above, explain why Sandra is at high risk of osteoporosis, describe the changes that occur in bone with this condition, and justify an appropriate exercise program for her, applying Wolff’s Law in your response.
  • ✓ Risk factor 1 — Post-menopausal female: Oestrogen plays a key role in inhibiting osteoclast (bone resorption) activity. After menopause, oestrogen declines sharply, causing accelerated bone resorption that exceeds bone formation — the most significant risk factor for osteoporosis in women.
  • ✓ Risk factor 2 — Sedentary lifestyle: Without mechanical loading from physical activity, osteoblasts receive minimal stimulus to build new bone (Wolff’s Law). Long-term inactivity leads to progressive bone loss throughout adulthood, compounding the post-menopausal decline.
  • ✓ Risk factor 3 — Age: At 58, Sandra is beyond peak bone mass. The trabecular bone network has been thinning for nearly 30 years since peak. The fragility fracture from a “minor trip” confirms severely compromised bone strength.
  • ✓ Bone changes in osteoporosis: Trabecular (cancellous) bone loses its dense honeycomb structure — struts thin and break down, leaving large voids. Bone mineral density falls below the diagnostic threshold (T-score ≤-2.5). The result is porous, brittle bone that fractures under loads that healthy bone would easily tolerate.
  • ✓ Exercise program — Wolff’s Law application: Wolff’s Law states bone adapts to mechanical load by stimulating osteoblast activity. Sandra’s program should include weight-bearing impact activities (e.g. walking, low-impact aerobics) to load the hip, spine and leg bones, stimulating bone formation at these sites.
  • ✓ Resistance training component: Progressive resistance exercises (e.g. leg press, squats) generate muscle pull on bone at tendon attachment sites, also stimulating osteoblasts. This is particularly important for the hip and spine — common osteoporotic fracture sites.
  • ✓ Considerations for Sandra specifically: Given her current fracture risk, exercise must be introduced gradually and high-impact or high-fall-risk activities avoided initially. Balance training (e.g. Tai Chi) should be incorporated to reduce fall risk, since preventing falls is as important as building bone density for fracture prevention.
⚠ Examiner tip: This question requires you to USE the stimulus — explicitly reference post-menopausal status, sedentary history and the wrist fracture. A response that ignores the stimulus and writes generically about osteoporosis will miss marks. For Wolff’s Law: name it, explain the mechanism (osteoblast stimulation), and apply it to a SPECIFIC exercise recommendation.
