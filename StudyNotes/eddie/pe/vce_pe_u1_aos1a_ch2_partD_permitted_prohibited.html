🩹 WADA — World Anti-Doping Agency

► Permitted vs Prohibited: Overview Diagram ▼
📚 WADA: Role, Structure and the Prohibited List HOT TOPICWADA criteria and role are commonly examined ▼

The World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) is an independent international organisation established in 1999 to lead the global effort against doping in sport. It sets the rules, maintains the Prohibited List and coordinates testing across all sports and countries.

► WADA’s Key Functions ▼
FunctionDetail
Maintain the Prohibited ListUpdated annually; lists all banned substances and methods in and out of competition. Athletes are responsible for knowing what is on the list.
Set the World Anti-Doping CodeThe framework that harmonises anti-doping policies across all sports and countries; adopted by all Olympic sports
Coordinate testingWorks with national anti-doping agencies (ASADA in Australia) to conduct in-competition and out-of-competition testing
Therapeutic Use Exemptions (TUEs)Athletes with legitimate medical needs can apply for a TUE to use a prohibited substance therapeutically (e.g. asthma medication)
Research and educationFunds research into detection methods; provides athlete education on anti-doping
► WADA Prohibited List: Criteria for Banning a Substance ▼

A substance or method is placed on the Prohibited List if it meets two of the following three criteria:

CriterionExplanation
1. Performance enhancementThe substance has the potential to enhance or enhances sport performance
2. Health riskThe substance represents a real or potential health risk to the athlete
3. Spirit of sportUse of the substance violates the spirit of sport — the core values of fair play, honesty, health, excellence and respect
💡 Key point: Only TWO of the three criteria need to be met for a substance to be banned. A substance can be banned even if it does not definitively enhance performance, as long as it poses a health risk AND violates the spirit of sport.
► Strict Liability and Athlete Responsibility ▼

Strict liability is a fundamental principle of anti-doping. It means that athletes are responsible for any prohibited substance found in their body, regardless of how it got there. The principle applies even if the athlete:

  • Did not intentionally take the substance
  • Was unaware the supplement contained a prohibited ingredient
  • Was given the substance by a coach, doctor or trainer without their knowledge
⚠ Implication for athletes: Athletes must verify every substance they consume — food, supplements, medications — before ingesting it. Ignorance is not a defence under strict liability. This is why the phrase “an athlete is responsible for what goes into their body” is a central tenet of anti-doping.
⚠ Exam tip: WADA criteria questions often ask you to identify which TWO of three criteria a substance meets. Always address all three criteria and then state which two apply.

🩹 Permitted Practices — Training and Nutrition

🩹 Permitted: Training Methods to Enhance MS Performance PERMITTED ▼

Training is the primary, universally permitted method of enhancing musculoskeletal performance. The most relevant training types for the musculoskeletal system are resistance training, plyometric training and flexibility training.

► Resistance Training ▼

Resistance training (also called strength or weight training) involves exercising muscles against an external resistance to improve muscular strength, power and endurance.

AdaptationMechanismBenefit
Muscle hypertrophyRepeated mechanical stress triggers muscle protein synthesis (MPS), increasing myofibril size and number within muscle fibresGreater cross-sectional area → greater force production capacity
Neural adaptationsImproved motor unit recruitment, rate coding and inter-muscular coordination (early gains)Faster, more forceful muscle contractions without hypertrophy — important for power
Increased tendon stiffnessMechanical loading stimulates collagen synthesis in tendonsStiffer tendons transmit force more efficiently — improved power transfer and reduced tendon injury risk
Increased bone densityMechanical loading stimulates osteoblast activity (Wolff’s Law)Reduced stress fracture risk; protection against osteoporosis
Improved joint stabilityStronger muscles provide greater support and control of joint positionReduced ligament and cartilage injury risk
💡 Progressive overload in resistance training: To continue making gains, training load must be progressively increased. This can be achieved by increasing weight, repetitions, sets, frequency or decreasing rest periods.
► Plyometric Training ▼

Plyometric training involves rapid, explosive movements that use the stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) — a rapid eccentric loading followed immediately by a concentric contraction — to develop power, speed and reactive strength.

AdaptationMechanismBenefit
Improved SSC efficiencyTraining improves the storage and reuse of elastic energy in tendons and the speed of reflex muscle activationGreater power output with less metabolic cost — jumping, sprinting, throwing
Increased rate of force development (RFD)Neural adaptations improve the speed of motor unit recruitmentFaster, more explosive muscle contractions — critical for reactive sports
Improved neuromuscular coordinationRepeated exposure to rapid loading patterns improves motor control and landing mechanicsReduced ACL and ankle injury risk through better landing patterns

Examples: Box jumps, depth jumps, bounding, medicine ball throws, hurdle hops.

► Flexibility Training ▼

Flexibility training improves the range of motion available at joints through systematic stretching of muscles, tendons and connective tissue.

TypeDescriptionBest Use
Static stretchingHold at end-range 20–60 sec; passive or activeCool-down; long-term flexibility development
Dynamic stretchingActive movement through full ROMWarm-up; sport-specific preparation
PNF (proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation)Contract-relax technique; uses reflex relaxation to achieve greater ROMMaximum flexibility gains; physiotherapy
Ballistic stretchingBouncing at end range; uses momentumSport-specific preparation for high-velocity movements; injury risk if used incorrectly

Benefits: Reduced strain/sprain risk; improved movement efficiency; reduced DOMS; better posture and joint health.

🩹 Permitted: Nutritional Supplements — Protein and Creatine PERMITTED HOT TOPICBenefits and harms of creatine are frequently examined ▼

Nutritional supplements are products consumed in addition to a regular diet to improve performance, health or recovery. Two key supplements relevant to the musculoskeletal system are protein supplements and creatine. Both are currently permitted by WADA.

► Protein Supplements ▼

Protein is essential for muscle repair, growth and immune function. Protein supplements (whey, casein, plant-based) allow athletes to conveniently meet increased protein requirements during heavy training.

AspectDetail
MechanismProvides amino acids (building blocks) for muscle protein synthesis (MPS) after resistance training. Whey protein is rapidly digested — ideal post-exercise. Casein is slow-release — ideal before sleep.
BenefitsSupports muscle hypertrophy; accelerates recovery between sessions; maintains lean muscle mass during energy restriction
Recommended intake1.6–2.2 g/kg body weight per day for strength/power athletes; spread across 3–5 meals (20–40 g per serving)
Risks / harmsExcessive intake (>3 g/kg/day) may stress kidneys in those with pre-existing renal conditions; expensive; can displace whole food nutrition; quality/contamination concerns with unregulated products
WADA status✓ Permitted — not on the Prohibited List
► Creatine Supplementation ▼

Creatine is a naturally occurring compound found in meat and fish and synthesised in the liver, kidneys and pancreas. Supplementation with creatine monohydrate is the most extensively researched sports supplement, with strong evidence of efficacy for high-intensity, short-duration activities.

AspectDetail
MechanismIncreases phosphocreatine (PCr) stores in muscle. PCr is used to rapidly regenerate ATP during high-intensity effort (CP system). More PCr → sustained power output for longer before fatigue
Performance benefitsImproved maximal strength and power; increased work capacity in repeated sprint or lifting efforts; accelerated recovery between high-intensity bouts; supports greater training volume over time → greater long-term hypertrophy
Most effective forActivities lasting 1–30 seconds (sprinting, jumping, weightlifting, throws, team sport repeated sprints)
Less effective forAerobic endurance activities (>3 min) — the CP system is not the primary energy source
Loading protocolLoading: 20 g/day for 5–7 days; Maintenance: 3–5 g/day. Alternatively: 3–5 g/day for 28 days (slower saturation, same endpoint)
Side effects / harmsWeight gain from water retention (1–2 kg); gastrointestinal discomfort at high doses; theoretical concern re: kidney stress in those with pre-existing conditions; risk of contamination with banned substances in unregulated products
WADA status✓ Permitted — NOT on the WADA Prohibited List. Legal in all sports.
💡 Creatine exam trap: Students frequently confuse creatine (permitted) with anabolic steroids (prohibited). Creatine is a naturally occurring compound and a legal supplement. It does NOT mimic or contain testosterone and is NOT banned by WADA.

🩹 Prohibited Substances — Steroids and Hormones

► Anabolic Steroids vs Peptide Hormones: Comparison Diagram ▼
🩹 Anabolic Androgenic Steroids (AAS) PROHIBITED HOT TOPICBenefits AND harms must both be known for exam answers ▼

Anabolic androgenic steroids (AAS) are synthetic derivatives of the male hormone testosterone. They have both anabolic (tissue-building) and androgenic (masculinising) effects. They are prohibited by WADA in and out of competition.

► How AAS Work: Mechanism ▼
  1. AAS enter muscle cells and bind to androgen receptors in the cell nucleus
  2. This activates gene expression for muscle protein synthesis (MPS)
  3. Increased MPS leads to greater accrual of contractile proteins (actin and myosin)
  4. Muscle fibres increase in size (hypertrophy) and number (hyperplasia in some studies)
  5. AAS also reduce recovery time by decreasing muscle damage and accelerating repair
  6. Androgenic effects include increased aggression (“roid rage”) and masculinisation
► Benefits and Harms of AAS ▼
Benefits (performance)Harms
Rapid and significant muscle hypertrophyLiver: peliosis hepatis, hepatocellular carcinoma (liver cancer), jaundice
Increased muscular strength and powerCardiovascular: left ventricular hypertrophy, reduced HDL cholesterol, increased LDL, hypertension, stroke, heart attack
Accelerated recovery between sessions — allows greater training volumeReproductive (male): testicular atrophy, reduced sperm production, infertility, gynaecomastia (breast tissue growth)
Reduced muscle damage from intense trainingReproductive (female): virilisation (masculine characteristics), menstrual irregularity/cessation, clitoral enlargement, deepened voice, facial hair
Possible psychological “benefit” of increased aggression and drivePsychological: mood swings, aggression (“roid rage”), depression, dependence
Skin: severe acne, male-pattern baldness
Youth-specific: premature closure of growth plates — permanently stunted height
⚠ Why AAS are prohibited (WADA criteria met):
✓ Criterion 1 — Performance enhancement: Significant muscle hypertrophy and strength gains
✓ Criterion 2 — Health risk: Liver damage, cardiovascular disease, reproductive harm, psychological effects
✓ Criterion 3 — Spirit of sport: Artificial physiological advantage violates fair competition and the natural athlete ideal
All three criteria are met — AAS are clearly prohibited.
🩹 Peptide Hormones: HGH, EPO, Insulin and IGF-1 PROHIBITED HOT TOPICHGH and EPO benefits and harms are examined ▼

Peptide hormones are naturally occurring hormones that regulate growth, metabolism and red blood cell production. Exogenous (externally administered) use is prohibited by WADA as it provides unfair advantage and poses significant health risks.

► Human Growth Hormone (HGH) ▼
AspectDetail
Natural roleProduced by pituitary gland; stimulates growth, cell reproduction and IGF-1 production. Peaks during puberty and sleep.
Mechanism of dopingExogenous HGH stimulates IGF-1 production, increases protein synthesis, promotes fat metabolism and supports muscle hypertrophy and repair
Performance benefitsAccelerated muscle repair and recovery; increased lean muscle mass; reduced body fat; improved power output over time
HarmsAcromegaly (abnormal bone/organ growth — enlarged jaw, hands, feet); type 2 diabetes; carpal tunnel syndrome; joint pain; potential increased cancer risk; cardiovascular complications; irreversible if started before growth plate closure
WADA status✗ Prohibited in and out of competition
► Erythropoietin (EPO) ▼
AspectDetail
Natural roleProduced by the kidneys in response to low oxygen; stimulates bone marrow to produce more red blood cells (RBCs)
Mechanism of dopingExogenous EPO dramatically increases RBC count and haemoglobin concentration, improving oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood
Performance benefitsGreater aerobic capacity (VO2max); delayed onset of fatigue in endurance activity; improved recovery. More relevant to aerobic performance than musculoskeletal power per se.
HarmsIncreased blood viscosity (blood becomes thicker) — significantly elevated risk of blood clots, stroke, heart attack and death (particularly during sleep when heart rate is low). Several professional cyclists have died from EPO-related causes.
WADA status✗ Prohibited in and out of competition

🩹 Ethical Considerations

📚 The Ethics of Performance Enhancement HOT TOPICEthical arguments for and against are frequently examined ▼

Beyond legality, performance-enhancing substances raise important ethical questions about the nature of sport, fair competition and athlete welfare. VCE PE requires students to be able to evaluate these arguments.

► Arguments Against Doping (Why Prohibition is Justified) ▼
ArgumentExplanation
Unfair competitive advantageDoping artificially and inequitably enhances performance, undermining the principle that sport should reward natural talent, training and effort
Health harm to athletesProhibited substances carry serious short and long-term health risks. Sport has a duty of care to athlete health.
Coercive effect on clean athletesIf doping is widespread, clean athletes face a choice between doping or being unable to compete at elite level — an unacceptable coercion
Violation of the spirit of sportThe spirit of sport celebrates natural human excellence. Doping replaces athletic achievement with pharmacological achievement.
Role model effectElite athletes are role models for young people. Doping normalises drug use and sends harmful messages to youth
► Arguments Sometimes Made in Favour (and Counter-Arguments) ▼
ArgumentCounter-argument
Athletes have autonomy over their own bodies — why shouldn’t they choose to use substances?Autonomy is limited by harm to others (coercion of clean athletes, harm to youth) and the implicit social contract of sport
Many legal supplements (protein, creatine, caffeine) also enhance performance — the line between permitted and prohibited is arbitraryWADA’s two-of-three criteria provides a principled, evidence-based framework for drawing this line — it is not arbitrary
Elite sport is already unfair due to genetics, wealth and access to resourcesPre-existing inequalities are not a justification for adding further artificial and dangerous ones
💡 Exam approach to ethics questions: VCE examiners want to see both sides of the ethical argument. Present arguments against doping clearly, then acknowledge counter-arguments, then provide a reasoned conclusion. A one-sided answer will not achieve full marks on an “evaluate” or “discuss” question.
