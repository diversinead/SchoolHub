🩹 Section 2.1 — Overview of Musculoskeletal Injuries

► Injury Classification Overview ▼
📚 2.1 Overview: Key Concepts ▼

Musculoskeletal injuries affect the bones, muscles, tendons, ligaments, cartilage and other connective tissues of the body. They are among the most common injuries in sport and physical activity.

💡 Two major categories:
Soft tissue injuries — involve muscles, tendons, ligaments, bursae and cartilage (do not involve bone)
Hard tissue injuries — involve bone (fractures, dislocations)
► Acute vs Chronic Injuries ▼
FeatureAcute InjuryChronic / Overuse Injury
OnsetSudden — single traumatic eventGradual — builds over time
CauseDirect blow, fall, collision, awkward movementRepetitive loading, poor technique, overtraining
ExamplesSprained ankle, fractured tibia, hamstring strainStress fracture, shin splints, tendinopathy
Pain onsetImmediateBuilds progressively
SignsSwelling, bruising, pain, reduced ROM immediatelyDull ache, stiffness, worsens with activity
► Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Risk Factors ▼
Intrinsic (internal — within the athlete)Extrinsic (external — outside the athlete)
AgePlaying surface (hard vs soft)
Previous injury historyEquipment (footwear, protective gear)
Muscle strength and flexibilityWeather/environmental conditions
Fitness levelRules of the sport
Body compositionCoaching quality and technique instruction
Structural alignment (e.g. flat feet)Training load and volume
Psychological factors (risk-taking)Referee enforcement of rules
⚠ Exam tip: Intrinsic factors are those within the athlete — remember “IN-trinsic = INternal.” Extrinsic factors come from the external environment.

🩹 Section 2.2 — Soft Tissue Injuries

► Soft Tissue Injury Classification Diagram ▼
🩹 2.2.1 Sprains — Ligament Injuries HOT TOPICFrequently examined — must distinguish sprain vs strain ▼

A sprain is a stretching or tearing of a ligament — the connective tissue that connects bone to bone and stabilises joints.

► Sprain Grades at a Glance ▼
GradeSeverityTissue DamageSigns & SymptomsReturn to Sport
Grade I (Mild)MinorMicroscopic tearing — fibres stretched but intactLocalised pain and tenderness, minimal swelling, no instabilityDays to 1–2 weeks
Grade II (Moderate)Partial tearSignificant proportion of fibres tornModerate swelling, bruising, pain, some joint instability3–8 weeks
Grade III (Severe)Complete ruptureFull thickness tear — ligament no longer functionalSevere swelling, significant bruising, marked instability, loss of function6–12+ months (may require surgery)
► Common Sprain Sites in Sport ▼
SiteLigament(s)Typical MechanismSport Examples
Ankle (lateral)ATFL (anterior talofibular)Inversion (rolling the ankle inward)Basketball, netball, football
Knee (medial)MCL (medial collateral)Valgus force (blow to outside of knee)AFL, rugby, soccer
Knee (anterior)ACL (anterior cruciate)Sudden deceleration, pivot, landingNetball, AFL, soccer
WristScapholunate ligamentFall on outstretched hand (FOOSH)Gymnastics, skateboarding
Thumb (UCL)Ulnar collateral ligamentForced abduction of thumbSkiing, rugby
⚠ Critical distinction (most common exam error):
SPRAIN = LIGAMENT (bone to bone) — think: SPrained ligament
STRAIN = MUSCLE or TENDON — think: STrained muscle
🩹 2.2.2 Strains — Muscle and Tendon Injuries HOT TOPICGrading system frequently examined ▼

A strain is a stretching or tearing of a muscle or its tendon — the connective tissue that connects muscle to bone and transmits force during movement.

► Strain Grades at a Glance ▼
GradeSeverityTissue DamageSigns & SymptomsReturn to Sport
Grade I (Mild)OverstretchingMinimal fibre tearing (<10%)Localised pain, stiffness, minimal swelling, full strength maintained1–2 weeks
Grade II (Moderate)Partial tearPartial muscle/tendon fibre disruptionModerate pain, swelling, bruising, weakness, reduced ROM3–6 weeks
Grade III (Severe)Complete ruptureFull thickness tear or complete tendon avulsionSevere pain at time of injury, significant swelling and bruising, complete loss of strength and function3–6 months (may require surgery)
► Common Strain Sites in Sport ▼
SiteStructureTypical MechanismSport Examples
HamstringBiceps femoris, semimembranosus, semitendinosusRapid sprint acceleration or high kick; eccentric overload at end rangeAFL, sprinting, soccer
Calf (gastrocnemius)Gastrocnemius/soleus musclesSudden push-off or change of directionTennis, squash, basketball
QuadricepsRectus femoris (most common)Powerful kicking or sudden decelerationSoccer, AFL, rugby
Groin (adductors)Adductor longusRapid change of direction or side-stepSoccer, ice hockey, AFL
Achilles tendonAchilles tendonSudden explosive push-off or change in training loadRunning, basketball, netball
💡 Why do strains commonly occur during eccentric contractions?
Eccentric contractions require the muscle to produce force while lengthening — this generates the highest mechanical stress. Hamstring strains during sprinting typically occur in the late swing phase when the hamstring eccentrically decelerates the leg.
🩹 2.2.3 Contusions ▼

A contusion (bruise) is a soft tissue injury caused by a direct blow or impact that does not break the skin. The blow damages the underlying capillaries and muscle fibres, causing bleeding into the surrounding tissue (haematoma).

► Types of Contusions ▼
TypeDescriptionCommon Site
Superficial (subcutaneous)Bleeding into subcutaneous tissue; visible bruising under skinShin, thigh, forearm
Deep (intramuscular)Bleeding confined within the muscle sheath; significant swelling and pressureQuadriceps — “corked thigh”
IntermuscularBleeding tracks between muscle compartments; bruising appears distallyCalf, quadriceps
💡 Intramuscular vs Intermuscular Haematoma:
Intramuscular bleeds have more swelling and pain but bruising may not appear immediately (blood stays contained). Intermuscular bleeds show rapid bruising as blood tracks between fascial planes — paradoxically often less painful.

Myositis ossificans: A complication of deep contusions where calcium deposits form within the healing muscle, causing hardening and pain. Occurs if the injury is inadequately managed (e.g. too-early massage or return to play).

🩹 2.2.4 Bursitis ▼

A bursa is a small fluid-filled sac that reduces friction between tendons, muscles and bone. Bursitis is inflammation of the bursa, typically caused by repetitive friction or direct trauma.

BursaLocationCommon CauseSport
Prepatellar bursaAnterior to patellaRepetitive kneeling / direct blowWrestling, volleyball
Subacromial bursaShoulder, under acromionOverhead activities, rotator cuff impingementSwimming, throwing
Retrocalcaneal bursaBehind the heel, between Achilles tendon and calcaneusExcessive uphill running or tight footwearRunning
Olecranon bursaTip of elbowRepeated leaning on elbow or direct blowGymnastics, wrestling
🩹 2.2.5 Cartilage Injuries ▼

The two types of cartilage relevant to sport injury are articular (hyaline) cartilage and fibrocartilage (menisci). Both have limited blood supply and heal poorly.

► Cartilage Injury Types ▼
InjuryStructureMechanismSigns
Articular cartilage damageHyaline cartilage on joint surfacesCompressive force, repetitive impact, joint instabilityJoint pain, swelling, crepitus (grinding), reduced ROM
Meniscal tearFibrocartilage menisci of the kneeTwisting/rotational force through flexed, weight-bearing kneeJoint line pain, locking, giving way, swelling
TFCC tearTriangular fibrocartilage complex of the wristRotational force through forearm / fall on outstretched handUlnar-sided wrist pain, instability
💡 Why cartilage injuries are serious: Cartilage has no direct blood supply (avascular) — it receives nutrients via synovial fluid. This means it cannot repair itself effectively, and injuries can lead to early onset osteoarthritis.

🩹 Section 2.3 — Hard Tissue Injuries

► Types of Fractures Diagram ▼
🩹 2.3.1 Fractures HOT TOPICFracture types and mechanisms are commonly examined ▼

A fracture is a break or crack in a bone. Fractures can be caused by a single traumatic force (acute fracture) or by repetitive stress over time (stress fracture).

► Fracture Classification Table ▼
Fracture TypeDescriptionMechanismCommon Sites
TransverseBreak at right angles to the long axis of the boneDirect blow perpendicular to boneTibia, radius
ObliqueDiagonal break across the boneAngled compressive forceFemur, fibula
SpiralFracture spirals along the boneTwisting or rotational forceHumerus, tibia
ComminutedBone shattered into three or more fragmentsHigh-energy trauma or crush injuryFemur, tibia
GreenstickIncomplete break — one side cracks, other bendsBending force in children (flexible bones)Radius/ulna in children
Stress fractureHairline crack from repetitive loading — overuse injuryCumulative microtrauma exceeding bone repairTibia, metatarsals, navicular
Avulsion fractureFragment of bone pulled off by a tendon or ligamentSudden forceful muscle contraction or ligament sprainAnkle, hip, fingers
Open (compound)Bone penetrates through skinHigh energy trauma; bone end displaces externallyTibia, femur
Closed (simple)Skin remains intact over fracture siteAny fracture where skin is not brokenAny bone
► Signs and Symptoms of Fractures ▼
  • Pain and tenderness at the fracture site (point tenderness)
  • Deformity — visible angulation or shortening of the limb
  • Swelling and bruising at and around the site
  • Loss of function — inability or reduced ability to move the limb
  • Crepitus — grating sound or sensation when bone ends move
  • Pallor and coolness distal to fracture (suggests vascular compromise)
  • Open wound with visible bone (compound fracture only)
💡 Stress Fracture — key points for exams:
Stress fractures are overuse injuries — they are NOT caused by a single traumatic event. Risk factors include: rapid increase in training load, hard surfaces, poor bone density, inadequate nutrition (low calcium/vitamin D), and female athlete triad. Most common in the tibia and metatarsals.
🩹 2.3.2 Dislocations and Subluxations ▼

A dislocation occurs when the articulating surfaces of a joint are completely separated so they are no longer in contact. A subluxation is a partial dislocation — the surfaces are disrupted but retain some contact.

► Dislocation vs Subluxation ▼
FeatureDislocation (Full)Subluxation (Partial)
Joint surfacesCompletely separatedPartial loss of contact
Ligament damageUsually complete disruptionPartial disruption
SeverityMore severeLess severe but significant
ReductionRequires relocation (often under anaesthesia)May self-reduce
DeformityObvious visible deformityMay be subtle
► Common Dislocation Sites in Sport ▼
JointMechanismAssociated DamageSport
Shoulder (glenohumeral)Forced abduction + external rotation; FOOSHLabrum tear (Bankart), rotator cuff, axillary nerveRugby, AFL, gymnastics
PatellaValgus force, pivoting on fixed footMedial patellofemoral ligament (MPFL) tearNetball, basketball, soccer
Finger (PIP joint)Hyperextension or lateral forceVolar plate injury, collateral ligament sprainBall sports, volleyball
ElbowHyperextension; FOOSHMCL, radial head fractureGymnastics, martial arts
⚠ Never attempt to relocate a dislocated joint — this must only be done by a trained medical professional. Reduction attempts without imaging risk trapping soft tissue, fracturing bone or damaging neurovascular structures.
► Signs and Symptoms of Dislocation ▼
  • Visible deformity — joint appears out of place
  • Severe pain and muscle spasm around the joint
  • Complete loss of normal joint movement
  • Swelling and bruising
  • Numbness or tingling (neurovascular compromise)
  • Limb may be held in an abnormal position
🩹 2.3.3 Stress Fractures (detail) ▼

Stress fractures are overuse / chronic injuries resulting from cumulative microtrauma — the bone is subjected to repeated loading that exceeds its capacity to repair and remodel.

► Stress Fracture Risk Factors ▼
CategoryRisk Factors
Training factorsRapid increase in training volume or intensity (“too much too soon”), hard surfaces, inadequate recovery
BiomechanicalAltered gait, leg length discrepancy, flat feet (pes planus), high arches (pes cavus)
Nutritional / hormonalLow bone density, low calcium/vitamin D intake, female athlete triad (disordered eating, amenorrhea, low BMD)
EquipmentWorn-out footwear, inappropriate footwear for activity
Bone healthOsteoporosis or osteopenia; previous stress fractures
💡 Progression of bone stress:
  1. Normal bone → bone stress reaction (pain, periosteal oedema)
  2. → Stress fracture (cortical disruption, periosteal reaction on imaging)
  3. → Complete fracture (if loading continues)

Management: rest from impact loading, consider bone stimulation, gradual return to activity.

Most common sites: Tibia (most common, 50%), metatarsals (“march fracture”), navicular (high risk — poor blood supply), femoral neck (high risk), fibula.

🩹 Section 2.5 — Spinal Injuries and Postural Conditions

► Spinal Injury and Postural Condition Overview ▼
🩹 2.5.1 Intervertebral Disc Injuries HOT TOPICDisc injuries and nerve compression are commonly examined ▼

The intervertebral disc consists of a tough outer ring (annulus fibrosus) and a gel-like inner core (nucleus pulposus). It acts as a shock absorber between vertebral bodies and allows spinal movement.

► Disc Injury Types ▼
TypeDescriptionSymptoms
Disc bulgeAnnulus fibrosus weakens and disc bulges outward but remains intact — less severeLocal back pain, may have mild referred pain
Disc herniation (prolapse)Nucleus pulposus pushes through a tear in the annulus fibrosus and compresses nearby nerve rootsSharp, radiating pain (sciatica if L4/L5/S1), numbness, tingling, muscle weakness in the leg
Disc extrusionNucleus pulposus breaks through the annulus entirely and material is free in the spinal canalSevere nerve compression symptoms; possible cauda equina syndrome (medical emergency)
► Causes, Risk Factors and Management ▼
Causes / MechanismRisk FactorsManagement
Repetitive flexion-compression loading (e.g. heavy lifting with poor technique)Age (disc dehydrates and loses elasticity)Conservative: rest, physiotherapy, core strengthening
Sudden excessive load or twist (e.g. deadlift)Sedentary lifestyle (poor core stability)Pain management: NSAIDs, epidural injections
Prolonged poor posture (increases disc pressure)Obesity — increases spinal loadSurgical: microdiscectomy in severe cases
Sports involving repeated spinal loading (rowing, gymnastics, weightlifting)Smoking (reduces disc nutrition)Ergonomic adjustments; return-to-sport program
💡 Sciatica: Pain that radiates from the lower back down the leg along the path of the sciatic nerve. Most commonly caused by L4/L5 or L5/S1 disc herniation compressing the nerve root. Symptoms include burning pain, numbness and tingling in the buttock, thigh, calf and foot.
🩹 2.5.2 Spondylolysis and Spondylolisthesis ▼

Spondylolysis is a stress fracture of the pars interarticularis — the narrow bridge of bone connecting the upper and lower facet joints of a vertebra. It is the most common cause of low back pain in young athletes.

Spondylolisthesis occurs when the stress fracture allows the affected vertebra to slip forward on the one below it.

► Spondylolysis vs Spondylolisthesis ▼
FeatureSpondylolysisSpondylolisthesis
DefinitionStress fracture of pars interarticularisForward slip of a vertebra on the one below
CauseRepetitive hyperextension + rotation loadingOften develops from bilateral spondylolysis
LevelMost common at L5 (fifth lumbar vertebra)Most common at L5 slipping on S1
SymptomsCentral low back pain, worse with extension, eases with restLow back pain + possible nerve symptoms if slip is significant
Sports at riskCricket (fast bowling), gymnastics, AFL, divingSame + heavy weightlifting
ManagementRest from extension activities, bracing, core strengtheningConservative initially; surgery if slip >50% or neurological symptoms
💡 Why young athletes? Spondylolysis is common in adolescent athletes because the pars interarticularis is at its weakest during the growth spurt. Sports that combine repeated hyperextension with rotation (e.g. a fast bowler’s action) are highest risk.
🩹 2.5.3 Postural Conditions: Kyphosis, Lordosis and Scoliosis ▼

The normal spine has natural curves: cervical and lumbar regions curve inward (lordosis), and the thoracic and sacral regions curve outward (kyphosis). Postural conditions arise when these curves become exaggerated or when abnormal lateral curvature develops.

► Three Postural Conditions Compared ▼
ConditionDescriptionCauseCommon SignsManagement
Hyperkyphosis Excessive outward (posterior) curve of the thoracic spine — “hunchback” or “rounded upper back” Poor posture (prolonged sitting/screen use), osteoporotic compression fractures, Scheuermann’s disease Rounded shoulders, forward head posture, upper back pain, restricted shoulder ROM Postural correction, thoracic extension exercises, strengthening mid-back muscles
Hyperlordosis Excessive inward (anterior) curve of the lumbar spine — “swayback” or “hollow back” Weak core/abdominal muscles, tight hip flexors, obesity, pregnancy Excessive forward pelvic tilt, protruding abdomen, low back pain Core strengthening, hip flexor stretching, postural re-education
Scoliosis Lateral (side-to-side) curvature of the spine — spine curves in an S or C shape when viewed from behind Idiopathic (most common — unknown cause in adolescents), congenital, neuromuscular Uneven shoulder/hip height, rib hump on forward bend, back pain in severe cases Observation (mild), bracing (moderate), surgery (severe >40-50 degrees)

Hyperkyphosis

  • Thoracic spine
  • Excessive outward curve
  • Rounded shoulders / hunched
  • Linked to poor posture, osteoporosis

Hyperlordosis

  • Lumbar spine
  • Excessive inward curve
  • Swayback / hollow back
  • Linked to weak core, tight hip flexors
💡 Scoliosis screening: Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis is most commonly detected during the adolescent growth spurt (10–16 years). The Adam’s forward bend test is used in schools — a rib hump visible when bending forward indicates vertebral rotation associated with scoliosis.
🩹 2.5.4 Spinal Cord Injuries ▼

A spinal cord injury (SCI) results from damage to the spinal cord itself, usually caused by fracture, dislocation or compression of the vertebral column. Unlike peripheral nerve injuries, SCI damage is often permanent because the central nervous system has very limited regenerative capacity.

► Classification of Spinal Cord Injuries ▼
TermDefinitionLevel of Injury
Tetraplegia (quadriplegia)Paralysis of all four limbs and the trunk; also involves loss of bladder, bowel and sexual functionCervical (C1–C7) injury
ParaplegiaParalysis of the lower limbs and trunk below the injury; upper limb function preservedThoracic (T1–T12) or lumbar injury
Complete SCINo motor or sensory function preserved below the level of injuryAny level
Incomplete SCISome motor or sensory function preserved below the injury levelAny level
► Common Causes in Sport ▼
Sport/ActivityMechanism
Rugby / AFLIllegal or poorly executed tackle; scrummaging collapse
Diving (pool/beach)Diving into shallow water — cervical hyperflexion/compression
EquestrianFall from horse with cervical impact
Gymnastics / trampoliningLanding on head/neck from height
Motor sportHigh-speed collision with axial loading of spine
⚠ First aid priority: Any athlete who sustains a significant neck or back injury and reports numbness, tingling, weakness or pain in the limbs must be treated as a suspected SCI. Do NOT move the athlete — stabilise the head and neck in a neutral position and call emergency services immediately.
