Short Answer3 marks
Explain THREE mechanisms by which taping a joint can prevent musculoskeletal injury.
  • ✓ Mechanical restriction: Rigid tape physically limits the range of motion of the joint, preventing it from moving beyond the safe range into positions where ligament injury occurs. The tape acts as an external ligament, absorbing tensile forces that would otherwise stretch or tear the ligament.
  • ✓ Proprioceptive enhancement: Tape stimulates cutaneous mechanoreceptors (sensory receptors in the skin), increasing joint position sense and accelerating the activation of protective muscles. For example, ankle tape speeds peroneal muscle activation in response to unexpected inversion, providing a muscular protective response before the sprain force fully develops.
  • ✓ Psychological support: Athletes report increased confidence and reduced fear of re-injury when taped. This allows more natural movement patterns, reduces hesitation and may lower the likelihood of compensatory movements that create new injury risks.
⚠ Examiner tip: Each mechanism needs a clear explanation of HOW it prevents injury, not just a name. “Taping improves proprioception” without explaining the sensory receptor mechanism and protective muscle response is worth partial marks only.
Short Answer2 marks
Distinguish between second impact syndrome and chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE).
  • ✓ Second impact syndrome is an acute, potentially fatal condition where a second concussion sustained before the first has fully resolved causes catastrophic, rapid cerebral swelling. It can cause death or severe disability within minutes and is the primary reason no athlete may return to play on the same day as a concussion.
  • ✓ Chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) is a progressive, long-term neurodegenerative disease caused by repeated head trauma over a career. It develops slowly over years and decades, and is characterised by memory loss, depression, behavioural changes and dementia. It can currently only be definitively diagnosed post-mortem.
⚠ Examiner tip: The key distinction is timeframe and mechanism: second impact syndrome is acute and immediately life-threatening; CTE is chronic and progressive. Do not confuse them.
Short Answer — Stimulus4 marks
During an AFL match, a 16-year-old player is involved in a heavy collision. He remains conscious but immediately reports a headache, feels dizzy and is unsure where he is. The coach wants to return him to play after a 10-minute rest as it is the final quarter of an important match.
Evaluate the coach’s decision. Using your knowledge of concussion management, explain what should happen and why.
  • ✓ The coach’s decision is incorrect. The athlete displays clear signs of concussion: headache, dizziness and disorientation. Under all current concussion management protocols, any athlete with suspected concussion must be removed from play immediately and permanently for the remainder of that game — regardless of the importance of the match or remaining time.
  • ✓ Same-day return is prohibited: Returning the player after 10 minutes violates the fundamental rule of concussion management: “if in doubt, sit them out.” Concussion symptoms can evolve over hours, and a 10-minute rest does not constitute adequate assessment or recovery.
  • ✓ Risk of second impact syndrome: Allowing the player to return before the concussion has resolved places him at risk of second impact syndrome — a second impact on an unresolved concussion can cause catastrophic, potentially fatal cerebral swelling. This risk is particularly significant in adolescents whose brains are still developing.
  • ✓ What should happen: The player should be removed from play, kept calm and monitored for deterioration. A medical professional should assess him as soon as possible. He must then complete the full 6-stage Graduated Return to Play protocol before being cleared to return to training or competition, with each stage taking at least 24 hours — a process taking a minimum of 5–6 days.
⚠ Examiner tip: This question asks you to EVALUATE — you must state the decision is wrong AND explain why with specific evidence (second impact syndrome, GRTP). Simply saying “the player should not play” without reasoning scores minimal marks. Reference the age of the player (adolescent) and the specific signs from the stimulus.
Extended Response6 marks
Compare taping and bracing as physical aids for preventing ankle sprains in sport. In your response discuss the mechanisms of each, their relative advantages and disadvantages, and recommend which is most appropriate for: (a) an elite athlete at immediate risk of a sprain during a game, and (b) a recreational athlete managing a chronic ankle instability problem.
  • ✓ Taping mechanism: Rigid zinc oxide tape provides mechanical restriction of inversion and plantarflexion, and enhances proprioception by stimulating cutaneous mechanoreceptors. Initial mechanical support is high but decreases by 20–50% after 20 minutes of vigorous activity. Proprioceptive benefit is maintained throughout.
  • ✓ Bracing mechanism: Lace-up ankle braces restrict inversion via semi-rigid support, providing consistent mechanical restriction throughout activity. Proprioceptive benefit is present but generally less than taping due to reduced skin contact. Support does not degrade with activity.
  • ✓ Advantages of taping: Superior initial mechanical support; excellent proprioceptive enhancement; can be customised to individual anatomy by a clinician; high compliance in high-level sport. Disadvantages: requires skilled application; expensive per session; mechanical support degrades; skin irritation risk.
  • ✓ Advantages of bracing: Consistent support throughout activity; self-applied; lower cost per use; reusable; no skin irritation. Disadvantages: less proprioceptive feedback; may be bulkier; initial cost higher; less customisable than tape.
  • ✓ Recommendation (a) — Elite athlete during a game: Rigid taping is most appropriate. The clinician-applied tape provides customised, high-level initial support and excellent proprioceptive enhancement for the duration of the game. The degradation of mechanical support over time is partially offset by the maintained proprioceptive benefit and the presence of sports trainers to re-tape if needed.
  • ✓ Recommendation (b) — Recreational athlete with chronic instability: A lace-up ankle brace is most appropriate for long-term management. The consistent support, ease of self-application, lower ongoing cost and durability make it more practical for daily recreational sport use. Research shows equal effectiveness to taping for reducing re-sprain rates, making it the cost-effective choice for repeated use.
⚠ Examiner tip: “Compare” means covering both similarities AND differences. The two-scenario recommendation at the end is what lifts this to a high-mark answer — it shows you can apply knowledge to context, not just recall facts. Always justify your recommendation with specific reasons.
