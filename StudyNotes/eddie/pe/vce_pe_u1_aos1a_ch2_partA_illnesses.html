🩹 Section 2.4 — Musculoskeletal Illnesses

📚 2.4 Overview: Illnesses vs Injuries ▼

Musculoskeletal illnesses are conditions that affect the bones, joints and connective tissues but arise from disease processes, degeneration or systemic factors rather than a single traumatic or overuse event. The two most commonly examined in VCE PE are arthritis and osteoporosis.

► Injury vs Illness: Key Distinction ▼
FeatureMusculoskeletal InjuryMusculoskeletal Illness
CauseTraumatic event or repetitive overuseDisease, degeneration, systemic/immune process, or nutritional deficit
OnsetAcute (sudden) or chronic (overuse)Typically gradual and progressive
ExamplesSprain, strain, fracture, dislocationOsteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, osteoporosis
ReversibilityOften heals with appropriate managementUsually chronic and manageable but not fully reversible
Treatment focusRepair, rehabilitation, return to sportManagement, symptom control, maintaining function
⚠ Exam tip: Students frequently blur the line between injury and illness. If a question asks you to identify whether a condition is an injury or illness, look for the cause — traumatic/overuse = injury; degenerative/systemic = illness.

🩹 Section 2.4.1 — Arthritis

► Osteoarthritis vs Rheumatoid Arthritis: Comparison Diagram ▼
🩹 2.4.1 Osteoarthritis (OA) HOT TOPICOA vs RA distinction is a common exam question ▼

Osteoarthritis is a degenerative joint disease characterised by the progressive breakdown of articular cartilage and the underlying bone, leading to pain, stiffness and loss of joint function. It is the most common form of arthritis.

► Causes and Risk Factors ▼
CategoryRisk Factors
AgeMost common after age 50 — cartilage loses its ability to repair with age
Previous injuryHistory of joint injury (e.g. ACL rupture, meniscal tear) accelerates cartilage breakdown — “post-traumatic OA”
Obesity / excess body weightIncreases compressive load on weight-bearing joints (knee, hip)
Repetitive joint loadingOccupational or sporting activities involving repetitive impact or heavy loads
GeneticsFamily history increases risk, particularly for hand and knee OA
SexWomen are at higher risk, particularly after menopause
Muscle weaknessWeakness around a joint reduces its ability to absorb load, increasing cartilage stress
► Pathophysiology: What Happens in OA ▼
  1. Articular cartilage begins to soften and thin (“chondromalacia”)
  2. Cartilage surface becomes irregular — cracks and fissures form
  3. Cartilage may wear away entirely, leaving bone-on-bone contact
  4. Underlying bone responds by hardening (subchondral sclerosis) and forming bony outgrowths (osteophytes / “bone spurs”)
  5. Synovial membrane becomes inflamed — fluid accumulation in joint (effusion)
  6. Result: pain, stiffness, crepitus, reduced range of motion, loss of function
► Signs, Symptoms and Management ▼
Signs & SymptomsManagement Strategies
Joint pain (worse with activity, eases with rest)Low-impact exercise (swimming, cycling) to maintain joint mobility and muscle strength
Morning stiffness (typically <30 minutes)Weight management to reduce joint load
Crepitus (grinding sensation in joint)Physiotherapy: strengthening exercises, joint protection techniques
Reduced range of motionPain management: anti-inflammatory medications (NSAIDs)
Joint swelling and warmthSurgical intervention (total joint replacement) in severe cases
Muscle weakness around affected jointHeat/cold therapy for symptom relief
💡 Physical activity and OA: Regular low-impact exercise is beneficial for people with OA — it maintains cartilage nutrition (via synovial fluid), strengthens supporting muscles and reduces joint load. Complete rest is counterproductive.
🩹 2.4.2 Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA) HOT TOPICMust distinguish RA (autoimmune) from OA (degenerative) ▼

Rheumatoid arthritis is a chronic autoimmune disease in which the body’s immune system mistakenly attacks the synovial membrane lining the joints, causing inflammation, pain and eventual joint destruction.

► Pathophysiology: What Happens in RA ▼
  1. Immune system produces antibodies that attack the synovial membrane (autoimmune response)
  2. Synovial membrane becomes thickened and inflamed (synovitis) — joint swells, becomes warm and painful
  3. Pannus (abnormal inflammatory tissue) forms and invades articular cartilage
  4. Cartilage and underlying bone eroded by inflammatory enzymes
  5. Joint deformity develops over time if untreated (e.g. ulnar deviation of fingers)
  6. Systemic effects: fatigue, anaemia, fever — RA affects the whole body
► Key Features of RA ▼
FeatureDetail
CauseAutoimmune — immune system attacks own synovial membrane
Age of onsetAny age — peak onset 30–60 years; also occurs in children (juvenile RA)
Joint patternSymmetrical — same joints on both sides of body affected simultaneously
Joints most affectedSmall joints of hands (MCP, PIP joints), wrists, feet, knees
Morning stiffnessProlonged — typically more than 1 hour (distinguishes from OA)
Systemic featuresFatigue, weight loss, low-grade fever, anaemia — OA does NOT have systemic features
Rheumatoid nodulesFirm lumps under the skin near affected joints

▲ OA — Remember

  • Degenerative / wear and tear
  • Cartilage breaks down
  • Usually older adults
  • Weight-bearing joints
  • Morning stiffness <30 min
  • No systemic symptoms

▲ RA — Remember

  • Autoimmune — immune attack
  • Synovial membrane attacked first
  • Any age; often younger
  • Symmetrical small joints
  • Morning stiffness >1 hour
  • Systemic symptoms (fatigue, fever)
⚠ Most common exam error: Students describe RA as a “wear and tear” condition — this is wrong. RA is autoimmune. The immune system attacks the synovial membrane. OA is the wear and tear condition.

🩹 Section 2.4.2 — Osteoporosis

► Bone Density Across the Lifespan Diagram ▼
🩹 2.4.2 Osteoporosis HOT TOPICRisk factors and physical activity role are frequently examined ▼

Osteoporosis is a systemic skeletal disease characterised by low bone mineral density (BMD) and deterioration of bone microarchitecture, leading to increased bone fragility and fracture risk. The word literally means “porous bone.”

► Normal Bone vs Osteoporotic Bone ▼
FeatureNormal (Healthy) BoneOsteoporotic Bone
StructureDense trabecular network (like a honeycomb with thick struts)Thinned, sparse trabeculae — large spaces between struts
DensityHigh bone mineral density (BMD)BMD >2.5 standard deviations below young adult mean (T-score ≤-2.5)
Fracture riskLow — bone can absorb normal loadsHigh — minor trauma or even daily activities can cause fracture
AppearanceSolid, ivory-coloured cross-sectionHoneycombed with large voids; lighter and more fragile

Osteopenia = bone density below normal but not yet meeting the threshold for osteoporosis (T-score between -1.0 and -2.5). A precursor state to osteoporosis.

► Risk Factors for Osteoporosis ▼
Non-modifiableModifiable (lifestyle)
Female sex (especially post-menopause)Low calcium and vitamin D intake
Advancing agePhysical inactivity / sedentary lifestyle
Family history / geneticsSmoking
Small body frameExcessive alcohol consumption
Early menopause (<45 yrs)Low body weight / eating disorders
Caucasian or Asian ethnicityFemale athlete triad (disordered eating, amenorrhea, low BMD)
► Role of Physical Activity in Osteoporosis Prevention and Management ▼

Bone responds to mechanical loading — when bone is stressed through weight-bearing and impact activity, osteoblasts (bone-building cells) are stimulated to lay down new bone tissue. This is called Wolff’s Law.

Activity TypeEffect on BoneExamples
Weight-bearing impactMost effective for stimulating bone formation — ground reaction forces stress boneRunning, jumping, netball, basketball, dancing
Resistance trainingMuscle contractions place tension on bone attachment sites — stimulates remodellingWeight training, bodyweight exercises
Non-weight-bearingMinimal bone-loading benefit — but useful for cardiovascular fitness with low fracture riskSwimming, cycling
💡 Peak bone mass is reached around age 25–30. Physical activity during childhood and adolescence is critical — higher peak bone mass provides a greater “bone bank” to draw from during the inevitable age-related decline.
► Fractures Associated with Osteoporosis ▼
Fracture SiteMechanismSignificance

Hip fracture (femoral neck)Fall from standing heightHighest morbidity and mortality — 20% die within one year
