📝 Short Answer Questions

Short Answer2 marks
Distinguish between a ligament and a tendon.
✅ Model Answer
✓A ligament is dense fibrous connective tissue that connects bone to bone at a joint, providing stability and limiting excessive range of motion. (1 mark)
✓A tendon is dense connective tissue that connects muscle to bone, transmitting the force of muscular contraction to the skeleton to produce movement. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: The most common error is reversing these definitions. You must state what each connects — "bone to bone" and "muscle to bone" are the key phrases that earn marks. Simply saying "ligaments hold joints together" is not enough for full marks.
Short Answer3 marks
Identify and describe THREE functions of the skeletal system.
✅ Model Answer

Any THREE of the following (1 mark each):

✓Support — the skeleton provides the structural framework that holds the body upright (e.g. the vertebral column supports the head and trunk).
✓Protection — bones shield vital organs from injury (e.g. the skull protects the brain; the ribcage protects the heart and lungs).
✓Movement — bones act as levers; muscles pull on bones to produce movement at joints.
✓Blood cell production — red bone marrow found in long bones produces red and white blood cells.
✓Mineral storage — bones store calcium and phosphorus, which are released into the bloodstream when needed.
✓Energy storage — yellow bone marrow in the diaphysis (shaft) of long bones stores fat as an energy reserve.
⚠ Examiner tip: Always include an example to support each function — examiners look for this when deciding between full and partial marks. Naming the function alone (e.g. just writing "protection") is often insufficient.
Short Answer4 marks
Describe the structure and function of a synovial joint. In your answer, refer to at least FOUR structural features.
✅ Model Answer

One mark per correctly named and described structural feature (any four):

✓Articular cartilage — smooth hyaline cartilage covering the ends of bones; reduces friction and absorbs shock during movement.
✓Synovial membrane — inner lining of the joint capsule that secretes synovial fluid.
✓Synovial fluid — viscous fluid within the joint cavity that lubricates the joint, reduces friction and nourishes the articular cartilage.
✓Joint capsule — fibrous outer sleeve enclosing the joint, providing stability.
✓Ligaments — connect bone to bone; reinforce the joint capsule and prevent excessive movement.
✓Bursae — fluid-filled sacs that reduce friction between tendons, muscles and bony prominences.
✓Menisci — fibrocartilage pads at the knee only; deepen the socket and absorb compressive forces.
⚠ Examiner tip: Each structural feature must include its function — naming it alone scores zero. Common error: writing "ligaments hold muscles to bones" — this describes tendons, not ligaments, and will lose marks.
Short Answer4 marks
Name the five sections of the vertebral column. For each, state the number of vertebrae and one function.
✅ Model Answer
✓Cervical (7 vertebrae) — supports the head; atlas and axis allow the head to move up, down and side to side.
✓Thoracic (12 vertebrae) — connects the ribcage to the vertebral column.
✓Lumbar (5 vertebrae) — supports the weight of the body; provides attachment for large back muscles.
✓Sacrum (5 fused vertebrae) — forms the posterior wall of the pelvis; fused and immoveable.
✓Coccyx (4 fused vertebrae) — base of the vertebral column (tailbone); provides muscle attachment.

Award 1 mark per section correctly named with number of vertebrae + function. Accept partial marks if name and number are correct but function is missing.

⚠ Examiner tip: The sequence 7–12–5–5–4 is worth memorising. A common error is stating the sacrum and coccyx allow movement — they are fused and immoveable.

📝 Extended Response Questions

Extended Response6 marks
Using a physical activity of your choice, identify THREE joints involved in the movement. For each joint, name the joint type, state the movements possible and identify the bones that form the joint.
✅ Model Answer — Example: Running

2 marks per joint (joint type + movements + bones forming it). Example using running:

✓Hip joint — ball and socket joint, formed by the head of the femur and the acetabulum of the pelvis. Allows flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, rotation and circumduction. During running, primarily hip flexion and extension occur.
✓Knee joint — hinge joint, formed by the femur and tibia (with the fibula laterally). Allows flexion and extension only. During running, the knee flexes during swing phase and extends during push-off.
✓Ankle joint — hinge joint, formed by the tibia, fibula and talus. Allows dorsiflexion and plantarflexion. During running, dorsiflexion occurs at heel strike and plantarflexion at push-off.
⚠ Examiner tip: Do not just name the joint and move on — you must state the TYPE (e.g. hinge, ball and socket), the BONES that form it, and the MOVEMENTS it allows. Missing any one of these elements will cost marks. Use sport-specific language to contextualise your answer.
Extended Response8 marks
A 16-year-old athlete has been experiencing knee pain after training. An MRI scan reveals damage to the medial meniscus and anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) of the left knee.
Using your knowledge of the structure of synovial joints, explain the role of the medial meniscus and ACL in the knee joint, and describe how damage to these structures could affect the athlete's ability to train and compete.
✅ Model Answer

Award marks across four areas (2 marks each):

✓Role of the medial meniscus (2 marks): The medial meniscus is a C-shaped fibrocartilage pad located between the femur and tibia on the medial (inner) side of the knee. Its functions include absorbing and distributing compressive forces across the joint, deepening the tibial socket to improve joint stability, and acting as a shock absorber during weight-bearing activities such as running and jumping.
✓Role of the ACL (2 marks): The ACL (anterior cruciate ligament) connects the femur to the tibia and prevents the tibia from sliding anteriorly (forward) relative to the femur. It also provides rotational stability to the knee joint, which is critical during changes of direction and landing.
✓Impact of meniscus damage (2 marks): A torn medial meniscus reduces the knee joint's ability to absorb shock and distribute load evenly across the joint surface. This would cause pain, swelling and instability during weight-bearing activities. The athlete would likely be unable to run, jump or change direction effectively, significantly limiting training capacity and competition.
✓Impact of ACL damage (2 marks): A ruptured ACL results in anterior instability of the knee — the tibia can slide forward on the femur. This causes significant pain, joint instability and loss of confidence in the knee, particularly during cutting, pivoting and landing movements. ACL injuries typically require surgical reconstruction and 9–12 months of rehabilitation, meaning the athlete would be unable to compete for an extended period.
⚠ Examiner tip: Extended response questions reward structured, detailed answers. Use anatomical terminology throughout — "tibia slides anteriorly on the femur" scores higher than "the knee becomes unstable". Always link the injury back to how it affects function and performance. Avoid vague phrases like "it would hurt and they could not play sport."
Extended Response6 marks
Explain how the structure of the vertebral column allows it to perform its key functions. In your answer, refer to specific sections of the vertebral column and the role of intervertebral discs.
✅ Model Answer

Award 1 mark per well-developed point, up to 6 marks:

✓The vertebral column consists of 33 vertebrae arranged in five sections: cervical (7), thoracic (12), lumbar (5), sacrum (5 fused) and coccyx (4 fused), forming an S-shaped curve that helps distribute body weight and absorb shock.
✓The cervical section supports the skull and allows a wide range of head movement via the atlas (C1) and axis (C2) joints, which enable flexion, extension and rotation of the head.
✓The thoracic vertebrae articulate with the 12 pairs of ribs, connecting them to the vertebral column and forming the protective thoracic cage around the heart and lungs.
✓The lumbar vertebrae are the largest and strongest, reflecting their role in bearing the weight of the entire upper body and providing attachment for major postural muscles.
✓The sacrum and coccyx are fused vertebrae that form the posterior pelvis and tailbone; being fused they provide a stable base for the vertebral column and lower limb attachment.
✓Intervertebral discs are fibrocartilage pads located between each pair of vertebrae. They act as shock absorbers, reducing the impact of compressive forces during activities such as running, jumping and lifting. They also allow slight movement between adjacent vertebrae, giving the spine its overall flexibility.
⚠ Examiner tip: The key to high marks here is linking STRUCTURE to FUNCTION — not just listing what each section is called, but explaining WHY it is structured that way. For example, lumbar vertebrae are larger BECAUSE they bear more weight. This cause-and-effect reasoning is what distinguishes Band 5–6 responses.
