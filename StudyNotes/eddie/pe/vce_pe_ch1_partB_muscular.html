💪 1.3 The Structure and Function of the Muscular System

Functions of the Muscular System

📈 Functions of the Muscular System ▼
FunctionDescriptionSport Example
MovementMuscles contract to pull on bones, producing movement at joints via the lever systemQuadriceps extend the knee to kick a football
Posture and stabilityContinuous low-level muscle contractions maintain body position against gravityCore muscles hold a gymnast upright on the beam
Heat productionMuscle contractions generate heat as a by-product of metabolism — critical for thermoregulationShivering is involuntary muscle contraction to generate warmth
ProtectionMuscles surrounding joints and organs provide a protective layer against impactAbdominal muscles protect internal organs during contact sport
Assist circulationSkeletal muscle contractions in the legs compress veins and assist venous return of blood to the heartCalf muscles act as a peripheral pump during running

💡 Movement is the primary function examiners focus on. Always link it to the lever system — muscles provide the FORCE on the bone (lever) to produce movement at the joint (fulcrum).

💪 Three Types of Muscle Tissue ▼
TypeLocationControlAppearanceKey feature
Skeletal muscle Attached to bones via tendons; throughout the body Voluntary (conscious control) Striated (striped) — alternating dark and light bands Produces movement, posture and heat. The focus of this AOS.
Cardiac muscle Heart wall only Involuntary (autonomic nervous system) Striated; branched, interconnected fibres with intercalated discs Never fatigues under normal conditions; contracts rhythmically and continuously for life
Smooth muscle Walls of hollow organs — blood vessels, stomach, intestines, bladder Involuntary (autonomic nervous system) Non-striated (no visible striations); spindle-shaped cells Controls internal organ function — e.g. vasoconstriction/dilation of blood vessels during exercise

💡 Only SKELETAL muscle is under voluntary control and is the focus of this chapter. Cardiac and smooth muscle are involuntary. All three are striated EXCEPT smooth muscle.

Muscle Structure

🥀 Diagram: From Muscle to Sarcomere — Structural Hierarchy ▼

Muscle organisation: Epimysium wraps the whole muscle → Perimysium wraps fascicles (fibre bundles) → Endomysium wraps individual fibres → Myofibrils contain actin and myosin → Sarcomere is the functional unit of contraction.

🥀 Muscle Structural Components ▼
StructureDescriptionRole
EpimysiumOuter connective tissue sheath wrapping the entire muscle bellyHolds the muscle together; merges with the tendon at each end
PerimysiumConnective tissue wrapping bundles of muscle fibres (fascicles)Groups fibres; carries blood vessels and nerves to the fascicle
EndomysiumConnective tissue surrounding each individual muscle fibre (cell)Carries capillaries and nerves directly to each fibre
FascicleA bundle of muscle fibres wrapped in perimysiumOrganisational unit between whole muscle and individual fibre
Muscle fibre (myocyte)Single, long, multinucleated muscle cell containing myofibrilsThe contractile unit visible to the naked eye
MyofibrilThread-like structures within a fibre containing actin (thin) and myosin (thick) filamentsContains the contractile proteins; site of the sliding filament mechanism
SarcomereThe segment between two Z-lines within a myofibril; contains actin and myosin arranged in overlapping filamentsFunctional unit of muscle contraction — shortens during contraction
ActinThin protein filament with binding sites for myosin heads; regulated by troponin and tropomyosinSlides toward the centre of the sarcomere during contraction
MyosinThick protein filament with globular heads that bind actin and perform the power strokePulls actin filaments inward using ATP-powered cross-bridge cycling
TendonDense connective tissue attaching muscle to bone; formed by the merging of epimysium at each muscle endTransmits force of contraction to the skeleton to produce joint movement

💡 The connective tissue layers (epi-, peri-, endomysium) all merge at the muscle ends to form the tendon. Understanding this helps explain how force is transmitted from fibre to bone.

Muscle Roles During Movement

📈 Muscle Roles — Agonist, Antagonist, Synergist, Fixator ▼
RoleDefinitionExample — Bicep Curl (upward phase)Example — Drop Punt Kick
Agonist (prime mover)The muscle primarily responsible for producing the movement; typically contracts concentricallyBiceps brachii (elbow flexion)Quadriceps (knee extension)
AntagonistOpposes the agonist; must relax or eccentrically contract to allow smooth, controlled movementTriceps brachii (relaxes/eccentrically controls)Hamstrings (eccentrically control knee extension speed)
SynergistAssists the agonist to fine-tune or support the movement; reduces unwanted joint actionsBrachialis, brachioradialisIliopsoas (assists hip flexion in backswing)
Fixator (stabiliser)Stabilises the origin bone of the agonist so it can pull effectively on the insertionDeltoid stabilises the shoulder jointCore muscles stabilise the trunk and pelvis

⚠ In ANY movement analysis exam question, always identify BOTH the agonist AND the antagonist. A common error is identifying only the agonist. You should also name the joint, joint type, and movement occurring.

▶️ Diagram: Movement Analysis — Running Stride ▼

Running uses alternating push-off (extension dominant) and swing (flexion dominant) phases. Note how agonist/antagonist roles REVERSE between phases — the hamstrings are antagonist during push-off but agonist during swing.

Motor Units & Recruitment

⚡ Motor Units and the Size Principle ▼
ConceptDescriptionSport Application
Motor unitA single motor neuron and ALL the muscle fibres it innervates. When the neuron fires, ALL its fibres contract (all-or-nothing)Fine motor tasks use small motor units; maximal effort uses large ones
All-or-nothing lawA muscle fibre either contracts fully or not at all — there is no partial contraction within one fibre. Force is graded by recruiting MORE motor unitsGripping a pen (few units) vs gripping a barbell maximally (all units)
Size principleMotor units are recruited in order from SMALLEST to LARGEST as intensity increases: Type I first, then Type IIa, then Type IIxLight jogging recruits only Type I; a maximal sprint recruits all types including Type IIx
Motor unit recruitmentThe process of activating more motor units to increase total muscle force outputIncreasing barbell weight requires recruiting more and larger motor units
Rate codingIncreasing the FREQUENCY of nerve impulses to a motor unit to increase force — firing faster produces more force from the same unitThe nervous system uses both recruitment AND rate coding to fine-tune force output

💡 The size principle explains why endurance exercise feels easy at first — only slow twitch Type I units are working. As intensity increases, the body progressively adds IIa then IIx units. This is why sprinting feels exponentially harder than jogging.
