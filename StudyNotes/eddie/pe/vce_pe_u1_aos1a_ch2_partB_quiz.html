📄 Exam Practice Tips: Write your answer before revealing the model. Each ✓ = one mark.

Short Answer2 marks
Distinguish between RICER and HARM in the management of an acute soft tissue injury.
  • ✓ RICER is a protocol of actions to apply immediately after an acute soft tissue injury: Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation and Referral. These actions work together to minimise bleeding, swelling and further tissue damage.
  • ✓ HARM identifies actions to avoid in the first 48–72 hours: Heat, Alcohol, Running and Massage. All four cause vasodilation or mechanical disruption that increases bleeding and swelling, worsening the injury.
⚠ Examiner tip: A common error is giving only the acronym letters without explaining the purpose. For both RICER and HARM you must state what each letter stands for AND why it either helps or harms recovery to score full marks.
Short Answer4 marks
Explain FOUR physiological effects of a warm-up and for each effect, describe how it reduces the risk of musculoskeletal injury.
  • ✓ Increased muscle temperature — metabolic activity generates heat in working muscles, making muscle fibres more extensible (pliable), reducing the risk of tearing during rapid or forceful movements (reduces strain risk).
  • ✓ Increased blood flow to muscles — vasodilation delivers more oxygen and removes metabolic waste, allowing muscles to function more efficiently and reducing the likelihood of fatigue-related coordination failures that cause injury.
  • ✓ Increased nerve conduction velocity — warmer tissues conduct electrical signals faster, improving reaction time and the speed of protective muscle activation in response to unexpected forces, reducing ligament and joint injury risk.
  • ✓ Increased synovial fluid production — joint movement stimulates the synovial membrane to produce lubricating fluid, reducing friction between articular cartilage surfaces and decreasing cartilage wear during activity.
⚠ Examiner tip: Each mark requires both the effect AND the injury prevention link. Writing only “increases blood flow” without explaining how that reduces injury risk is worth zero marks. Use the structure: effect → mechanism → injury benefit.
Short Answer — Stimulus3 marks
Jayden is a 17-year-old basketball player who rolled his ankle during a game. The team trainer suspects a Grade II lateral ankle sprain. Jayden wants to return to training as quickly as possible and asks the trainer if he can jog on it later that evening.
Using your knowledge of RICER and HARM, advise Jayden on the correct immediate management of his injury and explain why jogging should be avoided.
  • ✓ RICER advice: Jayden should immediately Rest (stop activity, no weight-bearing), apply Ice (20 min every 2 hours) to reduce bleeding and pain, apply Compression (firm bandage) to limit swelling, Elevate the ankle above heart level, and seek Referral to a physiotherapist or doctor for diagnosis and management planning.
  • ✓ Why jogging must be avoided (HARM): Running/jogging is the R in HARM — it increases blood flow and mechanical stress through the damaged ankle ligament, worsening bleeding into the tissue, increasing swelling, and risking further tearing of the partially torn ATFL fibres.
  • ✓ Link to stimulus: Despite Jayden’s desire to return quickly, jogging the same evening would constitute a HARM action. The Grade II sprain involves partial tearing — the ligament fibres are structurally compromised and cannot tolerate loading for at least 48–72 hours. Early return risks converting the Grade II injury into a Grade III complete rupture.
⚠ Examiner tip: Always reference the specific injury from the stimulus (Grade II lateral ankle sprain, ATFL). Generic RICER/HARM answers without linking to the scenario miss the highest-order marks.
Extended Response6 marks
Explain how meeting minimum fitness requirements can prevent musculoskeletal injury in sport. In your response, discuss at least THREE specific fitness components and link each to a clear injury prevention mechanism.
  • ✓ Muscular strength: Adequate strength in the muscles surrounding a joint allows them to absorb and dissipate impact forces, stabilise the joint and reduce the load transferred to ligaments and tendons. For example, strong quadriceps reduce anterior tibial translation during landing, decreasing ACL stress and sprain risk.
  • ✓ Muscular endurance: When muscles fatigue, their ability to generate force rapidly and maintain coordination deteriorates. Fatigued muscles can no longer protect joints from abnormal forces, increasing the risk of strains and sprains particularly in the final stages of competition. High muscular endurance delays this fatigue threshold, maintaining protective muscle function throughout activity.
  • ✓ Flexibility: Adequate flexibility allows joints to move through their full intended range of motion without placing excessive tension on muscles or tendons at end range. Athletes with poor hip flexor flexibility, for example, are at greater risk of lumbar and hamstring injuries as the restricted hip movement increases load on adjacent structures.
  • ✓ Neuromuscular control / proprioception: The ability to rapidly detect and respond to changes in joint position is critical for preventing injuries from unexpected forces. Athletes with poor proprioception (often following a previous injury) are significantly more likely to re-sprain a joint because protective muscle activation is delayed. Balance training specifically targets this component.
  • ✓ Cardiovascular fitness: A high aerobic fitness level delays general fatigue, maintaining concentration and movement mechanics across the full duration of training or competition. Breakdowns in running technique late in a game — such as foot strike pattern or trunk stability — are a common cause of ankle, knee and lower back injuries in fatigued athletes.
  • ✓ Conclusion / overall principle: When an athlete does not meet the minimum fitness requirements for their sport, they are more likely to fatigue rapidly, lose movement quality and expose joints to uncontrolled forces — the combination of which dramatically elevates injury risk. A well-designed pre-season conditioning program that targets all relevant fitness components is therefore a primary injury prevention strategy.
⚠ Examiner tip: Vague answers like “being fitter means less chance of getting hurt” score zero. Each fitness component must be linked to a SPECIFIC mechanism (e.g. muscle absorbs load, fatigue impairs coordination). Use sport-specific examples to demonstrate application of knowledge.
Extended Response7 marks
Sarah is a 22-year-old netball player who suffered a Grade III ACL tear eight weeks ago and has undergone surgical reconstruction. She is now beginning her rehabilitation program and is aiming to return to competition in 9–12 months.
Outline the four phases of rehabilitation Sarah will progress through on her return to sport. For each phase, describe the goals, key activities and criteria she must meet before progressing to the next phase.
  • ✓ Phase 1 — Acute / Protection: Goal: control post-surgical inflammation, protect the graft and manage pain. Activities: RICER principles, pain-free gentle ROM exercises (heel slides), crutch use to offload. Criteria to progress: swelling reducing, pain at rest minimal, able to perform straight leg raise without pain.
  • ✓ Phase 2 — Sub-Acute / Repair: Goal: restore full pain-free ROM, begin light loading, maintain cardiovascular fitness. Activities: hydrotherapy (pool walking/running), gentle closed-chain strengthening (mini squats, leg press), stationary cycling. Criteria: full pain-free ROM, no swelling with activity, normal walking gait restored.
  • ✓ Phase 3 — Remodelling / Strength: Goal: rebuild quadriceps and hamstring strength, power and neuromuscular control to pre-injury levels. Activities: progressive resistance training, proprioception exercises (wobble board, single-leg balance), plyometric progression (jumping, hopping, landing mechanics). Criteria: quadriceps strength ≥90% of uninjured leg (limb symmetry index), normal movement patterns, pain-free with loading.
  • ✓ Phase 4 — Return to Sport: Goal: restore full sport-specific function, confidence and integration into team training. Activities: netball-specific drills (cutting, pivoting, jumping for ball), contact practice, full training sessions, match simulation. Criteria: pass return-to-sport testing (hop tests, agility), psychological readiness, medical clearance from surgeon and physiotherapist.
  • ✓ Criteria-based progression principle: Sarah must meet the functional criteria for each phase before advancing — not just reach a calendar date. Given the ACL graft takes 12–18 months to fully mature (ligamentisation), her 9–12 month timeline must be guided by objective testing, not symptoms alone.
  • ✓ Rehabilitation principles applied: Progressive overload ensures each phase incrementally challenges the healing graft. Specificity means late-stage drills must replicate netball’s cutting and pivoting demands. Reversibility means Sarah’s overall fitness (cardiovascular, upper body) must also be maintained throughout rehabilitation, not just the ACL-specific work.
  • ✓ Link to stimulus: As a Grade III tear requiring surgical reconstruction, Sarah’s rehabilitation is more complex than a conservative injury. The graft is initially at its weakest point around 6–12 weeks post-surgery (the period Sarah is currently in), making careful Phase 2 management critical to avoid graft failure before progressing to heavy loading in Phase 3.
⚠ Examiner tip: This question has four named components (goals, activities, criteria, all four phases) — tick off each as you write. Students who only describe activities without stating goals and criteria miss multiple marks. Reference the ACL graft and surgical reconstruction from the stimulus to demonstrate higher-order application.
