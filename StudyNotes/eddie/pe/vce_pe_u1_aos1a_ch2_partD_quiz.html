Short Answer3 marks
Explain the role of WADA in regulating doping in sport. In your answer, identify the THREE criteria WADA uses to determine whether a substance should be placed on the Prohibited List.
  • ✓ WADA’s role: The World Anti-Doping Agency is the independent international body responsible for leading the global fight against doping in sport. It maintains the Prohibited List (updated annually), sets the World Anti-Doping Code, coordinates testing programs through national agencies (e.g. ASADA in Australia), manages Therapeutic Use Exemptions and funds anti-doping research and education.
  • ✓ Three criteria (a substance is banned if it meets TWO of three): (1) It has the potential to enhance or enhances athletic performance; (2) It represents a real or potential health risk to the athlete; (3) Its use violates the spirit of sport — the values of fair play, honesty, health and human excellence.
  • ✓ Key point — only two required: A substance does not need to meet all three criteria to be banned. For example, a substance could be banned if it enhances performance AND poses a health risk, even if the spirit of sport argument is less clear-cut.
⚠ Examiner tip: Students frequently state that all three criteria must be met — this is wrong. Only TWO of three are required. Always state this clearly. Also, many students describe WADA’s role as just “making the list” — expand to include testing coordination, TUEs and education for full marks.
Short Answer4 marks
Compare the performance benefits and health harms of anabolic androgenic steroids (AAS) and creatine supplementation. Include the WADA status of each.
  • ✓ AAS — Performance benefits: Rapid and significant muscle hypertrophy through androgen receptor binding and increased MPS; greater strength and power; accelerated recovery allowing more training volume. WADA status: PROHIBITED in and out of competition.
  • ✓ AAS — Health harms: Liver damage (hepatocellular carcinoma); cardiovascular disease (LVH, reduced HDL, hypertension, stroke); sex-specific harms (testicular atrophy and infertility in males; virilisation and menstrual disruption in females); psychological effects (aggression, depression); growth plate closure in adolescents.
  • ✓ Creatine — Performance benefits: Increases phosphocreatine stores in muscle, enhancing ATP regeneration during short, high-intensity efforts (1–30 seconds). Improves maximal strength, repeated sprint capacity and training volume over time. Most effective for power sports and team sports. WADA status: PERMITTED.
  • ✓ Creatine — Health harms (minimal): Short-term water retention (1–2 kg); gastrointestinal discomfort at loading doses; theoretical concern re: renal stress in those with pre-existing kidney conditions; contamination risk from unregulated products. No serious health risks in healthy individuals at recommended doses.
⚠ Examiner tip: The most common error is confusing creatine (permitted, naturally occurring) with steroids (prohibited, synthetic). They must be kept clearly separate. For AAS harms, always distinguish between male-specific, female-specific and general harms to show depth of knowledge.
Short Answer — Stimulus3 marks
Jaxon is a 16-year-old elite junior sprinter hoping to make the national team. His coach suggests he begin taking a hormone supplement that will “speed up his muscle growth and help him recover faster.” Jaxon’s parent looks up the supplement online and discovers it contains synthetic HGH.
Advise Jaxon’s family on whether he should take this supplement. Use your knowledge of HGH, WADA regulations and risks specific to adolescent athletes in your response.
  • ✓ WADA status — Jaxon must not take it: Human Growth Hormone (HGH) is on the WADA Prohibited List and is banned in and out of competition. If detected in testing, Jaxon would face a doping violation, suspension and potential permanent ban — ending his athletics career before it begins. Under strict liability, he is responsible for what he ingests regardless of who recommended it.
  • ✓ Adolescent-specific harm: At 16, Jaxon’s growth plates are still open. Exogenous HGH can cause premature growth plate closure, permanently stunting his height — an irreversible harm. HGH also carries risks of acromegaly (abnormal bone and organ growth), type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular complications.
  • ✓ Ethical dimension: Using HGH would violate the spirit of sport — replacing the legitimate outcome of training with a pharmacological shortcut. Jaxon’s coach has given dangerously irresponsible advice. Jaxon should instead focus on legal, evidence-based methods: structured sprint training, periodised nutrition, adequate sleep and potentially creatine supplementation (which is permitted and evidence-based for sprint performance).
⚠ Examiner tip: This question requires application to Jaxon specifically — reference his age (adolescent, open growth plates), the strict liability principle (he is responsible) and the specific harms of HGH. A generic “steroids are bad” answer without linking to the stimulus will score poorly.
Extended Response8 marks
Evaluate the use of anabolic androgenic steroids (AAS) as a method of enhancing musculoskeletal performance. In your response, explain the mechanism by which AAS enhance performance, outline the health harms for both male and female athletes, apply the WADA criteria to justify their prohibition, and discuss ONE ethical argument against their use.
  • ✓ Mechanism of AAS: Anabolic androgenic steroids are synthetic derivatives of testosterone. They enter muscle cells, bind to androgen receptors in the nucleus and activate gene expression for muscle protein synthesis (MPS). This increases actin and myosin content within muscle fibres, causing significant hypertrophy. AAS also reduce muscle breakdown (anti-catabolic effect) and accelerate recovery, allowing greater training volume.
  • ✓ Performance benefits: Rapid and dramatic muscle hypertrophy and strength increases beyond what is achievable naturally; accelerated recovery between sessions; ability to sustain higher training loads; increased aggression that may benefit certain competitive contexts.
  • ✓ Health harms — general (both sexes): Liver damage including peliosis hepatis and hepatocellular carcinoma; cardiovascular disease including left ventricular hypertrophy, reduced HDL, elevated LDL, hypertension, heart attack and stroke; severe acne; psychological effects including aggression, mood instability and depression; potential dependence.
  • ✓ Health harms — male-specific: Testicular atrophy (testes shrink due to suppression of natural testosterone production); reduced sperm production and infertility; gynaecomastia (breast tissue growth from excess testosterone converted to oestrogen).
  • ✓ Health harms — female-specific: Virilisation — development of male characteristics including facial hair, voice deepening, clitoral enlargement; menstrual disruption or cessation; these changes may be irreversible even after stopping AAS.
  • ✓ WADA criteria (all three met): (1) Performance enhancement — AAS clearly and substantially enhance muscular strength and power; (2) Health risk — extensive evidence of serious short and long-term health consequences; (3) Spirit of sport — the artificial pharmacological advantage fundamentally contradicts the principle that sport rewards natural talent, training and effort. Since all three criteria are met (and only two are required), AAS are unambiguously on the Prohibited List.
  • ✓ Ethical argument against use: AAS use creates an unacceptable coercion of clean athletes. If doping is widespread in a sport, athletes who want to compete at the elite level effectively face a choice between doping or being unable to compete. This undermines the autonomy argument sometimes made in favour of doping — when one athlete’s choice forces others to dope to remain competitive, individual freedom becomes a collective harm. Sport has a responsibility to protect athletes from this coercion.
  • ✓ Conclusion / evaluation: While AAS produce genuine and significant performance benefits, the combination of serious health harms, clear violation of all three WADA criteria and fundamental ethical contradiction with the purpose of sport provides overwhelming justification for their prohibition. No performance gain justifies the documented cardiovascular, hepatic, reproductive and psychological consequences, particularly in developing athletes.
⚠ Examiner tip: An 8-mark evaluate question requires you to cover ALL components: mechanism, benefits, sex-specific harms, WADA criteria and ethics. Students who write only about harms without explaining the mechanism or applying WADA criteria miss several marks. “Evaluate” also requires a concluding judgment — state clearly whether the use is justified and why.
