⚡ 1.5 Neural Control of Muscles

The Nervous System and Muscle Control

⚡ Diagram: Nervous Control of Muscular Movement — Pathway ▼

The nervous system controls muscle contraction via a 4-stage pathway: Brain → Spinal cord → Motor neurons → NMJ → Muscle fibres contract.

⚡ Divisions of the Nervous System ▼
DivisionFunctionRelevance to movement
Central nervous system (CNS)Brain and spinal cord — processes information and coordinates responsesBrain decides to move; spinal cord relays the signal to motor neurons
Peripheral nervous system (PNS)All nerves outside the CNS — connects the CNS to muscles, organs and sensory receptorsMotor neurons carry signals from spinal cord to muscles
Somatic nervous systemControls voluntary skeletal muscle movementResponsible for all conscious, deliberate movement in sport
Autonomic nervous systemControls involuntary functions (cardiac muscle, smooth muscle, glands)Regulates heart rate, breathing rate and blood vessel diameter during exercise

💡 Skeletal muscle is controlled by the SOMATIC nervous system (voluntary). Cardiac and smooth muscle are controlled by the AUTONOMIC nervous system (involuntary).

The Motor Neuron

🧠 Motor Neuron Structure and Function ▼
StructureDescriptionFunction
Soma (cell body)The main body of the neuron containing the nucleusHouses the genetic material; integrates incoming signals
DendritesShort branching projections from the somaReceive incoming signals from other neurons
AxonLong single projection from the soma that transmits signals away from the cell bodyCarries the electrical action potential toward the muscle fibre
Myelin sheathFatty insulating layer wrapped around the axon in segments, with gaps called nodes of RanvierSpeeds up electrical conduction by allowing the signal to jump between nodes (saltatory conduction)
Axon terminalsBranching endings of the axon that form the presynaptic side of the NMJRelease acetylcholine (ACh) into the synaptic cleft when an action potential arrives

💡 The myelin sheath is critical for fast signal transmission. Demyelinating diseases (e.g. multiple sclerosis) slow or block nerve conduction, causing muscle weakness and coordination problems.

The Neuromuscular Junction

🧸 Diagram: The Neuromuscular Junction — Detail ▼

At the NMJ: nerve impulse arrives at axon terminal → ACh vesicles fuse with membrane and release ACh into the synaptic cleft → ACh binds receptors on the motor end plate → action potential generated in the muscle fibre → Ca2+ released from SR → contraction.

Motor Unit Recruitment

📈 Motor Unit Recruitment — Summary Table ▼
ConceptDescriptionExample
Motor unitOne motor neuron and ALL the muscle fibres it innervates. All fibres in the unit contract simultaneously when the neuron fires (all-or-nothing)The soleus contains large motor units (many fibres per neuron) for sustained postural force
Size principleMotor units always recruited smallest to largest: Type I first, Type IIa second, Type IIx last. This order is fixed and cannot be reversedLight jogging: only Type I recruited. Sprint finish: Type I + IIa + IIx all recruited
RecruitmentActivating more motor units to increase total force outputIncreasing barbell weight requires more motor units to be recruited
Rate codingIncreasing the frequency of nerve impulses to a motor unit to increase the force it produces (firing more times per second)Firing at 50 Hz produces more force than the same unit firing at 20 Hz
TetanusSustained maximal force from a motor unit produced by high-frequency stimulation — the muscle cannot relax between impulsesMaximal isometric contractions (e.g. arm wrestling) involve near-tetanic stimulation
TwitchThe mechanical response of a motor unit to a single nerve impulse — a brief contraction followed by relaxationA single isolated muscle twitch lasts milliseconds; movement requires repeated twitches (tetanus)

💡 Force is graded by TWO mechanisms: (1) recruiting MORE motor units, and (2) increasing the firing RATE of already-active units. Both work together during exercise.

💪 1.6 Types of Muscular Contractions

Concentric, Eccentric and Isometric

💪 Diagram: Three Types of Muscle Contraction ▼

⚠ Eccentric contractions cause the greatest muscle damage and DOMS because the muscle is being forcibly lengthened against its contraction. Students often incorrectly describe eccentric contractions as the muscle "relaxing" — it is still actively contracting.

Isotonic and Isokinetic Contractions

📊 All Contraction Types — Full Comparison ▼
TypeMuscle lengthForce vs loadJoint movementExample
ConcentricShortensForce exceeds loadYes — joint angle decreases (flexion) or increases (extension)Lifting phase of bicep curl; kicking a football
EccentricLengthensLoad exceeds forceYes — controlled by the muscle as it lengthensLowering phase of bicep curl; landing from a jump; downward squat
IsometricNo changeForce equals loadNo — no joint movement occursHolding a plank; wall sit; gripping a bar
IsotonicChangesConstant force appliedYes — encompasses both concentric and eccentric phasesAny free-weight exercise (e.g. dumbbell curl has both phases)
IsokineticChangesVaries to maintain constant speedYes — at a controlled constant velocitySpecialised rehabilitation machines; competitive swimming

💡 Isotonic simply means the same (iso) force (tonic) is applied — it includes both concentric AND eccentric phases of a movement. Do not confuse isotonic with isometric (no movement).

Force Production and DOMS

⚠️ Eccentric Contractions, DOMS and Force Production ▼
ConceptExplanationSport application
Force production rankingEccentric contractions produce the MOST force, followed by isometric, then concentric. This is because during eccentric loading, the sarcomere is stretched and cross-bridge tension increases.This is why lowering weights (eccentric) can be done with heavier loads than lifting the same weight (concentric)
DOMS (Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness)Muscle soreness and stiffness appearing 24-72 hours after unaccustomed exercise, primarily caused by eccentric contractions creating microtrauma (small tears) in muscle fibres and connective tissueDownhill running, landing drills and new strength training programs cause significant DOMS due to high eccentric loading
Repeated bout effectAfter an initial bout of eccentric exercise causes DOMS, subsequent bouts of the same exercise cause significantly less damage and soreness — the muscle adaptsAthletes experience less soreness from the second week of a new training program compared to the first
Stretch-shortening cycle (SSC)A rapid eccentric pre-stretch immediately followed by a concentric contraction, storing elastic energy in the muscle-tendon unit and releasing it to enhance force outputJumping, sprinting and throwing all utilise the SSC — the countermovement in a jump stores energy that augments the push-off

💡 The force production ranking (Eccentric > Isometric > Concentric) is a common exam question. Remember the acronym EIC — Eccentric Is Clearly strongest.
